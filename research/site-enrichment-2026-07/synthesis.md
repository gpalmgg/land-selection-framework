# Synthesis — Site Enrichment Research, 2026-07

*Weaving the six tracks into one argument. Compiled 2026-07-03. Every load-bearing claim traces to a track and a named source. Where the reasoning is mine rather than the tracks', it is marked [SYNTHESIS].*

---

## 1. The core argument

A bioregioning tool that finds water-secure, temperate, affordable, beautiful rural land and points capital-holding newcomers toward it is, mechanically, an amenity-migration accelerant. That is not a risk the project runs alongside its mission. It is the same machine, running forward, doing exactly what it was built to do.

The research makes this concrete rather than theoretical. Track 5 states the tension in its sharpest form: the criteria that make a region "score" well on this tool (water security, elevation, temperature buffering, price relative to cities) are the identical variables that drive climate and amenity gentrification. The optimization target and the displacement mechanism are one variable. Track 5's flagship case is inside the map's own footprint: legacy Hispano residents inside Taos town limits fell from 2,039 in 2014 to 1,306 in 2019, a one-third loss in five years (Santa Fe New Mexican; Montañez et al., *Rural Sociology* 2024). The acequia mayordomo in Truchas reporting "former common lands being devoured by development" is describing the downstream effect of exactly the desirability signals this tool surfaces.

Here is where the six tracks stop being six briefs and become one.

Track 6 shows that the amenity engine now has a data supply chain: ProPublica × Rhodium (2020) named Vermont, the Great Lakes, and the Upper Midwest as the improving climate niche; First Street's 12th National Risk Assessment (Feb 2025) named the Montana-to-Wisconsin band, putting Driftless (WI) on the receiving side; Invest Appalachia (2024) sold Central Appalachia as "the largest habitable area in the continental U.S. by 2050." Track 3 shows the incoming population's character: Southern Appalachians arrivals "skew well-off out-of-state transplants," housing stock not keeping pace, displacement of existing rural residents (Cardinal News 2024; Daily Yonder 2023). Track 2 shows the legal system reacting to precisely this pressure, closing the door on speculative and non-resident acquisition across nearly every region at once. Track 1 supplies the physical desirability gradient the migration follows. Track 4 hands the tool the datasets to draw that gradient more sharply than before.

Read together, the tracks describe a closing loop. The climate-migration literature identifies the water-secure refugial regions; the finance sector prices the rest as uninsurable; capital-holding migrants move toward the named winners; legacy and Indigenous host communities get priced and cultured out; legislatures scramble to gate the inflow. A tool that adds soil, groundwater, wildfire, and Köppen-drift layers without changing its stance simply makes the desirability gradient legible to more people, faster.

The reciprocity spine is the only component that runs against the loop instead of greasing it. Track 5's philosophical backbone (Liboiron, *Pollution Is Colonialism*, 2021) is the load-bearing line: an obligation is not subject to choice, responsibility is. Reciprocity built as a scored, opt-in feature is a "settler move to innocence" in Tuck & Yang's (2012) exact sense, belonging-talk that soothes the arriving user while the land question stays untouched. Reciprocity built as unavoidable, unscored, place-specific context is the one thing that turns the tool from an accelerant into a brake.

The argument the six tracks make together, that none makes alone: **the enrichment the site can now perform is real and shippable, and every layer of it sharpens the amenity-migration blade unless the host-community-standing architecture is built in the same release.** The datasets and the ethics are not two workstreams. They are the two edges of one knife, and Track 5's closing line is the operational instruction: the strongest version of the project is the one that names the knife it is holding.

What would it mean to ship desirability data and displacement data in the same drawer, so that no user can see the first without the second?

## 2. What the site can add now

Concrete, buildable, ranked by integration cost and honesty. Three payloads: new map layers (Track 4 + Track 1), corrected network counts (Track 3), and the climate-hazard WMS endpoints that close named gaps (Track 1).

**The ship-now map layers, in order.** Track 4's ranking is defensible and I adopt it.

First: **SoilGrids 2.0 pH + texture** (ISRIC, `maps.isric.org`, standard WMS, CC-BY 4.0). Global, 250 m, same provider family as the site's existing soil-carbon layer, so it is the lowest-wiring true-new criterion available. Soil beyond carbon is the "can this land actually feed and house people" signal. Only caveat is the known ISRIC dynamic-WMS paint latency, already a pattern on the site's slower layers. Ship first.

Second: **Aqueduct 4.0 Groundwater Table Decline** (WRI, CC-BY 4.0, HydroSHEDS level-6 basin polygons via Esri Living Atlas ArcGIS-REST). If the project already processed Aqueduct extracts for surface water-stress (the CLAUDE.md notes it did), this is a new attribute on geometry the site already holds, near-zero marginal cost. Groundwater over 50–100 years is distinct from the surface signal and is exactly this tool's horizon. Verify current Aqueduct wiring, then ship.

Third: **wildfire, as a paired regional overlay.** USFS Wildfire Risk to Communities (public domain, ArcGIS-REST MapServer/ImageServer, CONUS only) plus Copernicus EFFIS/GWIS burnt-area (OGC WMS, Europe + global). Honest version ships fire-history and hazard, not the daily forecast that dates instantly. Explicit coverage caveat required: two regional layers with a Mexico gap (no clean public tile service found for Oaxaca).

Fourth: **Köppen-Geiger present + future climate class** (Beck et al. 2023, GeoTIFF, self-tile once then static, attribution-only). Download-only, so it costs a processing step, but it wins on distinctiveness: the future-scenario version shows climate-class drift across 2041–2099, which speaks to the belonging horizon better than any real-time layer. [SYNTHESIS] This is also the single layer most aligned with the reciprocity reframe, because it visualizes the place your descendants would actually inhabit, not the place as it looks in the listing photo.

Cheap add worth taking regardless: **RESOLVE Ecoregions 2017** (UNEP-WCMC, WMS + WFS, CC-BY 4.0) as a named-ecoregion string in the region drawer. It is identity, not a scored criterion, which is precisely why it fits "belong to a place" and framework discipline at once.

Deliberate cuts, so the working group does not chase them: Global Wind Atlas (CC-BY but no OGC WMS, needs COG self-tiling), micro-hydro (no shippable public tile layer for these regions), BII (download-only plus CC-BY-NC-SA, the non-commercial clause complicates a public tool), KBA/Half-Earth (use-restricted), GRACE recharge (too coarse). CHELSA growing-degree-days is the strong reserve: it closes a named existing agronomic gap but is download-only, so promote it only if food-sovereignty gets priority.

**The climate-hazard WMS wins from Track 1** overlap and reinforce the above. Track 1's immediate honest wins, all free standards-compliant WMS with no self-hosting: EDO/GDO Combined Drought Indicator (Copernicus, Europe + global, live GetCapabilities), US/North American Drought Monitor (covers US + Canada + Mexico via NADM, so Nova Scotia, Kootenays, and Oaxaca get real-time drought coverage), EFFIS/GWIS wildfire, USFS Wildfire Risk, and NOAA Sea Level Rise Viewer (US coast only, cached ArcGIS tiles). Track 1's honest recommendation for the drought layer specifically: pair the real-time WMS monitors (EDO/GDO + NADM) with the Köppen 2050 shift map as the forward-looking companion, so the site shows "current drought state" and "projected climate-class shift" side by side rather than pretending a real-time monitor is a projection.

The coverage note that matters for honesty: Track 1 flags that NOAA SLR does not cover Nova Scotia (Canada), and Hilary Beaumont, the Atlantic journalist, is from Nova Scotia. Coastal SLR for NS routes through CanCoast / ClimateData.ca, a named unclosed gap.

**The Track 3 corrections are not optional cleanup.** Six placement errors in the current regen-density data would embarrass the tool if a knowledgeable reader caught them:

Cloughjordan Ecovillage is in Co. Tipperary, roughly 150 km from West Cork, and must not be counted in the West Cork/Connemara region; the genuine in-region anchor is The Hollies at Enniskeane, and Connemara has no GEN anchor at all (THIN). The Red Ibérica de Ecoaldeas lists zero members in Galicia and zero in Asturias, so those counts must come from GEN and named sourcing, not the RIE, and both are correspondingly soft. ZEGG is in Brandenburg, not Saxony-Anhalt (only Sieben Linden is in-state). Huehuecoyotl is in Morelos, roughly 400 km from Oaxaca, and cannot anchor it. O.U.R. Ecovillage is on Vancouver Island, not the Kootenays (anchors there are Yasodhara Ashram and Argenta). Terra Perma is in the Laurentides, not the Eastern Townships (which is THIN).

Track 3's deeper finding is a metric failure the site should surface rather than hide: in Galicia and Oaxaca, ecovillage density is low but regenerative activity is high. Galicia's live movement is *repoblación* (roughly 2,000 abandoned villages, the Xunta's *aldeas modelo* catalogue, a 15% tax deduction, Proyecto Arraigo). Oaxaca's is Indigenous *comunalidad* (Zapotec/Mixtec/Chatino communal land over roughly 80% of state territory). The "network density" number under-counts exactly where the reciprocity framing matters most. [SYNTHESIS] That under-count is the tell that the ecovillage-count metric was built for the settler frame; the two regions where it breaks are the two where the community already there is the whole point.

## 3. The trajectory reframe

"Climate haven" is falsified goods, and the site should never use the noun again.

Track 6 is unambiguous: the two most-cited North American havens of 2020, Vermont and Southern Appalachia, were both hit by catastrophic flooding within four years of the label (Vermont 2023 and 2024; Helene, Sept 2024). Invest Appalachia sold Central Appalachia as the continent's largest habitable area in a January 2024 report; eight months later Helene demonstrated that "milder average climate" does not equal "safe from extreme precipitation," a point West Virginia Watch made explicitly that November. The instruction Track 6 hands the site is precise: **"refugia" survives only as an adjective, never as a noun.** A place can be refugial for one hazard at one elevation. No place is a haven.

The forward-looking honesty move is to present trajectory as scenario-tagged and hazard-specific, and to state the scenario in the copy. Track 6 flags that the most dramatic maps (ProPublica, the Alentejo desertification worst case) are RCP8.5, the pessimistic tail, and that moderate pathways diverge much less before roughly 2060. Site-facing trajectory copy that hides the scenario is dishonest by omission.

The clean structural split comes from Track 1 and Track 6 together, and it is the most decision-useful single finding in the whole research set. The 50–100 year water axis divides the 20 regions cleanly. The Mediterranean-and-Southwest arc is drying and losing viability: Alentejo (RCP8.5 pushes semi-arid climate over roughly two-thirds of mainland Portugal), Cévennes/Languedoc (fire-weather danger projected to triple), Northern New Mexico (Rio Grande snow-water-equivalent at 13% of median in April 2026, experts calling the decline "permanent…going to get worse," per Searchlight NM / Source NM 2026), and Oaxaca (drying signal, though Track 6 flags the state-vs-local water contradiction as likely an aggregation artifact). The humid-temperate Northeast arc is wetting and gaining it, trading scarcity for flood: Vermont, Québec Eastern Townships, Southern Appalachians, Nova Scotia, Ireland, Driftless, rural Estonia (precipitation +19% by 2100, crop yields projected 0 to +30%, the strongest net-positive ecological trajectory in the set). Cascadia and the Kootenays are the swing cases where summer snowpack and wildfire decide the outcome.

A separate cluster deserves its own name because it hides inside "water-secure": the water-timing collapse regions. Northern New Mexico, South Tirol (≤25% glacier mass surviving to 2100 even at +2°C), Kootenays (Columbia glaciers mostly gone by 2100), and Cascadia (snowpack −50% by 2100) keep their annual precipitation but lose the frozen reservoir that releases water through the dry summer. Annual totals look fine; the summer runs dry. A site that shows annual precipitation without showing snowpack trajectory would actively mislead a settler betting on a century.

The leading indicator is not the physical science. It is capital.

Track 6's finance findings are the sharpest early-warning signal in the research. First Street (Feb 2025) projects climate risk erasing $1.4–1.47 trillion in US real estate value over 30 years, driven primarily by insurance repricing, not physical destruction, with the line "insurance costs are increasing faster than mortgage payments." This is the mechanism by which the 50-year horizon reaches into today's affordability. The California FAIR Plan is the canary: insurers non-renewed 2.8 million homeowner policies 2020–2025 in fire-prone ZIPs; RFF/Milliman name Oregon and Texas as "not far behind." The Appalachian and Vermont flood signal is the inverse and just as damning: post-Helene western North Carolina had under 1% of residents in the hardest-hit areas holding flood coverage, roughly $9.5B in uninsured residential losses, the NFIP already over $20B in deficit. [SYNTHESIS] The site should treat insurance non-renewal and flood-coverage gaps as a first-class trajectory signal, because it is the market pricing a century of risk in real time, ahead of the settled science, and it is the signal a 50–100 year community most needs and least expects to see on a bioregioning tool.

What does it do to a region's "attractiveness" when the honest trajectory field reads: wetter, greener, and structurally uninsurable against the flood that wetting brings?

## 4. The reciprocity architecture

Track 5's central proposal is a fifth consideration, and it is the spine everything else hangs from.

The framework's Decision 7 gives four sovereignty axes. All four describe the arriving community's autonomy, resilience, exit-options, and jurisdictional latitude. All four are settler *self*-sovereignty. None describe the standing of the community already there. Track 5 names this precisely as Tuck & Yang's "settler futurity": a framework that optimizes the settler's future while rendering the prior inhabitant invisible or already-past. The fifth consideration, **Host-Community Standing**, is qualitative and never scored, populated by four sub-fields per region: whose land (Indigenous nation plus treaty/unceded status for NA; the living commons for Europe; comunal/ejido for Latin America), displacement pressure and trajectory, standing pathway (how a newcomer would earn standing), and existing reciprocal vehicle.

The single design instruction worth tattooing on the build: reciprocity must not be scored, and the refusal to score it is itself the argument. Track 5 (via Kimmerer) holds that scoring reciprocity is the extractive move, that reciprocity resists the quantification the rest of the tool performs, and that keeping it unscored is the point. A "reciprocity score" would market low-resistance host communities, which are the most displaceable ones. This is the one place I will use the framing directly: host-community standing is not a metric the tool computes. It is context the tool refuses to resolve, handed to the user as a question they must carry themselves.

The European answer to standing is the research's most useful gift, because it lets the tool honor prior claims in Europe without importing Indigenous framing where it does not belong.

Track 5 documents the living-commons "earning standing" model with precision. In the *montes veciñais en man común* of Galicia, roughly a quarter of Galician land, standing is explicitly residence-based and reciprocal: rights "are opened to anyone that starts living in a particular parish and expire when moving away" (Asociación Forestal de Galicia; *International Journal of the Commons*). The Portuguese *baldíos*, restored by law in 1976 under the slogan "Os baldios são do povo!", tie membership to registered residence in the village (FAO *Unasylva* 180). South Tirol's *maso chiuso* (Provincial Law Bolzano 17/2001, over 13,400 closed farms) keeps a farm indivisible across inheritance, structurally resisting the fragmentation amenity migration drives. The pattern is a coherent, rigorous, non-Indigenous answer to "how does a newcomer earn standing": through residence, labor, and participation, and it is revocable, and it is unpurchasable. That is a fundamentally different offer from fee-simple purchase, and it is the offer a reciprocity-first tool should surface per European region.

The North American precedents make the block concrete rather than gestural, and two stand out as transposable mechanisms.

Asitu'lɨsk (formerly Windhorse Farm, Nova Scotia) is a settler-founded land project that returned its land to the Mi'kmaq in a 2021 land-back initiative, now an Indigenous learning centre (Track 3). It sits inside the region the Atlantic journalist knows, and it is the cleanest possible answer to "what does arriving well actually look like": a settler project that gave the land back. The Sogorea Te' Land Trust and its Shuumi Land Tax (Track 5, founded 2012, Confederated Villages of Lisjan) is the single most transposable model in the research, because it converts "I live on this land" into a concrete, recurring, reciprocal obligation, a mechanism rather than a feeling; it received a $20M Shuumi gift from the Kataly Foundation, the largest known cash gift to a Native land trust. [SYNTHESIS] The site should link the host-standing field of each NA region *out* to the relevant nation's own land-back or co-stewardship body (Sogorea Te', Kwilmu'kw Maw-klusuaqn, EBCI), so the tool routes attention and potential contribution toward the community rather than merely representing it. That is CARE/OCAP applied to the tool's own naming of territories, and Track 5 is honest that this specific application is [SYNTHESIS], the honest implication of the sources rather than an instruction from them.

The place-specific reciprocity grammars matter, and the tool should name the local one rather than importing a generic "right relationship": Netukulimk in Nova Scotia, comunalidad/tequio/cargo in Oaxaca, acequia/parciante commons plus Pueblo in Northern New Mexico, montes veciñais/baldíos in Galicia and Portugal. Each place already has its own theory of belonging. Naming the local grammar is how the tool avoids the "settler nativism" flattening Tuck & Yang warn against.

## 5. The legal tailwind

The 2024–26 legislative wind is at the reciprocity thesis's back, and this is the finding most likely to surprise the working group.

Track 2's one-line reading of the period: the door is closing on speculative and non-resident acquisition across nearly every region, and opening, narrowly, for resident, working, collectively-held, agriculture-committed arrivals. The two movements are the same movement. Reciprocity-shaped entry (residence plus agricultural use plus collective tenure) is increasingly the *only* legal entry, not merely the ethical one.

The closings target speculation. Nova Scotia doubled its non-resident deed-transfer tax from 5% to 10% (in force 1 April 2025), with a six-month-relocation exemption, a fiscal reciprocity gate that penalizes arriving-without-belonging and rewards actual relocation. Québec's Bill 86 (assented 25 March 2025) tightens the CPTAQ Green Zone against non-farmers and imposes a fund moratorium retroactive to 5 December 2024. Romania's Law 175/2020 effectively closes direct farmland purchase to non-residents (5-year domicile plus 5-year agricultural activity plus 75%-income tests). France strengthened SAFER pre-emption in March 2025 to close the buy-the-farmhouse-plus-land loophole. The US state foreign-farmland wave (Arkansas Act 636, Wisconsin AB 218) targets foreign-adversary capital.

The openings reward residence and collective form. Galicia's Ley 11/2021 plus Decreto 170/2024 actively channels abandoned land to willing users through the Banco de Terras and Aldeas Modelo, the single most settlement-seeking regime in the set. Wales's One Planet Development (TAN 6) remains the most community-settlement-friendly rural route, permitting low-impact dwellings against a binding ecological-footprint target. The Agrarian Commons model (Agrarian Trust, live in 10 states) and Terre de Liens (France, roughly 10,000 ha held for agriculture) formalized further. For a community whose thesis is belonging over 50–100 years, the legal wind favors arriving as residents-who-farm-in-common, not buyers-who-hold.

[SYNTHESIS] This is the strongest possible external validation of the reciprocity spine, and it should reframe how the site presents legal data. The tool has been treating tenure law as a constraint the user navigates. The truer framing: the law is converging on the same conclusion the ethics reached, that residence-plus-labor-plus-collective-form is the legitimate way to arrive, and speculation is being closed off jurisdiction by jurisdiction. The reciprocity spine is not the site's idealism running ahead of reality. It is the site reading where the legal reality is already going.

One caution that binds everything above: Track 2 carries twelve verification flags, and none may be stated as settled. Québec Bill 86 (confirm which provisions are in force versus awaiting proclamation), Wisconsin AB 218 (passed Assembly, likely still a bill not law), Vermont Act 181 partial repeal (a 7 May 2026 House vote, confirm Senate and Governor), Romania Law 116/2024 (substantive amendments unverified), Arkansas 2025 amendments HB 1358/1680 (act numbers unverified), Oregon 2025 foreign-ownership bill and NC HB 463 (enactment unverified), Oregon 2025 c.355 (confirm whether it changes dwelling rules), France's Peio Dufau SAFER bill (Assembly-adopted, confirm final passage), the Saxony-Anhalt Agrarstrukturgesetz (likely still pending), Wales TAN 6 (no 2024–26 amendment found, treat as unchanged), the Estonia January-2026 Russia/Belarus draft (draft only), and Portugal's Golden Visa removal (confirmed, but no rural-specific carve-out found). Any site copy or interview claim built on these must carry the "proposed" or "pending" qualifier. The reciprocity read of the *pattern* is solid; the individual statutes are moving targets.

## 6. Open questions

Genuine unknowns the research surfaced, not rhetorical ones.

Does surfacing displacement trajectory actually slow displacement, or does it function as a due-diligence checkbox that launders the arrival? Track 5 warns that reciprocity built as an opt-in becomes a move to innocence. Whether unavoidable-but-unscored context changes behavior, or merely changes how the arriving user feels about the same behavior, is untested.

Can the tool measure host-community trajectory anywhere but Taos? The Taos legacy-population figures (Track 5) are the empirical backbone, but comparable longitudinal legacy-population data for the other 19 regions may not exist. If the displacement-trajectory field is populated for one region and blank for nineteen, does it mislead by implying the others are safe?

Is the rural form of climate gentrification real enough to cite as established? Track 5 is honest that climate gentrification is under-theorized in its rural form versus the urban Miami work (Keenan et al.), and that the amenity-migration literature is the best available proxy. This is a genuine gap in the discourse, not just in the research.

How does the tool handle contested host authority? Track 2 documents three regions where "whose land" has no single answer: the Sinixt excluded from the Columbia River Treaty table despite *R v Desautel* (2021) affirming their rights; Vermont's four state-recognized Abenaki tribes contested by Quebec's Odanak and Wôlinak in a 2025 genealogical report; Oaxaca simultaneously strengthening symbolic Indigenous rights (2024 Article 2 reform) while state fiscal policy pressures communal-tenure privatization. A land-standing field that names one host authority in these regions makes a contested call.

Does the Oaxaca state-versus-local water contradiction resolve, and which number does the site show? Track 6 flags S&P Global rating Oaxaca "low water-stress through 2050" against on-the-ground reports of acute scarcity (Oaxaca City receiving roughly 400 of the 1,200 L/s it needs, 40% infrastructure leakage). Showing the optimistic aggregate would be dishonest; showing only the local scarcity would contradict the modeled state figure.

What is the honest regen-density number when the metric itself is the wrong instrument? Track 3 establishes that no directory returns a 100 km radius count and that Galicia and Oaxaca break the metric. Whether to show a soft integer with named anchors, or to abandon the integer for a qualitative movement description in those regions, is a live design question.

Do the European insurance and demographic-projection gaps invalidate the trajectory copy for European regions? Track 6 flags that European catastrophe-insurance repricing has no clean FAIR-plan analogue in the sources, and that forward municipal-level depopulation projections were thin for Saxony-Anhalt, Transylvania, and Estonia. The European trajectory story currently rests on softer evidence than the US.

## 7. What this could become

Three destinations for this research: the working group, the r4 commentary, and the Atlantic interview.

**To the working group.** The ship-now datasets go as a proposal, framed as framework-legal additions (native units, no scoring, source/vintage/license visible): SoilGrids pH + texture, Aqueduct Groundwater Table Decline, the wildfire regional pair, Köppen present + future, RESOLVE Ecoregions as drawer context, plus the Track 1 real-time WMS drought and SLR layers. The Track 3 corrections go as a data-integrity fix, not a discussion item, because six misplaced anchor sites are simply errors. The fifth consideration, Host-Community Standing, is the one genuine framework question for the group, and it should go as Track 5 frames it: a fifth qualitative dimension paralleling Decision 7's four sovereignty axes, never scored, with the explicit argument that the four existing axes are all settler self-sovereignty.

**To the r4 reciprocity commentary.** Track 5 was written to feed the drafted r4 commentary directly, and it strengthens the existing argument in three places. The philosophical spine hardens from assertion to citation (Liboiron on obligation-versus-choice as the ground for "never scored, never optional"; Tuck & Yang on settler futurity as the precise name for Decision 7's blind spot). The European "earning standing" model (montes veciñais/baldíos as residence-based, revocable, unpurchasable standing) gives the commentary a non-Indigenous mechanism, answering the likely working-group objection that reciprocity framing does not transpose to Europe. Track 2's legal convergence gives the commentary its strongest external support: the argument is no longer only ethical, because the law across 2024–26 is closing speculation and opening residence-plus-collective-form, which is the reciprocity thesis written into statute.

**To the Atlantic interview.** The interview should foreground the knife, not hide it. Track 5's closing instruction is the interview strategy: state the tool's own failure mode out loud, that a bioregioning tool is mechanically an amenity-migration accelerant unless reciprocity constrains it, because owning that is the difference between a settler move to innocence and an actual reciprocal design. Taos is the case to lead with, since it is in the map's footprint and it shows regenerative in-migration hollowing out exactly the rooted community (Hispano acequia plus adjacent Pueblo) the tool claims to protect. The "climate haven is falsified goods" line lands hard with a science journalist and is defensible on the record (Vermont and Appalachia both flooded within four years of the label). Beaumont is from Nova Scotia, so Netukulimk, the 2025 Toqi'maliaptmu'k co-management arrangement, and Asitu'lɨsk (settler land returned to the Mi'kmaq) are the reciprocity story in the region she knows best, and Asitu'lɨsk is the concrete image of arriving well.

The through-line for all three audiences is one sentence. The site can now see the land more clearly than ever, in soil and groundwater and fire and the drift of climate classes across a century, and the sharper that sight becomes, the more it matters who the site teaches to look, and what it makes them see about the people already standing on the ground they are learning to want.

Where does a tool's responsibility end: at showing the land honestly, or at refusing to show it to the wrong eyes?
