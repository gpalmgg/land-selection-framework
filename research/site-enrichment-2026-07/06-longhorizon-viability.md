# Track 6 — 50–100 Year Viability & Climate-Refugia Signals

*Research brief for the Land Selection Framework. Prepared 2026-07-03.*
*Scope: long-horizon (2075–2125) signals across the 20 regions. State + trajectory per factor. No composite scoring — framework-forbidden.*

---

## How to read this brief

This track deals in **trajectories**, not conditions. A place that is comfortable today can be structurally water-short by 2075; a place that floods today may be the most temperate acre on the continent in 2090. Every number below is attached to a scenario and a source. Where a claim is speculative or scenario-dependent, it is flagged **[SPECULATION]** or **[SCENARIO-DEPENDENT]**.

Three cross-cutting cautions frame everything:

1. **"Climate haven" is a discredited noun, useful only as an adjective.** The two most-cited North American "havens" of 2020 — Vermont and Southern Appalachia — were both hit by catastrophic flooding within four years of the label (Vermont 2023 & 2024; Helene 2024). Refugia are relative and hazard-specific, never absolute.
2. **RCP8.5 vs. moderate scenarios diverge hard after ~2060.** ProPublica/Rhodium's most dramatic maps are RCP8.5 ("extreme emissions"). Treat their 2070 numbers as the pessimistic tail, not the expected case.
3. **The financial markets are already pricing the 50-year horizon** — insurance non-renewal and mortgage repricing are the earliest, hardest signals we have of where the long run is being called uninhabitable *by capital*, ahead of the physical science being settled.

## 1. Climate-refugia science — who appears in the literature

### North America

**ProPublica × Rhodium Group, "New Climate Maps Show a Transformed United States" (2020)** — the canonical U.S. climate-migration dataset (county-level, RCP8.5). Core finding: ~162 million Americans (nearly 1 in 2) face declining environmental quality (more heat, less water); by 2070 the human "climate niche" shifts north toward the Midwest and Great Lakes. Explicitly named as *improving* / becoming "more temperate, verdant and inviting": **Minnesota, Michigan, Vermont**, and Great Lakes cities. The Southeast broadly degrades; the Mississippi valley gains dangerous humidity. Relevant to our set: **Vermont, Driftless (WI), Cascadia (OR)** sit on the favorable side of the niche shift; **Northern New Mexico, Ozarks** trend toward the heat/aridity-stressed side.
Source: projects.propublica.org/climate-migration/ ; impactlab.org.

**First Street, "Property Prices in Peril" — 12th National Risk Assessment (Feb 2025)** — the finance-forward successor to ProPublica's physical maps. Names the *receiving* regions of climate migration: *"northern, currently less-populated areas from Montana to Wisconsin — and in parts of the East — taking in more people because of the region's greater climate resilience."* This directly flags the **Driftless (WI)** and, by extension, the **Upper Midwest/Great Lakes** band as demographic winners. Also models 5.2M internal climate migrants in 2025 and warns some metros hit population "tipping points" before 2055.
Source: firststreet.org/press/climate-driven-population-shifts... ; 12th National Risk Assessment PDF.

**Central Appalachia as projected "largest habitable area in the continental U.S. by 2050"** — attributed to EPA (2021) and NASA (2022) modeling, popularized by *Invest Appalachia's* "Climate Resilience in Central Appalachia" report (Jan 2024) and Dhillon et al., "Climate Change Migration and the Economic Rebirth of Central Appalachia" (*Social Sciences*, MDPI, 2022). Directly relevant to **Southern Appalachians (NC)**. **Critical caveat:** this framing was published *before* Hurricane Helene (Sept 2024) demonstrated that "milder average climate" does not equal "safe from extreme precipitation." See West Virginia Watch, "Appalachia is not ready to be a climate haven" (Nov 2024).

**Peer-reviewed refugia mapping (biodiversity, not human, but structurally informative):**
- Saunders et al., "Integrating climate-change refugia into 30×30 conservation planning in North America" (*Frontiers in Ecology & Environment*, 2023): <15% of North American refugia currently protected; beyond +2°C, most refugia collapse to **high latitudes and high elevations**. This is the structural logic behind the mountain regions in our set (Kootenays, S. Tirol, Cévennes, Transylvania, Southern Appalachians) retaining refugial value — elevation buys climate time.
- Conservation Science & Practice (2024), Pacific Northwest microrefugia mapping: identifies topographically complex, drought-buffered pockets in **Cascadia** but flags reduced snowpack as the dominant threat to those very refugia.
- Stralberg et al. / Michalak et al., "Distribution and protection of climatic refugia in North America" (PNAS-adjacent, 2018) — foundational; refugia concentrate in topographically diverse terrain.

### Europe

**"Identifying future climate refugia in Mediterranean Europe"** — EU Joint Research Centre / European Commission analysis (2022), fine-resolution over the Euro-Mediterranean biome, ~50-year horizon. Core finding: refugia concentrate in the **eastern** Mediterranean at **mid-altitude, highly rugged terrain**. Country refugia coverage: Greece 52%, Cyprus 37%, **Italy 30%** (highest in our western set). Iberia is conspicuously *not* a refugia stronghold. Implication for our set: **South Tirol (IT)** and mountainous **Cévennes (FR)** and **Transylvania (RO)** retain refugial pockets; **Alentejo, Galicia, Asturias** do not appear as refugia in Mediterranean-biome terms (their resilience is Atlantic-maritime, a different mechanism).
Source: environment.ec.europa.eu/news/identifying-future-climate-refugia-mediterranean-europe.

**Iberian drought futures** — Vicente-Serrano-lineage and multi-model work:
- "The future of Iberian droughts: multi-scenario multi-model ensemble" (*Natural Hazards*, Springer, 2023): general tendency toward more severe drought across all global-warming levels.
- EEA: **>75% of Spain and ~60% of Portugal now at risk of desertification.**
- JRC European Drought Observatory (2024): SE Europe/Mediterranean vegetation productivity failed to recover to the 2000–2020 baseline across ~160,000 km² — a signal of ratcheting, not cyclical, drought.

**Baltic / Northern Europe as climate-favored** — Second Assessment of Climate Change for the Baltic Sea Basin; Copernicus ESD (2022) Baltic projections to 2100. Estonia: +2.3–4.5°C, precipitation +19% by 2071–2100, longer growing season, crop yields projected **0 to +30%**. This is the European mirror of the ProPublica northward niche shift — **rural Estonia** is on the agronomically *improving* side.

## 2. Water futures on the multi-decade horizon

The single most decision-relevant long-horizon variable. Ranked roughly from most structurally water-secure to most structurally water-threatened.

### Structurally water-gaining / secure (2075+)
- **Rural Estonia** — precipitation +19% by 2100; rainfall intensity already +30%. Net water *gain*. Risk is flashiness/flooding, not scarcity. (Copernicus ESD 2022; ClimateChangePost.)
- **West Cork/Connemara (IE) & Pembrokeshire (Wales)** — wetter winters (+6–10% by 2050s–2080s), drier summers (Wales summer −15% by 2050s, −18–26% by 2080s). Net oceanic water security remains high; summer soil-moisture deficits are the emerging edge. (EPA Climate Ireland Research 471; NIC Wales.)
- **Galicia & Asturias (ES)** — Atlantic NW Iberia. Do *not* share interior/southern Spain's drought trajectory; orographic Atlantic rainfall keeps them water-secure relative to the peninsula. This is their defining long-horizon asset. (Contrast with Iberian drought ensemble, which centers on interior/south.)
- **Nova Scotia & Québec Eastern Townships** — humid continental/maritime, precipitation rising. Emerging risk is *water quality* (Nova Scotia's 2050s top-threat reclassification, per its 2022 climate assessment) and summer dry-spells (Estrie), not gross scarcity.
- **Vermont, Driftless (WI), Ozarks (AR)** — Northeast/Upper-Midwest are getting **wetter**; Northeast extreme precipitation +50% since 1995 (per Vermont Public / NOAA). Their water problem is *too much, too fast* (flooding), not depletion.

### Snowpack / glacial-baseflow decline — the "water-timing collapse" cluster
These regions keep annual precipitation but lose the **frozen reservoir** that releases water through the dry summer. This is the defining 50-100yr water risk for mountain regions.
- **Northern New Mexico (Rio Grande)** — the clearest structural-decline case in the set. April 2026 snow-water-equivalent at **13% of median** in the Rio Grande headwaters; 2009–2025 native flows nearly identical to the 1948–64 record dry period; San Juan-Chama Project yielding **39% of firm yield**. Experts explicitly frame it as **permanent, not drought**: *"this is a permanent thing that's not going to change. It's going to get worse"* (Searchlight NM / Source NM, 2026). Aquifer overdraft compounds it. **Verdict: structural water decline by 2075 is the base case, not the tail.**
- **South Tirol (IT)** — even at +2°C, only ~25% of South Tyrol's glacier mass survives to 2100; Alpine snowfall already −⅓ over 1920–2020 (−~50% on SW slopes). By 2100 peak flows arrive 2–3 months earlier and peak volume falls. Snowmelt (not glacier) is the region's dominant water source, so the snowpack decline bites directly. (Eurac Research; IPCC SROCC Ch.2; EGU.)
- **Kootenays / Columbia Basin (BC)** — Canadian Columbia glaciers projected to **mostly disappear by 2100**; ice-thinning rate 2014–18 was **4× the 2000–09 rate**; snowpack SWE −30% and snow depth −38% by end-century; June–Sept baseflow already declining 1989–2019; droughts exceeding the extreme 2023 event projected to become common. (NW Power & Conservation Council; *Climatic Change* 2022; *npj Natural Hazards* 2026.)
- **Cascadia (OR)** — snowpack −25% by 2050, **−50% by 2100** (up to −60% by mid-century under high emissions); every +1°C ≈ −15% summer flow in the lower Willamette; wildfire up to **9×**. Winter-wet/summer-dry split sharpens. The Willamette Water 2100 program (Oregon State INR) is the definitive regional study. **Abundant winter water, structurally scarce summer water.**
- **Transylvania (RO)** — Carpathians +2.5–3.0°C by 2050; drier winters, summer river-level declines, drought + soil erosion rising (UNEP Carpathian reporting; Carpathian Convention COP7 assessment). Less severe than the Alpine/Rockies cases but trending dry in summer.

### Structural drying (precipitation regime shift, not snowpack)
- **Alentejo (PT)** — the European worst-case in our set. Under RCP8.5, semi-arid climate (today confined to a small SE corner) expands to cover **~⅔ of mainland Portugal**; sub-humid→semi-arid transition sweeps the south; drought frequency/duration/intensity all rising; soil moisture projected to reach extreme deficits. Already high desertification risk. (Espinha Marques/IntechOpen 2024; *Journal of Hydrology* soil-moisture study 2022; EEA.)
- **Cévennes (FR)** — paradoxical: more *extreme* rain (autumn daily maxima +~30% since mid-20thC, >300mm/day events) yet hotter, drier summers and tripling fire-weather danger. Water arrives in destructive pulses, not reliable baseflow.
- **Saxony-Anhalt (DE)** — central Germany's drought core. DWD/ZALF flag drying into **deep soil layers** (not just topsoil), driving forest dieback and pest outbreaks across Saxony-Anhalt/Thuringia/Brandenburg. Structural soil-moisture decline is the headline risk. (HESS 2024; ZALF 2024; Umweltbundesamt 2023 monitoring report.)

### Anomalous case
- **Oaxaca (MX)** — acute *present* scarcity (Oaxaca City receives ~400 of the 1,200 L/s it needs; >half of municipalities in drought March 2024; ancient rivers run dry) — **but** S&P Global's water-stress modeling rates the *state* as **low exposure to water stress through 2050**, absent adaptation. The contradiction is real and important: aggregate state water balance is not alarming, but distribution, infrastructure decay (40% leakage), and highland micro-scarcity make lived water access precarious. **State-level trajectory: moderate. Local reliability: poor.** (Mezcalistas 2024; S&P Global Sustainable1.)

## 3. Insurability & mortgage/finance signals

Capital is the leading indicator. Where insurers withdraw, the market has decided the long-run risk is unpriceable.

### The macro signal
**First Street (Feb 2025):** climate risk is set to erase **$1.4–1.47 trillion** in U.S. real-estate value over 30 years, driven primarily by *insurance repricing*, not physical destruction — *"insurance costs are increasing faster than mortgage payments."* First Street's "Climate, the Sixth C of Credit" research argues climate is now a structural input to mortgage underwriting. **This is the mechanism by which the 50-year horizon reaches into today's affordability.** Named highest-premium-spike metros: Miami, Jacksonville, Tampa, New Orleans, **Sacramento** (the only one near our set, California-adjacent).

### California-adjacent wildfire (relevant to Cascadia, N. New Mexico, BC by contagion)
**California FAIR Plan** as the canary: insurers non-renewed **2.8 million** homeowner policies 2020–2025 in fire-prone ZIPs; FAIR Plan grew from 210,000 homes (2020) → 450,000+ policies with **$650B exposure by June 2025** (+289%); the Jan 2025 LA fires triggered $4B in FAIR losses and a $1B industry assessment. **Oregon and Texas are explicitly named by RFF/Milliman as "not far behind" California.** FAIR-type plans now cover ~3 million U.S. properties, >$1 trillion exposure. (Milliman; Stateline Oct 2025; Taxpayers for Common Sense Nov 2025.)
- **Cascadia (OR)** — Oregon is on the leading edge of the wildfire-insurance stress curve behind CA. Wildfire projected up to 9×. Expect FAIR-plan-style pressure and non-renewals to intensify through the 2030s–40s. **[SCENARIO-DEPENDENT but well-supported directionally.]**

### British Columbia wildfire (Kootenays)
BCFSA (BC Financial Services Authority) has formally flagged **non-renewal during active wildfire** as a consumer risk; insurers impose **temporary moratoria on new policies** in areas under imminent threat. As of 2024–25, wildfire coverage remains a *standard* inclusion in BC home policies (unlike CA's outright market exit) — the BC signal is **friction and moratoria, not withdrawal**, so far. Kootenays/Okanagan flagged as needing "special attention." **Trajectory: worsening, but not yet a market collapse.** (IBC; BCFSA advisory; Working Ventures Insurance 2025.)

### Appalachian / inland flood (Southern Appalachians NC, Vermont, Driftless, Ozarks)
The **uninsured-flood** signal is as damning as non-renewal. Post-Helene western North Carolina: **<1% of residents** in hardest-hit areas held flood coverage; only **5.2% of homes / 10.2% of commercial** properties had FEMA flood insurance; **~$9.5B in uninsured residential losses**. Private insurers have almost entirely exited inland flood; the NFIP is **>$20B in deficit** and Helene could double it. (Newsweek; Grist; Carolina Public Press; NC Newsline 2025.)
- **Southern Appalachians (NC):** the finance headwind is not premium spikes — it's a **near-total flood-protection gap** in a region whose flood risk was underestimated precisely because it was sold as a haven.
- **Vermont:** standard homeowner policies exclude flood; July 2023 average FEMA Individual Assistance payout only **$16,000** (max $41,000) — nowhere near rebuild cost. Repeat flooding (Lyndonville: 5× in 13 months) is the kind of loss frequency that eventually drives non-renewal even in "low-risk" states. **[Emerging signal, watch.]**
- **Driftless (WI) / Ozarks (AR):** same structural inland-flood underinsurance; steep unglaciated Driftless terrain concentrates flash-flood damage.

### Europe
No FAIR-plan analogue, but note: European home insurance increasingly excludes or surcharges flood/wildfire, and the EU is debating mandatory climate-risk disclosure. **Cévennes (flash flood + wildfire)** and **Alentejo (wildfire + drought crop loss)** are the most exposed to future European premium repricing. Estonia/Ireland/Wales/Galicia carry low catastrophe-insurance risk. **[Less data available — flag as a European research gap.]**

## 4. Demographic & rural-viability trajectory

The question: will there be enough people for the region to sustain services, schools, and a community over 50 years?

### Depopulating (structural headwind for service/community continuity)
- **Galicia & Asturias (ES)** — core of NW "**España vaciada**." Rapid aging + gradual decline; >80% of Spanish rural municipalities losing population; Spain projected to lose >500,000 people in 15 years, **70%+ of that loss in depopulated regions**. National population 48M by 2050, ~41M by 2100 (UN WPP 2024). **This is a real 50-year viability risk** — thinning of exactly the rural fabric a settling community would rely on. Counter-signal: cheap land + policy incentives (repopulation programs) + amenity in-migration could partially offset. (Del Molino's *La España vacía*; Esade; IPS Journal.)
- **Saxony-Anhalt (DE)** — eastern Germany's decades-long rural out-migration and aging; among the EU's steepest shrinkage regions. Compounds the soil-drought headwind. (Well-established trend; specific 2050 projections were thin in search — **flag as gap**.)
- **Rural Estonia** — Baltic rural depopulation is real and long-running, though partially counterweighted by the agronomic-improvement pull. Small national population (~1.3M) means rural service thresholds are fragile.
- **Transylvania (RO)** — Romania has among the EU's highest emigration/rural-outflow rates historically; **[not directly confirmed in this search — flag for follow-up]**, but the regional pattern points to continued rural thinning absent in-migration.

### In-migrating / amenity-growth (viability tailwind, gentrification risk)
- **Southern Appalachians (NC)** — projected net *in*-migration as a climate-migration destination; arrivals skew **well-off out-of-state transplants**. Housing stock isn't keeping pace → rising rents, displacement risk for existing rural residents. **Viability is not the risk here; affordability and social equity are.** (Cardinal News 2024; Daily Yonder 2023.)
- **Vermont** — climate-motivated in-migration documented (NPR, CSMonitor), reinforced by ProPublica's low-risk ranking — though 2023–24 floods have complicated the narrative. Net demographic tailwind.
- **Cascadia (OR)** — Willamette valley on the favorable side of the niche shift; continued in-migration expected despite wildfire.
- **Driftless (WI)** — First Street names the Montana-to-Wisconsin band as a demographic *receiving* region. Structural in-migration tailwind.
- **Québec Eastern Townships & Nova Scotia** — positioned as Canadian climate-refuge receiving regions; Natural Resources Canada and Westmount Magazine flag Estrie/Mauricie/Saguenay as future-attractive for water + temperate climate. Nova Scotia already seeing in-migration. **Tailwind, contingent on planning for the influx.**
- **Oaxaca (MX)** — distinct dynamic: strong *retention* via communal land governance (comunalidad, ejido/comunidad tenure) and deep place-attachment; less an amenity-migration story than an intact-community-persistence story. Out-migration to the U.S. historically high but land-tenure institutions keep communities anchored.

### Mixed
- **West Cork/Connemara (IE) & Pembrokeshire (Wales)** — historically depopulating rural peripheries now seeing partial reversal via remote-work and amenity migration; Ireland's national population is *growing* (rare in rural Europe). **Modestly stabilizing.**
- **Cévennes (FR) & South Tirol (IT)** — Cévennes long depopulated but with a durable back-to-the-land counterculture; South Tirol is wealthy, stable, high-amenity, and demographically secure (its constraint is land cost/availability, not depopulation).

## 5. Ecological carrying capacity & regenerative upside

Where can a community plausibly build **more** ecological capacity over a century (net-positive trajectory), versus where is the base degrading faster than restoration can keep up?

### Net-positive potential (base is stable/improving; restoration can outrun degradation)
- **Rural Estonia** — the standout. Warming + wetting + longer growing season (+0–30% yields) means the *biophysical envelope for regeneration widens* over the century. Abundant water, low population pressure, large forest/mire base. **Strongest net-positive ecological trajectory in the set.** [Caveat: peak-rain intensity +30% threatens soil erosion if bare-soil ag persists — regenerative practice is the hinge.]
- **Driftless (WI)** — deep, fertile loess soils; the region is a *global reference case* for regenerative/contour agriculture (the original Coon Creek watershed conservation work, 1930s). Water-secure, climate-"winner" per First Street. Erosion on steep slopes is the constraint; the soil base and knowledge base to build capacity are exceptional. **High regenerative upside.**
- **Galicia, Asturias, West Cork/Connemara, Pembrokeshire** — Atlantic maritime: reliable water, long growing seasons, high biomass productivity. Soil carbon and forest/hedgerow capacity can be built. Constraint is demographic (labor) and, on the Celtic fringe, historic overgrazing/peat degradation that is *restorable* (peatland rewetting = large net-positive carbon lever). **Moderate-to-high upside.**
- **Nova Scotia & Québec Eastern Townships** — humid, forested, water-abundant, warming into a more productive envelope. Forest and soil base intact. **Net-positive potential**, constrained by SLR (NS coast) and 2023-scale wildfire risk.
- **Vermont** — intact forest, water-abundant, cool-temperate; strong soil/forest regenerative base. Flooding damages built capital but the ecological base is resilient. **Moderate-high upside.**

### Contested / knife-edge (restoration possible but racing degradation)
- **Transylvania (RO)** — extraordinary existing ecological base (some of Europe's last primary forests, high-nature-value farmland, low-intensity mosaic). The century question is whether that base is *held* against summer drought, illegal logging, and land-use intensification. **The base is the asset; the risk is losing it, not building it.**
- **Cévennes (FR)** — resilient Mediterranean-mountain mosaic with deep agroecological tradition, but wildfire (fire-weather danger tripling) can erase decades of forest capacity in a single event. Restoration is possible; fire is the reset button. **Volatile.**
- **South Tirol & Kootenays** — high current ecological capacity, but the water-timing collapse (snowpack/glacier) sets a hard ceiling on how much can be sustained through late-century summers. Alpine/montane ecosystems migrate upslope until they run out of mountain.

### Degradation likely outrunning restoration (net-negative base risk)
- **Alentejo (PT)** — the hardest case. Desertification advancing under RCP8.5 (semi-arid over ⅔ of Portugal), soil-moisture collapse, declining precipitation. The montado (cork-oak agro-silvo-pastoral) system holds deep drought-adaptation knowledge and *is* a regenerative template — but the biophysical trend is toward less water and thinner soils. **Regeneration is possible only within a shrinking envelope; net-positive over a century is an uphill fight.** [The counter-case: dryland regenerative pioneers argue Alentejo is *precisely* where regenerative water-retention landscaping matters most — genuinely contested, flag as SPECULATION either way.]
- **Saxony-Anhalt (DE)** — deep-soil-layer drought driving forest dieback faster than replanting establishes; the arable base is productive but the water trend is against it.
- **Northern New Mexico** — structural water decline caps carrying capacity hard. Acequia/traditional dryland systems carry centuries of adaptation knowledge (a real asset), but a river that is "permanently" declining sets a ceiling restoration can't lift. **Net capacity likely declines regardless of practice.**
- **Oaxaca** — biodiversity and communal-governance base are world-class assets *for* regeneration; the constraint is water reliability and soil erosion on steep highland terrain. **Knife-edge, tilted by governance quality.**

## 6. The long-horizon verdict — per-region tailwind / headwind

*Single biggest 50–100yr tailwind and headwind each, with the evidence. Decision-relevant "trajectory" content. No ranking.*

### Europe

**Alentejo (PT)**
- **Tailwind:** Deep dryland/agro-silvo-pastoral (montado) adaptation tradition + low population pressure → room to practice water-retention regeneration where it matters most.
- **Headwind:** Desertification. RCP8.5 pushes semi-arid climate over ~⅔ of mainland Portugal; declining precipitation + soil-moisture collapse. *The clearest structural-drying case in Europe.* (IntechOpen 2024; EEA; J.Hydrology 2022.)

**Galicia (ES)**
- **Tailwind:** Atlantic water security — decoupled from interior-Iberian drought; reliable rainfall + high biomass productivity.
- **Headwind:** España vaciada demographic decline — aging, out-migration, thinning rural service base. (Esade; UN WPP 2024.)

**Transylvania (RO)**
- **Tailwind:** Exceptional intact ecological base (primary forest, high-nature-value farmland) + mid-altitude refugial terrain (per EU JRC Mediterranean-refugia logic).
- **Headwind:** Summer drought/heat rising (+2.5–3°C by 2050, drier summers, falling river levels) plus rural depopulation risk. (Carpathian Convention COP7; UNEP.)

**West Cork / Connemara (IE)**
- **Tailwind:** Mild, water-secure oceanic climate; low wildfire; national population growing (rare rural-Europe tailwind); restorable peatland carbon.
- **Headwind:** Coastal sea-level rise (0.42–0.82m by 2100) + stormier, wetter winters → coastal/flood exposure. (EPA Climate Ireland Research 471.)

**Pembrokeshire (Wales)**
- **Tailwind:** Temperate maritime, water-secure winters, high pasture/forest productivity.
- **Headwind:** SLR 43–76cm by 2080s + summer rainfall −18–26% by 2080s (coastal + summer-drought squeeze). (NIC Wales.)

**Cévennes (FR)**
- **Tailwind:** Mid-altitude refugial terrain + orographic rainfall + durable agroecological/back-to-land culture.
- **Headwind:** Wildfire — fire-weather danger projected to **triple**, burned area +40–100%; compounded by flash-flood pulses. (MedECC; Turco et al.; PMC 2020.)

**South Tirol (IT)**
- **Tailwind:** Highest refugia coverage of our western-European set (Italy 30%, per EU JRC) + wealthy, stable, high-adaptive-capacity society.
- **Headwind:** Snowpack/glacial baseflow collapse — ≤25% glacier mass by 2100 even at +2°C; peak flow 2–3 months earlier; drier spring/summer. (Eurac Research; IPCC SROCC.)

**Asturias (ES)**
- **Tailwind:** Atlantic water security + rugged topographic refugia + intact forest/soil base.
- **Headwind:** Demographic aging/decline (España vaciada NW). (Esade; IPS Journal.)

**Saxony-Anhalt (DE)**
- **Tailwind:** Flat, productive arable base + infrastructure + affordable land.
- **Headwind:** Deep-soil-layer drought driving forest dieback, compounded by eastern-German rural depopulation. (HESS 2024; ZALF; DWD.)

**Rural Estonia**
- **Tailwind:** The widening biophysical envelope — warming + precipitation +19% + longer season + crop yields +0–30%; strongest net-positive ecological trajectory in the set. (Copernicus ESD 2022.)
- **Headwind:** Rural depopulation + fragile small-population service thresholds; rain-intensity +30% erosion risk. (Baltic depopulation literature — *flag as data-thin*.)

### North America

**Cascadia (OR)**
- **Tailwind:** Favorable side of the ProPublica niche shift; temperate, water-abundant *in winter*; high forest productivity.
- **Headwind:** Twin snowpack-loss (−50% by 2100, summer flow −15%/°C) and wildfire (up to 9×) + rising insurance stress (Oregon "not far behind California"). (Willamette Water 2100; Milliman.)

**Vermont**
- **Tailwind:** ProPublica-designated among the lowest-climate-risk U.S. counties; cool, water-abundant, forest-rich; climate in-migration.
- **Headwind:** Extreme-precipitation flooding — Northeast +50% extreme precip since 1995; catastrophic floods 2023 & 2024; standard policies exclude flood, payouts far below rebuild cost. *The "haven that flooded."* (Vermont Public; USGS SIR 2025-5016.)

**Southern Appalachians (NC)**
- **Tailwind:** Projected "largest habitable area in continental U.S. by 2050" (EPA/NASA/Invest Appalachia) + climate in-migration.
- **Headwind:** Extreme-precipitation flooding with a near-total insurance gap — Helene (2024): <1% flood-insured, ~$9.5B uninsured losses. Plus gentrification/housing pressure. *The haven label was falsified in real time.* (Invest Appalachia 2024; Newsweek; Carolina Public Press.)

**Driftless (WI)**
- **Tailwind:** Exceptional soil + global-reference regenerative-ag base + First-Street-named demographic "winner" + water-secure.
- **Headwind:** Flash flooding — intense downpours on steep unglaciated terrain; inland-flood underinsurance. (First Street 2025; WICCI; La Crosse Tribune.)

**Ozarks (AR)**
- **Tailwind:** Water-secure (getting wetter), forested, affordable land.
- **Headwind:** Rising humid-heat + wet/dry whiplash + inland-flood underinsurance; on the warmer-stressed edge of the niche shift. (EPA Midwest; National Climate Assessment.)

**Northern New Mexico**
- **Tailwind:** Deep acequia/dryland adaptation tradition + high-elevation refugial terrain + cultural land continuity.
- **Headwind:** **Structural, likely-permanent water decline** — Rio Grande SWE 13% of median (2026), flows matching the worst dry period on record, aquifer overdraft, experts calling it "permanent…going to get worse." *The clearest 2075 water-loss case in the entire set.* (Searchlight NM; Source NM 2026.)

**Nova Scotia**
- **Tailwind:** Temperate maritime, water-abundant, Canadian climate-refuge receiving region, intact forest base.
- **Headwind:** Sea-level rise up to **1m by 2100** (+70cm more possible from Antarctic melt) + emerging wildfire (2050s) and water-quality decline. (NS Climate Change Assessment 2022; National Observer.)

**Kootenays (BC)**
- **Tailwind:** Low population pressure, high current ecological capacity, mountain water *for now*.
- **Headwind:** Snowpack/glacial baseflow collapse (glaciers mostly gone by 2100; SWE −30%; summer baseflow declining) + wildfire + insurance moratoria. (NW Power & Conservation Council; *npj Natural Hazards* 2026; BCFSA.)

**Québec Eastern Townships**
- **Tailwind:** Canadian climate-refuge receiving region; water access + temperate-warming envelope + forest/soil base. (NRCan; Westmount Magazine.)
- **Headwind:** Summer dry-spells/drought + soil erosion + 2023-scale extreme wildfire season. (Equiterre; 2023 Québec wildfire assessment.)

**Oaxaca (MX)**
- **Tailwind:** World-class biodiversity + strong communal land governance (comunalidad) enabling persistence and regeneration; state-level water stress rated **low through 2050** (S&P Global).
- **Headwind:** Acute *local* water scarcity + infrastructure decay (40% leakage) + highland drought variability — lived water reliability is poor despite favorable aggregate. (Mezcalistas 2024; S&P Global Sustainable1.)

## Gaps & open questions (research honesty)

- **European rural-depopulation *projections* (not just current trends)** were thin for Saxony-Anhalt, Transylvania, and Estonia specifically. Current decline is documented; forward 2050 municipal-level projections need dedicated follow-up (Eurostat regional demographic projections, ARDECO).
- **European catastrophe-insurance repricing** has no clean FAIR-plan analogue in the sources; the Cévennes/Alentejo insurance-withdrawal question is under-documented vs. the U.S.
- **Oaxaca's state-vs-local water contradiction** deserves scrutiny — the S&P "low stress to 2050" rating sits uneasily against on-the-ground scarcity reports; likely an aggregation artifact. Do not over-rely on the optimistic state figure.
- **Nova Scotia / Québec as "receiving regions"** rests partly on advocacy/journalism (Westmount Magazine, NRCan framing) rather than hard migration modeling — softer evidence than the First Street U.S. numbers.
- **RCP scenario transparency:** the ProPublica and Alentejo-desertification worst cases are RCP8.5. Under moderate-emissions pathways the divergence is much smaller before ~2060. Any site-facing "trajectory" copy should state the scenario.
