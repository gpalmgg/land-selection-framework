# Track 5 — Reciprocity & Host-Community Standing

Research brief for the Land Selection Framework's ethics/values section, the qualitative "land standing" dimension, and the practitioner argument for a fifth "host-community standing" consideration.

Compiled 2026-07-03. Distinguishes **[ESTABLISHED]** scholarship/precedent (named, dated, sourced) from **[SYNTHESIS]** (this brief's own reasoning). Web-verified precedents are dated. Where a source is paywalled or a claim is thin, it is flagged.

---

## Framing note: the practitioner argument this track supports

The framework's Decision 7 gives four "sovereignty axes." The r4 reciprocity commentary (drafted, `r4-reciprocity-commentary-DRAFT.md`) observes that **all four describe settler *self*-sovereignty** — the arriving community's autonomy, resilience, exit-options, jurisdictional latitude. None describe the standing of the community *already there*. This is the exact structural blind spot Tuck & Yang name as "settler futurity": frameworks that optimize the settler's future while rendering the prior inhabitant invisible or already-past. Track 5 supplies the scholarship and precedent to justify a **fifth consideration — host-community standing** — surfaced as qualitative context, never scored.

## 1. Frameworks for ethical settlement / arrival

**[ESTABLISHED] Tuck & Yang, "Decolonization Is Not a Metaphor" (2012), *Decolonization: Indigeneity, Education & Society*, 1(1).** The foundational text. Two load-bearing concepts:
- **"Settler moves to innocence"** — evasions that "problematically attempt to reconcile settler guilt and complicity, and rescue settler futurity." Includes "settler nativism," "fantasizing adoption," and treating decolonization as metaphor for any general improvement project.
- **The incommensurability thesis** — "Decolonization brings about the repatriation of Indigenous land and life; it is not a metaphor for other things we want to do." Decolonization is not accountable to a settler-inclusive future; it is not a synonym for social justice.
- **Direct relevance:** a "bioregioning tool" that helps settlers *belong to a place* is, on Tuck & Yang's terms, at risk of being a move to innocence — belonging-talk that resolves settler discomfort while the land question stays untouched. The tool has to metabolize this critique openly rather than route around it. This is the sharpest adversarial source and should be engaged, not cited for cover.

**[ESTABLISHED] Robin Wall Kimmerer, *Braiding Sweetgrass* (Milkweed, 2013) and *The Serviceberry* (2024; orig. Emergence Magazine essay, 2022).** Potawatomi botanist. Two frameworks the tool can operationalize:
- **The Honorable Harvest** — a protocol grammar: ask permission; never take the first or the last; take only what you need; take only what is given; minimize harm; use everything you take; share; give thanks; reciprocate. This is a *decision procedure*, not a sentiment — directly translatable into questions a tool can force a user to sit with.
- **The gift economy / reciprocity vs. sustainability** — Kimmerer distinguishes "sustainability" (preserving systems so we can extract in perpetuity) from "reciprocity" (the Earth cares for us; we are obligated to care in return). A tool whose spine is reciprocity must be careful it is not covertly optimizing extraction-in-perpetuity dressed as stewardship.

**[ESTABLISHED] Kyle Powys Whyte** (Citizen Potawatomi Nation, Univ. of Michigan). Key papers on **kinship as the unit of environmental ethics** and "Indigenous Peoples, Climate Change Loss and Damage, and the Responsibility of Settler States." Whyte reframes climate/land ethics away from individual choice toward **reciprocal responsibilities modeled on kinship** — moral bonds connecting humans and non-humans. His framing of settler colonialism as "a form of domination that violently disrupts human relationships with the environment" gives the tool language for *why* arriving badly is not merely rude but a continuation of a structural harm.

**[ESTABLISHED] Max Liboiron (Métis/Michif), *Pollution Is Colonialism* (Duke, 2021).** Crucial distinction for the tool's stance: **obligation vs. responsibility.** "An obligation is not subject to choice; responsibility is." All research (and by extension all land-selection) "has land relations" that "align with or against colonialism as a particular form of extractive, entitled land relation." Even benevolent-seeming projects can be premised on colonial access to land. For a tool: attentiveness to whose land this is is not a virtue the user opts into; it is a precondition. This is the philosophical spine for "never scored, never optional."

**[ESTABLISHED] Indigenous data sovereignty — CARE & OCAP.**
- **OCAP® (Ownership, Control, Access, Possession)** — articulated 1998 by the First Nations Information Governance Centre (FNIGC), Canada. First Nations collectively own information about themselves and control its collection and use.
- **CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance** (Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics) — released Sept. 2019 by the **Global Indigenous Data Alliance (GIDA)**; formalized in Carroll et al., "The CARE Principles for Indigenous Data Governance," *Data Science Journal* (2020). Designed as a complement to FAIR data principles, adding the question FAIR omits: *whose benefit, on whose authority.*
- **Direct relevance:** a geospatial tool that names Indigenous territories is itself doing Indigenous-data work. CARE/OCAP obligate it to ask whether surfacing territory names, treaty status, or nation boundaries was done *with authority to control* — or whether the tool is extracting and re-presenting Indigenous geographic knowledge without consent. Flag: the site currently names nations from "public sources" — CARE would push toward citing Indigenous-authored sources (nation websites, land-back orgs) over settler databases.

**[ESTABLISHED] Land Back scholarship.**
- **Yellowhead Institute, *Land Back: A Red Paper* (Oct. 2019)**, Toronto Metropolitan Univ. The academic anchor: Land Back = reclaiming Indigenous *jurisdiction*, not just parcels. Analyzes how Canada dispossesses and how nations re-assert jurisdiction outside reserve boundaries. Reframes the whole question from "access to land" to "who decides."
- **NDN Collective's LANDBACK campaign** (launched 2020) and **Nick Estes** (Kul Wicasa / Lower Brule Sioux), *Our History Is the Future* (Verso, 2019) — movement-side framing; jurisdiction and self-determination over restitution-as-symbol.
- **"What is Land Back? A Settler FAQ"** (Briarpatch, 2020) — practitioner-facing; the clearest short statement that Land Back "requires that settlers work to repair the harm colonialism has done and continues to do."

## 2. Indigenous co-stewardship & land-back precedents (North America regions)

Dated, named precedents mapped to the site's NA regions. These make the "land standing" block concrete rather than gestural.

**Mi'kma'ki / Nova Scotia (Mi'kmaq).**
- **Toqi'maliaptmu'k Arrangement** ("we will look after it together"), Mi'kmaq of Nova Scotia + Parks Canada, signed **Dec. 2025** — co-governance/co-management across the parks Parks Canada administers in Nova Scotia; each party decides using its own protocols. (Kwilmu'kw Maw-klusuaqn / mikmaqrights.com; Canada.ca; The Narwhal.)
- **Mi'kmaq Forestry Initiative** — management of ~30,000 ha across Hants, Annapolis, Halifax, Antigonish, Guysborough, Cape Breton, Richmond, Inverness counties.
- **Netukulimk** — the governing Mi'kmaw concept: take only what you need; live in harmony with the land. Directly parallels Kimmerer's Honorable Harvest and is *the* place-specific reciprocity grammar for the Nova Scotia region on the map (the Atlantic journalist Hilary Beaumont is from Nova Scotia — this is the region she knows).

**Kootenays / West Kootenay (Sinixt, Ktunaxa, Syilx/Okanagan).**
- **R. v. Desautel, 2021 SCC 17** (Apr. 23, 2021) — Supreme Court of Canada, 7-2, held the **Sinixt** (declared "extinct" in Canada in 1956, reservation in Washington State) retain constitutionally protected rights to hunt in ancestral territory in BC's Arrow Lakes region. Washed away the extinction declaration. A living example that "unceded / present" is a legal fact, not a metaphor, in the exact Kootenays region on the map.

**Vermont / Québec borderlands (Abenaki).**
- **Oct. 2023** — Nulhegan Abenaki Tribe received a **354-acre** gift ("feoffment," no money) from Molly Davies in Wheelock, VT, described as "a restoration." (Stowe Reporter/vtcng.)
- **2022 agreements** for future harvest of traditional medicine/craft species; **UVM research** (Jessica Rubin & Josef Görres) tying riparian-buffer restoration to Abenaki rematriation.
- **Abenaki Land Link Project** (2020–, with NOFA-VT) — seed rematriation + food sovereignty.
- **Atowi / Vermont Land Trust Abenaki Land Access Agreement**; proposed VT bill to enshrine Abenaki use of state lands.

**Southern Appalachians (Eastern Band of Cherokee Indians).**
- **Kituwah Mound** ("the Mother Town," place of Cherokee origin) placed **into federal trust, May 2022**; joint celebration by EBCI, Cherokee Nation, and United Keetoowah Band.
- **Nikwasi Mound** returned via the town of Franklin, NC; ongoing co-stewardship of acquired forest habitat "in accordance with the values of Cherokee land stewardship." (First Nations Development Institute; National Geographic; Cherokee One Feather.)

**Driftless / southern Wisconsin (Ho-Chunk).**
- **2026** — Dane County transferring **165 acres** of Ho-Chunk ancestral homeland (funded by the Dane County Conservation Fund; ~$6.41M purchase), including six archaeological sites and 22 burial mounds. Part of the Ho-Chunk Nation's reclamation of **2,000+ acres** in Wisconsin. (PBS Wisconsin; Channel3000; WKOW.) Directly in the Driftless region on the map.

**Northern New Mexico (Pueblo + Hispano acequia commons).**
- **Acequias** — "acequias de común," described as among the longest continuous democratic institutions in North America; governed by *mayordomo* + elected commissioners, water shared among *parciantes*. Pueblo shared water-catchment systems predate them by ~1,000 years. This is a **living commons** example, not a historical one — and note it sits *between* the Indigenous and "rooted community" categories (Hispano land-grant commons layered over Pueblo territory). See §5 for its displacement pressures.

**Oaxaca (Zapotec, Mixtec, Chatino — comunal governance).**
- **Usos y costumbres** — **418 of Oaxaca's 570 municipalities** govern under customary Indigenous/peasant self-governance; communal tenure administers **~80% of Oaxacan territory** and correlates with higher native-species richness than privatized lands.
- **Ixtlán de Juárez** (Sierra Norte) — flagship community forestry: *comunidad agraria* of 384 *comuneros*, seven community enterprises, forestry begun in the 1980s. A model of Indigenous-controlled land generating community wealth without alienating title. (Terralingua; UBC Wiki; Outriders.)
- **Comunalidad** — the Oaxacan Indigenous theory (Floriberto Díaz, Zapotec; Jaime Martínez Luna, Zapotec) naming the communal as an ontology: shared land, shared labor (*tequio*), shared authority (*cargo* system), shared celebration. This is the place-specific reciprocity grammar for the Oaxaca region.

## 3. European "rooted community" reciprocity

The site deliberately refuses to impose Indigenous framing on Europe. The right frame is **living commons + rooted rural communities**, where standing is earned through residence, labor, and participation rather than treaty. Scholarship confirms these are *functioning legal institutions*, not folklore.

**[ESTABLISHED] Montes veciñais en man común (Galicia, NW Spain).** Community-owned common lands (~1/4 of Galicia) belonging to residents of a parish. **Standing is explicitly residence-based and reciprocal: rights "are opened to anyone that starts living in a particular parish and expire when moving away."** This is a precise, real answer to "how does a newcomer earn standing in a commons" — you earn it by *living there and taking part*, and you lose it by leaving. The *comunidades de montes* have become vehicles of rural revival and neo-rural in-migration. (See academic work compiled in the SUSPLACE / "Managing the commons" project; *International Journal of the Commons*.)

**[ESTABLISHED] Baldíos (Portugal — Trás-os-Montes, Alentejo, uplands).** Legally recognized commons held by *compartes* (commoners), usually the registered residents of a village. Rights restored by law in **1976** after the 1974 revolution (the slogan "Os baldios são do povo!" — the commons belong to the people). Historically provided pasture, fuel, building material. Again: **membership = registered residence in the village.** (FAO *Unasylva* no.180; *Geographies* 2024 mapping study.)

**[ESTABLISHED] Maso chiuso (South Tirol / Alto Adige).** A legal institution (Provincial Law Bolzano 17/2001) keeping a farm *indivisible* across inheritance — the "closed farm" passes to a single heir, never fragmented. **>13,400** closed farms remain. Relevance: a rooted-community mechanism that resists the fragmentation and sell-off that amenity migration drives — a living example of how a community structurally protects its land base against outside capital.

**[ESTABLISHED] Composesorat / obște (Transylvania, Romania) & other post-socialist commons** — village forest/pasture commons with membership tied to lineage and residence; largely revived/restituted post-1990. (Named in the site's copy; scholarship exists but is thinner in English — flag for deeper sourcing if used prominently.)

**[ESTABLISHED] Gaeltacht (Ireland)** — the Irish-speaking rural districts. Standing here is **linguistic-cultural**: to belong is partly to carry the language. Údarás na Gaeltachta governs regional development. Relevance: a European case where "reciprocity with the community already there" means *not linguistically/culturally hollowing out* the place — the newcomer's obligation is to the living culture, not just the land.

**[ESTABLISHED] Neo-rural integration scholarship (the "earning standing" question).**
- **"Newcomers and rural crisis: Beyond the demographic challenge — a case study in Andalusia,"** *Land Use Policy* / ScienceDirect (2024): repopulation-by-headcount is *not itself* a solution; **lack of newcomer integration has negative consequences.** The number arriving matters less than the quality of relationship formed.
- **Sierra de Aracena (Andalusia)** neo-rural study, *Rural Sociology* (2024/2025) — neo-rurals reshape socio-identity and socio-economic life; integration is contested, not automatic.
- **Riace, Calabria** (Italy) — the well-known case of a depopulating village revived through hospitality to migrants; and the **Holapueblo** initiative (100+ Spanish municipalities, 2023) courting repopulation. Useful counter-examples: in-migration can *save* a dying community or *displace* a living one — the variable is reciprocity and consent.

**[SYNTHESIS]** The European pattern gives the tool a non-Indigenous but rigorous answer to standing: **in a living commons, standing is earned through residence + labor + participation, and it is revocable.** That is a very different offer from fee-simple purchase. A reciprocity-first tool can surface, per European region, *whether a living commons exists* and *what it asks of a newcomer* — without pretending Europe has no prior claims to honor.

## 4. Community land trusts & reparative land models

Named, dated models where land is held reciprocally or returned to community/Indigenous control — i.e., alternatives to the fee-simple purchase the tool's users might default to.

**[ESTABLISHED] Sogorea Te' Land Trust** (founded 2012; Confederated Villages of Lisjan / Ohlone; East Bay, CA). Urban, Indigenous-**women-led** land trust facilitating rematriation of Chochenyo/Karkin land. **Shuumi Land Tax** ("shuumi" = "gift") — a *voluntary annual contribution by non-Indigenous residents* of Lisjan territory. Now ~55 acres across 10+ sites; received a **$20M** Shuumi gift from the Kataly Foundation (largest known cash gift to a Native land trust). **This is the single most transposable model for the tool:** it converts "I live on this land" into a concrete, recurring, reciprocal obligation — a mechanism, not a feeling.

**[ESTABLISHED] Northeast Farmers of Color Land Trust (NEFOC LT)** — led out of Soul Fire Farm (Petersburg, NY). A **hybrid** model fusing community-land-trust commons + conservation land trust, aimed at permanent secure tenure for BIPOC farmers; goal of 2,000+ acres. Its "Reciprocity" giving page explicitly frames donation as land-based wealth redistribution and reparations.

**[ESTABLISHED] Agrarian Trust / Agrarian Commons** — national US model holding farmland in commons to keep it affordable and out of speculation, leasing long-term to farmers. **Central Virginia Agrarian Commons** received an **80-acre reparations donation** from Callie & Dan Walker (white Amelia County landowners) for Black farmers. Structure: land held by the commons, stewardship by the farmer, title never re-privatized.

**[ESTABLISHED] Land in Common (Maine)** and **community land trust (CLT) model generally** — the CLT separates land ownership (held in trust, permanently) from use (leased), specifically to defeat speculation and displacement. Origin lineage worth naming: the CLT model in the US traces to **New Communities Inc. (Georgia, 1969)**, founded by Black civil-rights farmers (Shirley & Charles Sherrod, Slater King), itself modeled on the Jewish National Fund / *moshav* land-lease structure — i.e., the CLT was *born* as a reparative, anti-displacement instrument.

**[SYNTHESIS]** The common thread: **decouple stewardship from speculative ownership.** For the tool, the design question becomes — can it present, per region, whether reparative/commons tenure vehicles *exist* (Sogorea Te-style land taxes, agrarian commons, Indigenous land trusts) as an alternative to "buy the parcel"? That reframes the user from *purchaser* to *contributor/steward*.

## 5. When the honest answer is "not there"

The scholarship on amenity/climate migration is unambiguous that in-migration by wealthier newcomers *displaces* rural and Indigenous host communities. This is the empirical backbone for `deeper.html`#ethics's line: *"where arriving would harm the community already there, the honest answer is not there."*

**[ESTABLISHED] Amenity migration displaces — the mechanism.** Amenity migrants are "generally upper or middle class... retirees, self-employed, telecommuters" seeking nature/scenery/recreation. Displacement is rarely direct eviction; it is **exclusionary, socio-cultural, and commercial displacement** — locals priced out of housing, culture hollowed, and (Gosnell & Abrams; *Journal of Rural Studies*/ScienceDirect) social ties and sense of community *decline* for long-time residents. "Mountain regions, often home to rural, indigenous communities, are rapidly becoming preserves for the social elite." (Löffler et al., "Managing Amenity-Led Migration in Mountain Regions.")

**[ESTABLISHED] Taos County, New Mexico — the flagship cautionary case (overlaps the site's N. New Mexico region).**
- **Legacy Hispano residents inside Taos town limits fell from 2,039 (2014) to 1,306 (2019) — a one-third loss in five years**; county-wide legacy Hispanic population down ~20% (2010–2019). (Santa Fe New Mexican; La Coalición de Taos; High Country News, "Troubled Taos.")
- An **acequia mayordomo in Truchas** reports properties selling far over market value, spiking property taxes that "impact locals the hardest"; "former common lands are being devoured by development." (High Road to Taos land-price reporting.)
- Montañez et al., "Nowhere Else to Go," *Rural Sociology* (2024) — housing insecurity in this Hispanic-majority county compounded by pandemic-era gentrification.
- **This is the most important single case for the tool**, because it shows amenity/regenerative in-migration hollowing out *exactly* the kind of rooted community (Hispano acequia + adjacent Pueblo) the tool claims to want to protect — and it is in a region the map presents as attractive.

**[ESTABLISHED] Tulum / Riviera Maya (overlaps a warm-migration destination pattern).** Touristification + the **Tren Maya** (cutting ~ millions of trees; expropriating/pressuring *ejido* land) has driven "large-scale dispossession of social property." Luis Hernández Navarro (2020): "savage capitalism that dispossesses the original peoples." The **X-Maben ejido** conflict (Felipe Carrillo Puerto, Quintana Roo) documented in *Tlalli* (UNAM). Cultural Survival: the train "partitions the Yucatán Peninsula and Maya peoples from each other and their lands." A concrete "not there" — or "not like this" — case.

**[ESTABLISHED] Rural gentrification / touristification, Mexico generally** — Hernández-Cordero et al., "Rural gentrification, touristification, and displacement," *Journal of Rural Studies* (2021): systematizes the displacement typology for Global-South rural contexts.

**[ESTABLISHED] Climate gentrification** — the process by which climate-risk-driven demand (e.g., higher-elevation, water-secure, "climate-haven" land) displaces lower-income incumbents. Directly implicates a *climate-resilience-optimizing* land tool: the very criteria that make a region score well (water security, temperature, elevation) are the criteria driving climate-gentrification displacement. **The tool's optimization target and the displacement mechanism are the same variable.** (Keenan et al. on Miami climate gentrification is the canonical urban study; the rural analog is the amenity-migration literature above.)

**[SYNTHESIS] The core tension for this tool, stated plainly:** a well-built land-selection/bioregioning tool is, mechanically, an *amenity-migration accelerant* — it identifies water-secure, beautiful, climate-buffered, cheap-relative-to-cities rural regions and points capital-holding newcomers at them. That is the Taos machine. The reciprocity spine is not decoration; it is the only thing standing between the tool and its own default failure mode. This should be said out loud in the ethics section, in the interview, and in the r4 commentary.

## 6. Design implications — what a reciprocity-first tool does differently

Concrete, buildable. Consistent with framework discipline (no composite scoring; state + trajectory; source + vintage + license; qualitative context never scored).

### 6.1 Add a fifth consideration: **Host-Community Standing** (qualitative, never scored)
The four sovereignty axes measure the *arriving* community. Add a fifth, populated by the `land-standing.js` dimension, that measures the *standing of the community already there.* Sub-fields, per region:
- **Whose land** (Indigenous nation + treaty/unceded/ceded status for NA; the living commons / rooted community for Europe; comunal/ejido for Latin America).
- **Displacement pressure / trajectory** — is the host community *growing, holding, or being hollowed out*? (Taos-style legacy-population loss where data exists.) This is the one place a *trajectory arrow* belongs — pointing at the host community's fate, not the settler's amenity.
- **Standing pathway** — *how* would a newcomer earn standing here (treaty responsibility; commons residence-membership à la montes veciñais/baldíos; Shuumi-style land tax; comunal *cargo*)? What does the place *ask*?
- **Existing reciprocal vehicle** — is there a land trust / land tax / co-stewardship body / commons a newcomer could contribute to rather than buy out? (Sogorea Te', NEFOC, Agrarian Commons, comunidad de montes.)

### 6.2 Force the user to sit with questions the tool refuses to answer for them
Reciprocity-first means the tool *withholds* rather than *optimizes*. On the region drawer, before any "shortlist" action, surface (not as a gate to click through — as unavoidable text):
- *"Who already belongs to this place, and is this community growing or being displaced?"*
- *"What would this place ask of you before it would call you kin?"* (Kimmerer/Honorable-Harvest register.)
- *"Is there a way to contribute here without acquiring — a land tax, a trust, a commons?"*
- *"If your arriving here would accelerate the displacement of the people already here, are you willing to hear 'not there'?"*
These are the Honorable Harvest protocol and Liboiron's "obligation not choice," rendered as interface.

### 6.3 What the tool must refuse to optimize
- **No composite "reciprocity score."** (Already framework-illegal, and doubly wrong here — scoring reciprocity is the extractive move Kimmerer names. Reciprocity resists the quantification the rest of the tool performs; keeping it *un-scored* is itself the argument.)
- **No ranking of regions by "ease of belonging."** That would market low-resistance host communities — i.e., the most displaceable ones.
- **Do not let climate/water/price criteria silently function as a displacement-targeting engine.** Where a region scores well on the very axes (water security, elevation, affordability) that drive climate/amenity gentrification, the host-standing field should say so.

### 6.4 Sourcing discipline (CARE/OCAP applied to the tool itself)
- Cite **Indigenous-authored sources** for territory/nation data (nation websites, Native Land Digital, land-back orgs, Yellowhead) over settler government databases — and say which.
- Treat the naming of nations and territories as **Indigenous-data work** subject to CARE (authority to control, collective benefit). Where possible, link out to the nation's *own* land-back / co-stewardship body (Sogorea Te', Kwilmu'kw Maw-klusuaqn, EBCI) so the tool routes attention and potential contribution *to* the community rather than merely representing it.
- Keep **source + vintage + license** on land-standing fields exactly as on every other value.

### 6.5 Place-specific reciprocity grammars (don't universalize)
Different regions have their *own* reciprocity ethics; the tool should name the local one rather than importing a generic "right relationship":
- Nova Scotia → **Netukulimk** (Mi'kmaw).
- Oaxaca → **Comunalidad / tequio / cargo** (Zapotec-Mixtec).
- N. New Mexico → **acequia / parciante** commons + Pueblo.
- Galicia/Portugal → **montes veciñais / baldíos** residence-membership.
- This resists the "settler nativism" flattening Tuck & Yang warn against — respecting that each place already has a theory of belonging.

### 6.6 Honesty move for the ethics section & the Atlantic interview
State the tool's own failure mode out loud: *a bioregioning tool is mechanically an amenity-migration accelerant unless reciprocity constrains it.* Owning this is the difference between a "settler move to innocence" (belonging-talk that soothes) and an actual reciprocal design (a tool willing to say "not there"). The strongest version of the project is the one that names the knife it is holding.
