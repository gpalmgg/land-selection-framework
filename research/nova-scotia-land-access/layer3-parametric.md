This means that no treaty, war, or land surrender has legally transferred ownership of the land to the Crown (Canada/the province).
- **Treaties Involved:** The Peace and Friendship Treaties (1725–1779) are the relevant legal documents. Unlike the Numbered Treaties (1–11) in western Canada, where Indigenous nations ceded vast tracts of land in exchange for reserves, annuities, and other benefits, the Peace and Friendship Treaties are **explicitly** about peace, friendship, and trade. They contain **no land cession or surrender** clauses.
- **Distinction:** The Mi'kmaq never surrendered their title to the land. The legal relationship is one of coexistence, not extinguishment. This is recognized in Canadian law (e.g., *R. v. Marshall*, *R. v. Bernard*).
- **Key Actors/Funders:** Not applicable to land status itself. However, for a regenerative community, this has direct implications:
    - **Duty to Consult:** The Crown (Province/Federal) has a constitutional duty to consult Mi'kmaq communities (bands/First Nations) regarding any development or land-use changes that could affect established Aboriginal or Treaty rights (e.g., hunting, fishing, gathering, traditional land use).
    - **Land Use:** You cannot simply "buy" land and ignore Mi'kmaw legal and spiritual claims. While you can hold a fee-simple title from the Crown, the underlying Mi'kmaw title remains unresolved. This affects logging, water rights, and even settlement design.
    - **Recent State (from my training):** The Peace and Friendship Treaty relationship is a live, evolving area of law and politics. There have been protests, court cases (e.g., fishery disputes), and ongoing negotiations. **Absolutely no land in Nova Scotia is free of this claim.**
