# Interview Brief — Hilary Beaumont (The Atlantic) / Land Selection Framework

**Call:** Thu 3 or Fri 4 July 2026, informal pre-interview (possibly on-record later)
**Her:** Climate journalist, science section. Angle: "land selection for the apocalypse." From Nova Scotia — family there, friends building homesteads/communes there. NS is one of your 20 regions.
**Read posture:** thoughtful, honest, regenerative-practitioner. You're not selling; you're describing a tool and a stance. Let the framework's discipline (it filters, never scores or recommends) do the de-hyping for you.

---

## 0. The one thing to keep returning to

It's a **bioregioning tool with reciprocity built in.** The point is helping a community *arrive in relationship* with a place and the people, lifeways, and Indigenous nations already there — not site-shopping, not landing on top of anyone. **"Where arriving would harm the community already there, the honest answer is: not there."**

The "apocalypse" word is her hook (and a light wink on your end). The substance is **regenerative community on a 50–100 year horizon.** Acknowledge the hook, then bridge to the substance every time.

---

## 1. The 30-second "what is this"

- A shared, open geospatial framework comparing **20 candidate regions** (10 in Europe, 10 in North America) across **8 land/region criteria** — climate trajectory to mid-century, water stress, soil carbon, forest-cover direction, solar potential, conflict proximity, regenerative-network density, population density.
- It exists because people siting community land for the next 50–100 years are making a **once-in-a-lifetime, high-cost decision** off data you can't get from real-estate listings — climate trends, soil, water futures, who already holds the land.
- Crucially: **it filters, it never scores, ranks, or recommends.** No composite "livability" number. The person combines the criteria themselves, against their own priorities.
- Every value carries its **source, vintage, and native units** — nothing is reshaped into a fake universal grid, nothing is hidden behind a black-box rating.

**Quotable:**
> "It's a bioregioning tool. It helps you think in watersheds and living systems instead of borders and property listings — and it's built so that arriving means entering relationship with a place, not buying something empty."

> "It deliberately won't give you a winner. There's no overall score. It shows you honest data and hands the judgment back to you, because a hundred-year decision shouldn't be made by a ranking algorithm."

## 2. Nova Scotia — "how did it come to be on your map?"

This is her personal in. Be specific and honest; NS is your **worked example of the reciprocity stance**, not a recommendation.

**Why it's a genuine candidate (the data):**
- **Cool maritime climate** — ~9.3 °C mean annual temperature mid-century (2041–2060). A climate envelope that stays livable as the world warms.
- **Low water stress** — 0.08 on the Aqueduct scale, about as water-secure as anywhere in the slate; ~1,300 mm rain a year.
- **Reachable and relatively affordable** — rural/Cape Breton parcels sit outside the federal foreign-buyer ban, and there are **provincial immigration pathways** in.
- **No conflict exposure**, moderate-to-high soil carbon (~40 g/kg).

**The named tensions — say these plainly:**
- **Forest-cover loss of about −8% per decade** from industrial forestry — the **second-worst forest trajectory of all 20 regions.** A real mark against it.
- **A thin regen scene** — roughly 5 sites (e.g. Treehouse Village ecovillage), plus an active organic-farming network. Sparse, but real.
- **It's unceded Mi'kma'ki.** Arriving there means entering obligation with the Mi'kmaq, not treating the land as available.
- Practical: a **2025 federal cut (~50%) to the provincial nominee quota** means international co-founders realistically settle one or two members first, then buy together; wastewater on thin fractured-bedrock soils and new late-summer drought are the real design problems.

**Quotable:**
> "Nova Scotia shows up well on water and climate, and you can actually get there. But it's losing forest faster than almost anywhere else on the map, the regen community is thin, and it's unceded Mi'kma'ki. So it's not a 'go here' — it's a 'here's what this place would ask of you, eyes open.'"

> "If your friends are already building there in relationship with the place, that's the real version of this. The framework is just the part you can put in a spreadsheet — the rest lives in conversations with the people who already know the ground."

## 3. The hardest questions — drafted honest answers

**(a) Isn't selecting land for (mostly privileged, Western) settlers a form of green colonialism / escapism?**
- Name it as the real risk, don't deflect. That's exactly why **reciprocity is built into the tool, not bolted on.**
- The framework explicitly refuses the site-shopping use. Much of this land is held in Indigenous or communal title; arriving is entering obligation, not acquiring something empty.
> "That's the failure mode the whole thing is designed against. The honest line in the project is: where arriving would harm the people already there, you don't go. A tool that helped privileged people parachute onto someone else's land would be the opposite of what this is for."
- Honest caveat: the tool can't *verify* that someone integrates well — "whether a place welcomes you, and on what terms, is trust built over decades, not a checkbox." The work sits with the person arriving.

**(b) Isn't this just doomer-prepping?**
> "Prepping is about retreat and stockpiling for a bad year. This is about putting down roots for a hundred years and leaving the land better than you found it. The horizon is the opposite of a bunker."
- The apocalypse framing is a wink; the substance is regeneration over generations. If anything it's anti-prepper: it rewards joining existing communities and building soil, not isolating.

**(c) Who is this for — and who is it NOT for?**
- **For:** serious groups making a long-horizon community-land decision who want shared, honest, transparent data instead of marketing. It's open-source, open-data, non-commercial.
- **Not for:** quick browsing, speculation, anyone looking for a "best place to flee" answer, or anyone wanting to land on top of an existing community. **"You choose once"** — it's built for a decision that has to be right the first time, not for shopping.

**(d) Who made it / who originated it? — AUTHORSHIP GUARDRAIL (be precise here)**
- **Do NOT claim you originated or founded it.** You hold the practitioner seat.
> "It started as a working group — Askja originated it, with Adam on the GIS architecture, Deca synthesizing, Monty on the research side. I came in as the practitioner reality-check, the eco-village-embedded perspective. I've been the most active person on it since the spring and I built the prototype, so in practice it's become mostly my project to carry — but the framework's bones aren't mine alone."
- If she wants names/attribution for print, flag that the others are collaborators you'd want to check with before naming them publicly.

**(e) Does it tell people where to flee?**
> "No. It doesn't recommend anything. It filters and shows data — you set what matters to you, it shows you what passes and what doesn't, with the sources attached. There's no 'go here' coming out of it, by design."

**(f) Why no overall score?**
> "Because the moment you collapse climate, water, soil, and who-holds-the-land into one number, you've hidden the most important judgments inside a weighting nobody agreed to. A community weighing this for a hundred years deserves to see the trade-offs, not a rating. That was a non-negotiable from the start, and we kept it."
- It's also intellectually honest: the data lives in **native units** with **state + trajectory** per criterion (current value *and* direction of travel), so you can see, e.g., a place that's water-rich today but trending dry.

## 4. Bridge lines (apocalypse hook → regeneration)

- "The apocalypse framing is half a joke — but the real question underneath it is serious: where can a community actually flourish for a hundred years? That's a regeneration question, not a survival one."
- "People come to this scared. The work is turning that fear into something generative — roots, soil, relationship — instead of a bunker."
- "It's less 'where do we run to' and more 'where could we belong, and what would belonging there actually ask of us.'"
- "Climate is the pressure that brings people to the question. Reciprocity is what keeps the answer from becoming just another land grab."

## 5. Landmines + things NOT to say

**Avoid:**
- Don't claim you founded/originated it. ("It's mostly my project now / I hold the practitioner seat" is true and fine. "I started it" is not.)
- Don't recommend Nova Scotia (or any region) as a place to go. It filters; it never recommends. Especially don't tell *her* where her friends should buy.
- Don't lean into the prepper/doomer/"escape the collapse" frame. Acknowledge it's the hook, then redirect to regeneration. Easy to get quoted out of context here.
- Don't present the numbers as precise truth. They're best-available, hand-curated midpoints with sources and vintages attached — say "best-available, sourced, and dated," not "accurate."
- Don't name or characterize the other collaborators publicly beyond roles without flagging you'd check with them first.
- Don't oversell the prototype — it's a *demonstration artifact*, not the V1 build, not a product. "It's a communicative prototype, not a finished tool."

**Watch the framing of NS forest loss:** be straight that −8%/decade is industrial-forestry driven and one of the worst in the slate — owning the bad number builds far more trust than spinning it, and she'll know the NS forestry story already.

**If asked something you don't know** (e.g. exact methodology, V2 timelines): "I don't want to overstate it — that's still an open question for the group" is always a clean answer.
