# Energy autonomy — South Tirol, Italy

**Headline finding:** South Tirol has one of Europe's most advanced regional renewable-energy profiles: hydropower already supplies the vast majority of electricity, solar potential is documented at building level via EURAC's Solar Tirol cadaster, and the province has a public 100%-renewable-by-2050 target with practical regulatory backing. Practitioners benefit from one of the most mature legal frameworks for self-consumption in Europe.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas long-term PVOUT for South Tirol: **~1,250–1,500 kWh/kWp/yr** depending on aspect and altitude (Solargis / World Bank Global Solar Atlas, 2020 release; consistent with EURAC's higher-resolution Solar Tirol cadaster, ongoing). Mid-altitude south-facing slopes (Vinschgau / Val Venosta) reach the upper end of this range.

**Supporting facts:**
- South Tirol's electricity supply has been dominated by hydropower for over a century; the province operates large reservoir and run-of-river hydro plants alongside hundreds of small/micro installations (Alperia, the regional energy company, holds the majority of large-hydro concessions).
- Global Wind Atlas (DTU / World Bank, v3.3, 2023) shows mean wind speed at 100 m of ~3–5 m/s in interior valleys — generally below commercial wind threshold; some high-ridge sites reach 7–8 m/s but are aesthetically/regulatorily constrained.
- EURAC Research's Solar Tirol cadaster provides freely accessible, building-level solar potential assessment — practitioners can determine roof-specific PV yield before purchasing or designing (EURAC Institute for Renewable Energy, ongoing).
- The province has an explicit 100%-renewable-by-2050 target ("KlimaPlan Energy-South Tyrol-2050"); the Italian *autoconsumo collettivo* and *CER* (Comunità Energetiche Rinnovabili) framework (Legislative Decree 199/2021) supports community-energy schemes with simplified permitting.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
South Tirol's energy environment is unusually settler-friendly: existing grid is robust, hydropower already covers most household needs, regulatory framework supports self-consumption and energy communities, and EURAC's research-and-services infrastructure means practitioners can get high-resolution technical advice. Micro-hydro is genuinely feasible on the dense alpine stream network — small plants (5–50 kW) using existing weirs or *Waale* infrastructure are widely installed and permitted via Provincia Autonoma's streamlined process. The bigger constraints are aesthetic (PV must integrate with traditional stone/wood architecture in protected zones) and economic (energy is already cheap; payback periods for additional renewable investment longer than in less-served regions). Wood-fuelled heating remains widely used for valley-floor farms; mid-altitude settlements increasingly combine PV + heat pump + biomass.
