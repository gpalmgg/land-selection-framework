# Legal + Economic — South Tirol, Italy

**Headline finding:** Italy places no nationality restriction on freehold land ownership for EU citizens and for nationals of states with reciprocity (which includes most OECD countries, US, Switzerland). South Tirol's distinguishing legal layer is the *Höfegesetz / Legge sui masi chiusi* (Closed-Farm Law) — a 16th-century institution preserved under the province's autonomy that prevents the partition or sale of a designated *geschlossener Hof* below a minimum economic-viability threshold. Land prices are the highest in Italy outside Barolo and a handful of vineyard zones.

**Key data point (with vintage):** North-East Italy (which includes Trentino-Alto Adige) agricultural land average 2024: **€47,100/ha** (Eurostat 2024 release published Jan 2026). South Tirol vineyard land specifically: **DOC Val Venosta/Isarco €300,000–€500,000/ha**, **Lake Caldaro €600,000–€1,100,000/ha** (Italian wine-press summaries 2024, citing CREA / SCENARI cooperative valuations). Italy national agricultural mean 2024: **~€22,400/ha** (~+1% YoY, CREA 2024). Mountain pasture (*Almweide*) in South Tirol values are heavily distorted by *maso chiuso* effects and rarely transact on open market.

**Supporting facts:**
- Italy ownership regime: foreign-buyer reciprocity rule via Article 16 of Disposizioni sulla legge in generale; most OECD nationals enjoy full ownership rights. Non-reciprocity states (rare) require case-by-case clearance.
- Standard purchase process: Codice Fiscale (Italian tax code), notarised compromesso → atto definitivo, registration with Agenzia delle Entrate and Catasto. Costs include 2–9% registration tax depending on classification (agricultural land registers differently from residential).
- South Tirol *Maso Chiuso* / *Geschlossener Hof*: provincial law LP 17/2001 and predecessors, rooted in 16th-century Tyrolean tradition. Designated farms must pass to a single heir (*Anerbe*) — typically the *Hofnachfolger* identified by the family — at a *Hofschätzwert* (assessed valuation often well below market). Partition is forbidden. The Maso Chiuso commission of the province administers this.
- *Maso Chiuso* prevents foreign buyers from acquiring fragmented parcels but does not prevent purchase of a complete Hof on the market; the practical issue is supply — few Höfe come to open market because most pass by family succession.
- Construction permits on rural land regulated by provincial planning law (Landesraumordnungsgesetz 2018, revised) and detailed municipal *Bauleitpläne*. Conversion of agricultural land to residential is tightly limited.
- Collective-ownership in South Tirol: *Eigenverwaltete Bürgerliche Nutzungsrechte* (citizen-administered commons rights) and *Interessentschaften* (interest-cooperatives) cover significant alpine common pasture; these are not open to outsiders.
- Italy has no equivalent of Spain's or Portugal's defunct Golden Visa; investor visa (€2m or €500k startup), self-employment, elective-residence (passive-income) visas are standard non-EU routes.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
South Tirol is the most legally distinctive of the 10 regions in this dossier. The *Maso Chiuso* regime is not a nationality barrier — it is a structural one: closed farms simply do not fragment, so the inventory of small parcels for sale is artificially constrained, and prices on what does transact are among Europe's highest. For non-EU buyers the legal path is open in theory but the inventory and the price point are real constraints. Foreign-led community projects almost never form here; the practical regen route is to operate as a tenant, partner, or biodiverse-conversion contractor within an existing Hof.
