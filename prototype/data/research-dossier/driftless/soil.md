# Soil & Biodiversity — Driftless Area (Southwest Wisconsin), United States

**Headline finding:** The Driftless Area's unglaciated landscape produced a distinctive soil mosaic: loess-mantled ridge tops and upper slopes carry Hapludalfs and Argiudolls (silt-loam soils with moderate organic carbon), while coulee bottoms hold deeper alluvial soils with higher carbon stocks. The most important data point for regenerative practitioners comes from a peer-reviewed digital soil mapping study conducted directly in the Driftless: mean topsoil (0–20 cm) organic carbon was measured at **23 g/kg**, with forested topsoils averaging 28 g/kg and agricultural topsoils lower. This is a moderate baseline — comparable to Cascadia or Connemara, lower than Saxony-Anhalt's Chernozems — with substantial regeneration potential given the region's history of erosion from conventional row-cropping on steep slopes.

**Key data point (with vintage):** SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC, 2020) and direct Driftless-specific soil carbon mapping: topsoil (0–20 cm) soil organic carbon mean **~23 g/kg** across the Driftless landscape, forested topsoils **~28 g/kg**, under-agricultural topsoils lower; from digital soil carbon mapping study using >400 soil samples across Crawford, Grant, Iowa, Lafayette, and Richland counties, Wisconsin (Adhikari et al., *Geoderma*, 2014, confirmed by USDA NRCS SSURGO county data, 2024).

**Supporting facts:**
- The Driftless Area was not covered by Pleistocene glaciation, so soils developed on weathered bedrock and loess deposits rather than glacial till. Dominant soil orders: Alfisols (Hapludalfs, Fragiudalfs) on ridge slopes; Mollisols (Argiudolls) on some flatter ridges with prairie history; Inceptisols and Entisols in steep coulee walls; alluvial Fluvaquents and Udifluvents in valley bottoms (USDA NRCS SSURGO, Wisconsin soil surveys).
- Loess deposits — wind-blown silt from glacial outwash to the east — cover much of the ridge terrain, forming parent material that is inherently silty and well-structured but erosion-prone on slopes. Thickness ranges from <1 m on steep coulee walls to 3–5 m on broad ridgetops.
- The peer-reviewed Driftless digital soil carbon study (Adhikari et al., 2014, *Geoderma* 230–231) found mean topsoil (0–20 cm) SOC of 23 g/kg across ~400 sample sites spanning the Wisconsin Driftless landscape, with forest cover as the strongest predictor of higher SOC (28 g/kg under forest vs. lower under row crops).
- Conventional row-cropping on steep Driftless slopes has historically caused significant sheet and rill erosion — some of the worst-eroding agricultural land in the Midwest. USDA NRCS Kickapoo River watershed restoration work has documented 1–3 cm of topsoil lost per decade in the worst-eroded fields. This is the dominant soil liability.
- Regenerative land-use conversion can substantially increase SOC: studies across the Upper Midwest document 0.3–0.8 t C/ha/yr gains under perennial cover; New Forest Farm's 106-acre Driftless perennial system (Viola, WI) is a documented local example of carbon sequestration through agroforestry on former row-crop land.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
A Driftless soil assessment has two faces depending on where on the landscape you look. Ridge-top fields that have been farmed for generations with conventional tillage may have lost 20–40% of their original topsoil depth — you are starting from a degraded baseline that regenerative practice can meaningfully restore, but the initial carbon stocks are lower than they appear from area averages. Coulee-bottom alluvial soils and wooded slopes tell a better story: these have often escaped the worst tillage-driven erosion and can carry genuinely high organic matter. The practitioner strategy is therefore to choose parcels carefully by topographic position (prioritise deeper valley alluvium and any remaining woodland soils over historically cropped steep slopes), then design permanent cover on all slopes immediately. The good news: regenerative return on investment is high here precisely because the degradation baseline means there is a lot of carbon to recover.
