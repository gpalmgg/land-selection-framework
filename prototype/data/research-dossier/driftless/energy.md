# Energy — Driftless Area (Southwest Wisconsin), United States

**Headline finding:** Southwest Wisconsin sits in the lower tier of US solar resource — the Driftless Area receives approximately 1,350–1,380 kWh/kWp/yr specific PV yield, comparable to southern Germany or the UK's better regions, and meaningfully lower than the US Southwest or even central France. This is workable for off-grid and net-metered systems but requires correctly-sized battery banks and higher installed capacity relative to southern US benchmarks. Wind resources in the coulees and ridges are modest. The region's real energy story is thermal: cold winters demand serious heating load (typically 6,000–7,000 degree-days base 65°F), and passive solar design, super-insulation, and earth-sheltered construction are the appropriate first responses — not maximising PV area.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas v2.7 / NREL NSRDB specific PV yield for SW Wisconsin (approx. 43.5°N, 90.8°W): **~1,350–1,380 kWh/kWp/yr** (derived from: NSRDB data for La Crosse area showing mean annual GHI of 3.77 kWh/m²/day = ~1,376 kWh/m²/yr; conversion to specific yield at standard system efficiency gives ~1,350–1,380 kWh/kWp/yr for fixed-tilt optimised-angle systems; consistent with NREL PVWatts regional benchmarks for the Upper Midwest, 2024).

**Supporting facts:**
- Mean annual global horizontal irradiance (GHI) for the La Crosse / Onalaska area (nearest NSRDB station to Driftless core): 3.77 kWh/m²/day, equivalent to ~1,376 kWh/m²/yr (Solar Energy Local / NREL NSRDB data, 2024).
- Monthly GHI varies dramatically: December nadir ~2.2 kWh/m²/day, July peak ~6.7 kWh/m²/day — a 3:1 seasonal swing that means winter solar production is deeply limited and battery/backup sizing must account for 4–6 consecutive low-production days in December–January.
- For comparison: Madison, WI has a similar mean GHI of 3.83 kWh/m²/day; Phoenix, AZ is ~5.8 kWh/m²/day; southwest Germany ~3.4 kWh/m²/day. The Driftless is slightly better than Germany's sunniest regions and substantially better than Ireland or Scotland.
- Heating degree-days (base 65°F / 18.3°C) for La Crosse: approximately 6,800–7,000 HDD/yr — among the highest in the contiguous US Midwest, comparable to Minnesota and far above anything in Western Europe. This determines community energy budgets more than PV yield.
- Wisconsin has net metering law (Wis. Stat. §196.655) and interconnection standards, making grid-tied PV financially viable for parcels with utility access; rural properties often rely on propane or heating oil for backup, with growing uptake of cold-climate heat pumps (which work to −25°C modern units) as an electrification pathway.
- Wind: the Driftless coulee topography funnels some ridge-top wind resources but the area is not a primary wind energy zone; commercial-scale wind turbines are present in Grant and Iowa counties, but small-scale on-farm wind is marginal at most sites.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The energy design imperative for a Driftless settlement is thermal efficiency first, electricity generation second. A community designing for 50–100-year resilience should prioritise: (1) passive solar orientation of all heated structures — south-facing slopes (the Driftless has plenty of them) with earth-sheltering into north-facing coulee walls; (2) superinsulation to Passive House or close-equivalent standards, reducing heating load to a fraction of conventional construction; (3) right-sized PV systems with honest winter-production expectations — plan for 25–30% of summer yield in December; (4) biomass backup as a regionally-available hedge (the Driftless has significant woodland under active forestry management). The region's abundant biomass from agroforestry systems (chestnuts, hazelnuts, coppice) and managed woodland make combined heat and power a credible long-horizon option.
