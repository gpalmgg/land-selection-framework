# Legal + Economic — Driftless Area (Southwest Wisconsin), United States

**Headline finding:** Wisconsin's land regime is open to all US citizens and legal residents without restriction, and currently permits foreign nationals to hold up to 640 acres of rural land under Wisconsin Statute §710.02 — though 2025 legislation (AB 218, passed Assembly, pending Senate as of early 2026) proposes tightening this to 50 acres for agricultural land. For community land trusts and cooperative ownership structures, US law provides excellent vehicles: LLCs, nonprofit land trusts, and cooperative corporations are all well-established. Land prices in the Driftless core are among the most affordable agricultural land in the Upper Midwest — cropland averaging $6,800/acre statewide in 2024, with Driftless-specific parcels (steep, wooded, or mixed) regularly available at $3,500–$6,000/acre, and use-value property tax assessment dramatically reducing the annual tax burden on farmland.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Wisconsin average cropland value 2024: **$6,800/acre ($16,800/ha)**, up $180 from 2023; Wisconsin average overall farm real estate: **$6,120/acre ($15,100/ha)**, unchanged from 2023; Driftless-specific mixed/wooded parcels often transact at **$3,500–$6,500/acre ($8,600–$16,000/ha)** depending on slope, road access, and tillable proportion (USDA NASS Wisconsin Cash Rent and Land Values 2024; Vernon County transaction data from Farmland Intel and local listings, 2024).

**Supporting facts:**
- **Use-value assessment (Wisconsin's most important farmland legal instrument):** Under Wisconsin Statute §70.32(2) and the 1995 Act 27 framework, agricultural land is taxed at its income-generating use value, not market value. In practice, cropland use-value of ~$242/acre (Wisconsin DOR, 2024 guidelines) versus market values of $6,000–8,000+/acre means assessed value for tax purposes is approximately **96% below market** — a transformative ongoing cost reduction for active farming operations.
- **Farmland Preservation Program:** Wisconsin's Farmland Preservation Program (Chapter 91, Wis. Stats.) offers direct tax credits to landowners who record a farmland preservation agreement (covenant running with the land) committing to agricultural use. Credits average $10/acre/yr for agreements and up to $200/acre/yr for certified land in an agricultural enterprise area. This is functionally a subsidy to keep land in production.
- **Managed Forest Law (MFL):** Wooded parcels can enrol in MFL, reducing property taxes to $0.74–$2.04/acre/yr (2024 rates) in exchange for 25- or 50-year sustainable forestry management commitments. For Driftless parcels with significant woodland, this is a material annual saving.
- **Cooperative and community ownership structures:** Wisconsin recognises cooperative corporations under Chapter 185 of Wisconsin Statutes, making farmer cooperatives straightforward to form. Community land trusts (CLTs) operate as §501(c)(3) nonprofits and can hold land perpetually, separating ownership from usage rights — a well-established US model. LLCs with operating agreements specifying multiple-member governance are commonly used for intentional community land ownership.
- **Foreign ownership — current law:** Wisconsin Stat. §710.02 limits non-resident aliens, foreign corporations, and entities with >20% foreign ownership to a maximum of 640 acres of Wisconsin land, with a 4-year divestiture requirement for violations. This cap has been in place for decades. AB 218 (2025), if enacted, would reduce the cap to 50 acres for agricultural land, require divestiture within 3 years, and add prohibitions near military installations.
- **No restrictions on EU/European community members:** There is no state or federal barrier to European nationals purchasing land as individuals or via US-registered entities, provided they have appropriate immigration status for residence purposes. Land purchase itself does not grant any immigration benefit.
- **Federal AFIDA reporting:** The Agricultural Foreign Investment Disclosure Act (AFIDA) requires foreign persons to report US agricultural land acquisitions to USDA FSA within 90 days — a reporting obligation, not a restriction (for non-adversary-nation buyers).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The legal and economic environment for a regenerative community in the Driftless is among the most favourable in this framework. Use-value assessment is a profound structural advantage: a community that actively farms (even in perennial systems — Wisconsin's use-value programme includes agricultural forest) will pay property taxes closer to $30–80/year per acre rather than the $500–900/acre that market-value assessment would generate. This changes the multi-decade financial model materially. The corporate farming statute (§182.001) restricts certain corporate structures from owning operational farmland, but cooperatives with natural-person members and nonprofit land trusts are explicitly carve-outs — well-suited to community ownership models. The foreign-ownership legal uncertainty is worth monitoring: AB 218 is a live threat and could restrict non-US members' direct land holding. The pragmatic response is to use a US-registered cooperative or CLT as the land-holding entity from day one, sidestepping the individual foreign-ownership question entirely.
