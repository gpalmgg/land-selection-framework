# Legal + Economic — Québec Eastern Townships (Estrie), Canada

**Headline finding:** Québec operates a fundamentally different legal and land regime from the rest of Canada — and from any European comparator. Two distinct layers govern land acquisition here: (1) the Québec *Civil Code* (civil law, not common law), which shapes property rights, notarial transaction requirements, and collective ownership structures; and (2) the CPTAQ (*Commission de protection du territoire agricole du Québec*) together with the newly enacted Bill 86 (SQ 2025, c. 5, assented March 2025), which is now the strongest agricultural-land protection regime in North America. For a regen settlement seeking to steward rural land long-term, the CPTAQ is simultaneously the most protective institution (preserving agricultural land from speculation) and the most constraining (requiring demonstrated agricultural intent for any non-farmer acquisition in the Green Zone).

**Key data point (with vintage):** Québec agricultural farmland average value 2024 (Farm Credit Canada Farmland Values Report 2024): **CAD $17,720/acre** provincial average (~$43,800/ha), with the Montérégie region at $22,800/acre ($56,300/ha) and Estrie/Eastern Townships below the provincial average — estimated CAD $8,000–$14,000/acre (~$20,000–$35,000/ha) based on regional comparables and FCC commentary on "more tempered" Eastern Townships pricing versus Montérégie. Québec farmland values rose **+7.7% in 2024** and +4.8% in first half 2025 (FCC Farmland Values Report 2024–2025).

**Supporting facts:**

*Civil law framework:*
- Québec's legal system derives from the French Civil Code (Napoleonic tradition), not English common law — the only civil law jurisdiction in North America. The *Civil Code of Québec* (CCQ, 1994) governs all private law including immovable property.
- Property transfers must be executed before a notary (*acte authentique*) — this is mandatory, not optional, unlike the rest of Canada where lawyers handle conveyancing. The notary registers the transaction directly with the Québec Land Registry (*Registre foncier*).
- Collective ownership structures available: *Association* (non-profit, Loi sur les compagnies, Part III), *Coopérative* (Loi sur les coopératives — powerful and well-established in Québec), *Fiducie* (trust, under CCQ articles 1260–1298), *Emphytéose* (long-term emphyteutic lease separating ground rights from surface improvements, useful for separating land from built assets). The Québec *fiducie d'utilité sociale* (social utility trust) is a growing instrument for community land-holding.

*CPTAQ and agricultural-land protection:*
- The *Act Respecting the Preservation of Agricultural Land and Agricultural Activities* (LPTAA, P-41.1, in force since 1978) established the permanent "Green Zone" (*zone agricole*) covering all prime and secondary agricultural land in Québec. Inclusion in the Green Zone does not require government acquisition — it is a planning designation. Activities on Green Zone land are restricted to agriculture by default.
- The CPTAQ authorizes exclusions from the Green Zone (to allow residential or commercial development) and authorizes non-agricultural uses within it. Applications are public, contested, and slow (12–36 months typical).
- *Act Respecting the Acquisition of Farm Land by Non-Residents* (A-4.1, 1979): non-residents of Québec wishing to acquire agricultural land over 4 hectares must obtain CPTAQ authorization. The CPTAQ grants authorization if (a) the land is not suitable for cultivation, or (b) the buyer declares intent to become a resident of Québec. Non-resident status includes non-Canadian citizens, foreign companies, and Canadians not residing in Québec.
- **Bill 86 (SQ 2025, c. 5, assented 25 March 2025)**: The most significant revision to the LPTAA since 1978. Key measures: (1) Moratorium (retroactive to December 5, 2024) on agricultural land purchase by investment funds and non-agricultural legal entities; (2) Most non-farmer buyers now require prior CPTAQ authorization before any agricultural land purchase — not just non-residents; (3) Non-resident share acquisitions that would make a land-holding corporation "non-resident" now require CPTAQ authorization; (4) Penalties up to $5,000 (natural persons) / $10,000 (legal entities) for non-compliance.
- The CPTAQ evaluates non-farmer acquisitions on the criterion that the buyer intends agricultural use, not financial or speculative purpose. A regen community with genuine agricultural programming (CSA, market garden, organic farm, permaculture demonstration) is more likely to satisfy this criterion than a passive investment or lifestyle purchase.

*Language:*
- French is the sole official language of Québec. The *Charte de la langue française* (Bill 101) requires that government services, legal documents, CPTAQ applications, municipal permits, and commercial contracts be in French. Notarial deeds are in French.

*Federal foreign-buyer restriction:*
- The federal *Prohibition on the Purchase of Residential Property by Non-Canadians Act* (2022, extended to January 1, 2027) bans non-Canadians from purchasing residential properties within Census Metropolitan Areas (CMAs) and Census Agglomerations (CAs). Rural Estrie land outside Sherbrooke's CMA is generally exempt. This restriction is residential-property focused — it does not override CPTAQ's agricultural-land regime.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The CPTAQ is the defining institution for any regen land project in Estrie. It is not hostile to regenerative use — it exists precisely to keep agricultural land in agricultural use — but it is procedurally demanding and does not move quickly. A community land trust or cooperative purchasing agricultural land in the Green Zone must demonstrate credible agricultural intent: a farm plan, actual agricultural activity, or registration as an agricultural operation under MAPAQ. Projects that frame themselves purely as residential communities, spiritual retreats, or lifestyle developments will struggle at the CPTAQ. Projects that lead with the agricultural mission — a farm, a market garden, agroforestry — and layer community living onto that foundation are exactly what the regime was designed to protect and support. This is a significant alignment between Québec's regulatory intent and genuine regen values. Bill 86's investment-fund ban, in particular, is a significant protective factor: it actively discourages speculative land aggregation and is likely to keep rural Estrie more accessible to genuine practitioners than to capital investors over the coming decade.
