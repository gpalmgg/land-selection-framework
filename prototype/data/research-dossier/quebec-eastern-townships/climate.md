# Climate — Québec Eastern Townships (Estrie), Canada

**Headline finding:** The Eastern Townships sit in one of northeastern North America's most livable climate zones — a humid continental regime (Köppen Dfb) with cold but navigable winters and warm, productive summers. By mid-century under SSP2-4.5, Ouranos projects southern Québec warming by roughly 2–3°C relative to the 1991–2020 baseline, lifting Sherbrooke's current mean annual temperature (~5.8°C) toward approximately 7.5–8.5°C by 2041–2060. This is warming, but it arrives from a cold baseline: the region is not approaching heat extremes but is shedding some of its hardest frost weeks and extending its growing season meaningfully.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Mean annual temperature 2041–2060 under SSP2-4.5 (WorldClim CMIP6 v2.1 multi-model ensemble + Ouranos CMIP6 downscaling, 2024): approximately **7.5–8.5°C** for the Sherbrooke/Estrie core, up from a 1981–2010 baseline mean of ~5.8°C (Environment Canada Climate Normals, Sherbrooke station, period 1981–2010). The Ouranos ESPO-G6-R2 v1.0 ensemble projects a 2–3°C annual mean increase for southern Québec relative to 1991–2020 under SSP3-7.0; SSP2-4.5 projections for 2041–2060 fall at the low end of this range (~2°C above baseline).

**Supporting facts:**
- Current climate (1981–2010 normals): mean annual temperature ~5.8°C; mean July high +25°C; mean January low −18°C; annual precipitation ~1,050 mm well distributed year-round (Environment Canada, Sherbrooke station 7017600).
- Frost-free period currently ~140–150 days; by 2050 this is projected to lengthen by 2–3 weeks across southern Québec (Ouranos, Temperatures — Projected Changes, 2024).
- Annual precipitation is projected to increase by 5–10% in winter and spring and remain stable or slightly decrease in summer under SSP2-4.5, reducing drought risk relative to European regen regions (Ouranos, Precipitation — Projected Changes, 2024).
- Growing degree days (base 5°C) currently ~2,000–2,200 GDD/yr around Sherbrooke; by 2050 projected to rise by ~300–400 GDD — extending viable zones for temperate tree crops (apples, walnuts, some chestnuts).
- The humid continental climate produces reliable snowpack (average winter snow depth 40–70 cm), feeding spring soil moisture across the rolling Appalachian landscape.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The climate story here is the inverse of Alentejo or southern Transylvania: practitioners are not adapting to heat stress but to cold seasonality and variable shoulder seasons. The risk trajectory is positive for growing-season length and negative for infrastructure requiring no freeze-thaw cycling. Buildings must be designed for −25°C winter lows and for ice-dam and foundation-frost loads — both well-understood in local vernacular. Conversely, a warming climate is reducing heating-degree days and extending outdoor-work windows, which is net-positive for regen community operations. Flash flood risk is rising across southern Québec as precipitation intensity increases even if total summer volumes decline; earthworks and water management design should account for higher peak runoff in storm events.
