# Water — Québec Eastern Townships (Estrie), Canada

**Headline finding:** The Eastern Townships sit within one of the most water-abundant regions in North America. Québec as a province holds approximately 3% of the world's renewable freshwater, and the Estrie region is threaded by the Saint-François River watershed with dozens of significant lakes (Memphremagog at 42 km long, Grand Lac Saint-François at 51 km², Massawippi, Brome, and Mégantic). WRI Aqueduct 4.0 classifies the sub-basins covering southern Québec in the lowest water-stress category — effectively no meaningful scarcity now or in the 2050 BAU projection. This is a region where water abundance is the baseline assumption, not the prize.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 baseline water stress for the Saint-François watershed (sub-basin covering Sherbrooke/Estrie center): **Low (score < 0.1 on the 0–1 ratio scale)** — total withdrawals represent well under 10% of available renewable supply. The 2050 BAU projection (SSP3-RCP7.0) shows no meaningful deterioration for this sub-basin; Canada does not appear in the high-stress tier of Aqueduct 4.0's country rankings. (WRI Aqueduct 4.0, 2023 release; Canada national context confirmed via MELCCFP Québec Water Strategy 2018–2030.)

**Supporting facts:**
- The Saint-François River drains a 10,200 km² watershed entirely within Estrie/Centre-du-Québec, with 18 managed reservoirs providing multi-year storage buffer (Hydro-Québec watershed management data, 2024).
- Lake Memphremagog is the primary drinking-water source for Sherbrooke (population ~172,000); municipal water security is classified as high by the Québec Ministry of Environment (MELCCFP).
- Annual precipitation in the region averages ~1,050 mm distributed year-round with no pronounced dry season — unlike Mediterranean or semi-arid regen candidate regions.
- Groundwater: the Appalachian geology of Estrie supports productive fractured-rock aquifers; private wells are common in rural areas with high success rates (IRDA Québec, 2022).
- Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) blooms have been documented on some heavily-used lakes (Lac Brome, Lac Magog) in warm summers — an eutrophication concern driven by agricultural runoff, not scarcity. Water quality management, not quantity, is the practitioner challenge.
- Climate projections show increased winter/spring precipitation and stable or slightly reduced summer precipitation for Estrie by 2050 — spring flood risk rises, summer low-flow stress remains minimal (Ouranos, 2024).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Water abundance should not be mistaken for water simplicity. The practitioner challenges here are qualitative, not quantitative: agricultural runoff (phosphorus from dairy-intensive farming) affects lake quality and requires careful site positioning relative to downstream water bodies; beaver-driven wetland dynamics are a feature of the landscape and require integration rather than conflict in earthworks design; and ice and spring thaw produce significant hydrology events that earthworks and roads must accommodate. For a regen settlement, the Saint-François watershed offers exceptional water-security resilience — but a site audit for water quality (well testing, proximity to agricultural drains) is essential before purchase.
