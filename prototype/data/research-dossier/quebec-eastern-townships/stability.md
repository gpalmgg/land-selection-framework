# Stability — Québec Eastern Townships (Estrie), Canada

**Headline finding:** The Eastern Townships and the broader 200 km radius centered on Sherbrooke contain zero recorded events in the UCDP Georeferenced Event Dataset (GED v24.1, 1989–2023). Canada has not experienced organized armed conflict meeting the UCDP threshold (≥1 direct death from organized violence between armed actors) on its territory since the dataset's coverage begins in 1989. This is one of the strongest stability records of any region on Earth. The relevant risk vectors for a 50–100 year horizon are political and institutional rather than physical: Québec sovereignty debates, federal-provincial tensions, and the specific regulatory regime governing agricultural land.

**Key data point (with vintage):** UCDP GED v24.1 armed-conflict events within ~200 km of Sherbrooke [−72.0, 45.4], 1989–2023: **0 events**. Canada's overall political stability score (World Bank Governance Indicators 2023): **+1.10** on the −2.5 to +2.5 scale (90th percentile globally).

**Supporting facts:**
- Canada ranks consistently in the top 5 of the Global Peace Index (Institute for Economics and Peace, 2024 edition), and Québec specifically has no history of armed internal conflict in the UCDP data period.
- Political risk specific to Québec: the sovereignty movement (Parti Québécois / Bloc Québécois) remains an active political current. The 1995 referendum on separation narrowly failed (50.6% No). A future referendum is not currently on the agenda, but the possibility creates a long-horizon governance uncertainty — particularly for foreign investors holding land under Québec provincial law.
- Québec civil law continuity: the Civil Code of Québec has been stable and well-administered since 1994; any sovereignty scenario would face the challenge of legal continuity with the federal system, but the civil law private-property framework itself is unlikely to be materially disrupted.
- No significant natural disaster risk: no major earthquake, tsunami, hurricane, or volcanic risk. The region experiences periodic ice storms (notably the January 1998 ice storm that left millions without power for weeks) — the primary infrastructure resilience challenge. Subsequent grid hardening and community experience with these events is now substantial.
- Crime rates: Estrie's crime severity index is below the national average (Statistics Canada, Uniform Crime Reporting Survey 2022). Sherbrooke, the regional capital, has crime rates typical of a medium-sized Canadian city.
- Indigenous land rights: the Eastern Townships are primarily within territory whose Indigenous title was not formally ceded by treaty. Abenaki and other First Nations peoples hold ongoing land-rights claims across the region — a legal and ethical dimension that practitioners should research carefully before site selection.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The zero-conflict record is unambiguous and durable. The legitimate stability risks for a 50–100 year regen settlement in Estrie are quieter: the Québec sovereignty question, evolving federal foreign-buyer restrictions, and unresolved Indigenous territorial claims. Of these, the Indigenous dimension is the most ethically load-bearing — practitioners considering site selection in the Eastern Townships should proactively engage with Abenaki Nation governance and understand whether the specific parcel is within a recognized claim area. This is not just ethical due diligence; in a 50-year horizon it is also prudent legal risk management as Canadian Indigenous rights jurisprudence continues to expand.
