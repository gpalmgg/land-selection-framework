# Soil — Québec Eastern Townships (Estrie), Canada

**Headline finding:** Estrie's soils are a mixed inheritance of the Appalachian geological formation — Brunisols and Podzols dominating the forested uplands, with fertile Gleysols and Luvisols in valley bottoms and former agricultural lowlands. Topsoil organic carbon is moderate-to-good by global comparison: SoilGrids 2.0 estimates for this humid continental temperate-deciduous zone typically run 25–45 g/kg SOC in the 0–30 cm horizon under permanent vegetation, with agricultural fields somewhat lower due to tillage history. The Canada Land Inventory rates a meaningful proportion of Estrie farmland as CLI Class 2–3 (capable of sustained crop production with moderate limitations), making it legitimate agricultural territory rather than marginal land.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Topsoil SOC (0–30 cm) estimated via SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC, 2023 release) for the Sherbrooke/Estrie area centred at [−72.0, 45.4]: approximately **25–40 g/kg** (mean ~32 g/kg) under mixed agricultural and forested land cover — consistent with humid continental Brunisol/Podzol systems. Under long-term grassland or perennial cover, values toward 40–50 g/kg are documented in regional soil surveys (Soil Landscapes of Canada SLC 3.2; IRDA Québec soil health assessment 2017–2022).

**Supporting facts:**
- Dominant soil orders in Estrie: Brunisols (brown Bm horizon, moderate fertility, less leached than Podzols) on gentle Appalachian slopes; Podzols (thick organic horizon, acidic, forest-adapted) on steep or rocky terrain; Gleysols in wetland margins and valley floors (Soils of Quebec, Saskatchewan OER, 2023).
- Canada Land Inventory (CLI) for the Stanstead, Richmond, and Compton county areas (core of Eastern Townships) documents that "the climate and the variety of soils support all types of farming, such as dairying, vegetable, fruit, poultry, livestock, cash crop, and mixed farming" (AAFC Soil Survey PQ-18, 1978, field data still valid for soil classification).
- Dairy farming has historically been the dominant land use in Estrie — the legacy of ruminant grazing means a significant fraction of agricultural soils have received consistent organic matter inputs, keeping SOC values higher than cash-crop monoculture regions.
- IRDA Québec conducted a province-wide soil health assessment (2017–2022) covering Estrie, identifying moderate compaction in some intensively farmed lowland parcels but generally healthy biological activity indices — earthworm populations, aggregate stability — in fields transitioning from dairy to mixed production.
- Soil pH in agricultural areas is typically 5.8–6.5 — slightly acidic, lime-responsive, well within the range correctable with standard agroecological inputs.
- Active reforestation on abandoned marginal farmland is building topsoil SOC across the landscape; the Statistics Québec land-use analysis found ~73 km² of former Estrie agricultural land reverted to forest between the 2000s and 2010s, representing ongoing carbon accumulation.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Soil quality in Estrie is genuinely good by northern temperate standards, but it is heterogeneous. The rolling Appalachian topography creates sharp transitions within a single parcel: productive loamy soils on south-facing mid-slopes, shallow rocky soils on ridges, wet anaerobic soils in depressions. A regen practitioner cannot assume uniform fertility across a parcel — a soil survey (including organic matter, pH, and compaction profiling) at the 1:5,000 scale is a worthwhile pre-purchase investment. The clay-loam soils prevalent in the St-François valley bottomlands are highly productive but prone to compaction under vehicle traffic during the spring thaw window — machinery timing and controlled-traffic principles matter here.
