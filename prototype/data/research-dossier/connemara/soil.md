# Soil & Biodiversity — West Cork / Connemara, Ireland

**Headline finding:** Western Irish soils are dominated by blanket peat and shallow podzols over acidic bedrock — high in stored carbon, low in agricultural fertility, and extraordinarily sensitive to disturbance. Connemara's blanket bogs and West Cork's upland heath are Natura 2000-protected and globally significant; the practitioner trade-off is between honouring undisturbed carbon stocks and converting to productive ground.

**Key data point (with vintage):** SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC, 2020 release) reports topsoil (0–5 cm) soil organic carbon for blanket-peat areas of the Irish west at **>120 g/kg** (and often 300+ g/kg on intact bog). Ireland's bogs hold ~53–62% of the national soil organic carbon despite covering only ~20% of the land area (EPA / Teagasc estimates, 2010s; recent peatland-mapping refinements 2024).

**Supporting facts:**
- Carbon stock values for blanket peats range from ~240 t C/ha (lowland blanket peats) to >3,000 t C/ha (deep raised bogs) — an order of magnitude greater than typical mineral-soil stocks (Tomlinson, 2005; recent re-estimates via Sentinel-2 mapping, 2024).
- Connemara National Park, Killarney National Park, and the Caha Mountains protect mosaics of blanket bog, oak woodland, and heath as Special Areas of Conservation under the EU Habitats Directive (NPWS designations, ongoing).
- Mineral soils on better-drained moraines and valley floors (Stagnosols, Gleysols, shallow Cambisols) are acidic, often heavy-textured, and historically managed as wet pasture; native soils rarely productive for grain but well-suited to grass-based livestock.
- Invasive *Rhododendron ponticum* is degrading blanket-bog and oak-woodland SACs across the west — practitioners adjacent to invaded sites inherit a multi-decade clearance liability.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
This is the *peat tension* flagged in the PRD made concrete. Disturbing intact blanket bog — by drainage, ploughing, peat cutting, or afforestation — releases century-deep carbon stocks rapidly and is functionally irreversible on settlement timescales. A practitioner siting on blanket bog faces a stark choice: leave the bog intact and design above/around it (raised platforms, careful track-building, no drainage), or accept a substantial carbon debt at the start of the project. The historic "improvement" pattern of cutting and draining bog for grass is now widely understood to be the wrong move climatically *and* economically — restored bogs hold and sequester carbon faster than they release once rewetted. A serious practitioner in the west of Ireland engages with the National Parks and Wildlife Service early; many parcels carry SAC or NHA designations that constrain land-use changes by law.
