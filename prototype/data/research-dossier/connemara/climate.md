# Climate — West Cork / Connemara, Ireland

**Headline finding:** The west of Ireland is projected to warm comparatively slowly — roughly +0.7–1.5°C mean annual temperature by mid-century under intermediate scenarios — and remain wet, cloudy, and Atlantic-maritime. The trajectory points to longer growing seasons, fewer frosts, more intense rainfall events, and less wind on average. Practitioners here face a climate that drifts rather than transforms.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Mean annual temperature projection 2041–2060 under SSP2-4.5 for the west of Ireland: **~11.0–11.8°C** (Met Éireann / ICHEC TRANSLATE high-resolution regional projections, EPA Research 471, 2023; near-surface temperature rise of 0.5–0.7°C 2021–2050 under SSP1-2.6 nationally, with smaller increases over the west than the east).

**Supporting facts:**
- Met Éireann / EPA project longer growing seasons, decreases in frost and snow, and increases in both dry and heavy rainfall events for Ireland through 2050 (EPA Climate Ireland, 2023).
- Mean annual precipitation 1,200–2,400 mm depending on aspect and elevation; total precipitation projected stable to mildly increasing in winter, with summer drying of ~5–10% in the southwest by mid-century (EPA Research 471, 2023).
- Atlantic-edge location makes the region highly sensitive to AMOC-shift scenarios — a substantial Gulf Stream weakening would cool the west of Ireland against the global warming trend; IPCC AR6 frames this as low-probability but not negligible by 2100.
- Storminess and Atlantic-low frequency projected to shift northward; mean wind speed expected to decrease slightly through 2050, but storm extremes increase (Met Éireann climate change overview, 2024).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
For a settlement, the western Irish climate trajectory is among the most favourable in Europe in terms of *not* being pushed across thresholds: no projected heat-stress regime change, no drought regime change of consequence, no fire regime change. The risks are subtler. Wetter, milder winters favour fungal disease pressure on crops and structures; storm-intensification reinforces the case for sheltered siting and over-built roof structures. The AMOC-shift tail risk deserves explicit scenario-planning at a settlement designed for multi-generational occupation — the west of Ireland is one of the few places where the "what if global warming reverses locally" question is non-trivial. Practitioners siting here are essentially betting on Atlantic-temperate continuity, with modest drift.
