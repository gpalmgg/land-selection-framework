# Energy autonomy — West Cork / Connemara, Ireland

**Headline finding:** The west of Ireland is among Europe's strongest onshore and offshore wind resources, with Class 5–6 wind on coastal ridges. PV is genuinely weak by European standards. Micro-hydro is feasible on a small number of Atlantic-flowing rivers; biomass and air-source heat-pumps dominate the practical heating stack.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas long-term PVOUT for the west of Ireland: **~900–1,050 kWh/kWp/yr** (Solargis / World Bank Global Solar Atlas, 2020 release, ~1 km resolution). This is among the lowest in continental Europe — about half of Alentejo.

**Supporting facts:**
- Global Wind Atlas (DTU / World Bank, v3.3, 2023) shows mean wind speed at 100 m of **8–11 m/s** along the west coast — IEC Class S / IEC Class I conditions, supporting commercial-scale onshore turbines and floating offshore developments.
- Ireland's offshore wind pipeline includes ESB / Equinor's 1,400 MW floating wind farm off Clare and Kerry; SEAI (Sustainable Energy Authority of Ireland) supports community and microgeneration with the Microgeneration Support Scheme (MSS, active 2022–) paying for excess export up to 6 kW domestic / 50 kW non-domestic.
- Planning permission for individual small turbines in Connemara and West Cork remains feasible under rural-zoning provisions but is politically sensitive in scenic landscapes; community-led schemes (e.g., Templederry, Cloughjordan, Aran Islands) provide established legal templates.
- Domestic heating: peat fuel is being phased out under Ireland's Climate Action Plan; firewood, wood-pellet boilers, and air-source heat-pumps now dominate new-build practice. Average household heating-energy demand in older West Cork rural stock is high (~15–20 MWh/year) due to thermal performance of traditional construction.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The west of Ireland inverts the energy assumptions a Mediterranean or central-European practitioner brings. PV alone cannot reasonably power a household here; a 10 kWp array generates ~9,500 kWh/year, but skewed heavily toward summer when heating demand is lowest. The viable stack is small-wind (5–15 kW) plus PV plus battery plus a serious thermal envelope upgrade (external insulation, heat-recovery ventilation) plus either heat-pump or biomass winter heating. SEAI's Better Energy Homes scheme and the One-Stop Shops program substantially de-risk the retrofit side of this. Practitioners building new should target Passivhaus or near-Passivhaus standards — the climate's mildness makes this achievable, and it collapses winter energy demand by 70–80% versus default construction.
