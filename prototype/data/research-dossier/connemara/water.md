# Water — West Cork / Connemara, Ireland

**Headline finding:** The west of Ireland has Europe's most water-abundant settings outside high mountain ranges: 1,200–2,400 mm annual precipitation, an order of magnitude more reliable summer flow than the Iberian comparators, and almost no water-quantity stress in the WRI Aqueduct mapping. The binding constraints are water *quality* (peat-stained, locally acidic), drainage management on heavy ground, and groundwater scarcity in fractured-bedrock aquifers.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 baseline water stress for the west of Ireland: **<0.1 (low)** across virtually all of Connemara and West Cork (Aqueduct 4.0 dataset, 2023 release). Drought risk also among the lowest in Europe.

**Supporting facts:**
- Mean annual precipitation 1,200 mm (eastern West Cork lowlands) to >2,400 mm (Twelve Bens, Connemara highlands); 200+ rain-days per year typical (Met Éireann 1991–2020 normals).
- Hydrological regime is rainfall-fed perennial: rivers carry summer baseflow even in dry years; spring discharge is dependable; rainwater harvesting yields enormous volumes per unit roof area.
- Groundwater is dominated by fractured-bedrock aquifers (Devonian Old Red Sandstone in West Cork; metamorphic basement in Connemara) — borehole yields are typically modest (1–5 L/s) but reliable. Geological Survey Ireland (GSI) provides public aquifer-vulnerability mapping.
- Drinking-water quality risk is locally elevated by peat staining (humic compounds) and microbial contamination from poorly-managed septic systems; the EPA's Drinking Water Quality reports flag west-coast private supplies as the country's main quality concern.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The west of Ireland's water reality is the opposite of Iberia's: water you cannot get rid of fast enough, rather than water you cannot find. The implication for settlement is that surface management, drainage, and dampness-resistant building dominate the design effort. Rainwater harvesting from a 200 m² roof captures roughly 240,000–480,000 L/year — comfortably above any plausible household demand even with generous garden use. The harder problems are (1) treating the captured water (peat colour, microbial), (2) safe wastewater handling on saturated peaty/clayey soils where conventional septic systems often fail percolation tests, and (3) managing surface runoff and seasonal flooding on low-gradient parcels. Constructed wetlands and willow-bed wastewater systems work well in this climate and are widely permitted by Irish EPA guidance.
