# Legal + Economic — West Cork / Connemara, Ireland

**Headline finding:** Ireland places **no nationality restriction** on land ownership — non-EU citizens may buy on the same terms as EU citizens, with no minimum residency, no government approval, and no acreage cap. The friction is not legal but planning: Cork County Council and Galway County Council operate among Ireland's most restrictive rural one-off-house planning regimes, and Gaeltacht planning rules in Connemara add language-commitment criteria. Land prices in 2024 rose sharply nationally; West Cork is materially above Connemara on average.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Median agricultural land prices 2024: **€9,988/acre nationally** (≈€24,680/ha); **Cork region €17,875/acre** (≈€44,200/ha — third-highest in Ireland); **West region (Galway/Mayo/Roscommon) €7,027/acre** (≈€17,360/ha — Ireland's most affordable). Arable land median 2024 nationally: **€17,898/acre** (€44,250/ha) — CSO Ireland *Agricultural Land Prices 2024* release, published 5 November 2025. National YoY change 2023→2024: **+10.3%** (CSO 2024 release). Note: published Connemara-specific medians are not separately broken out; values within the wider West region for marginal mountain/bog parcels often transact well below €5,000/acre.

**Supporting facts:**
- No nationality restriction on real-property ownership in Ireland — non-EU buyers acquire on same terms as Irish/EU buyers (Conveyancing Act 1881 and subsequent; Land and Conveyancing Law Reform Act 2009). No Brexit-related impact on UK buyers (separate Anglo-Irish residency arrangements via Common Travel Area).
- Stamp duty on non-residential land: 7.5% (Finance Act 2017 onwards; first €1m of residential 1%, balance 2%).
- Land transactions registered with Property Registration Authority of Ireland / Tailte Éireann (merged April 2023). Title is registered to ~90% of the country with Registry of Deeds covering the remainder.
- Cork County Council and Galway County Council both operate strict one-off rural-housing planning policy (CDP 2022–2028) — most prohibit "urban-generated" rural dwellings; require demonstrated local need or family connection. *This is the dominant practitioner constraint.*
- Connemara Gaeltacht: planning applications in Gaeltacht areas may require evidence of Irish-language competence and community commitment (Údarás na Gaeltachta input to County Council); set out in Galway CDP and Údarás policy 2023.
- Collective-ownership structures: Cooperative Society (Industrial and Provident Societies Acts 1893–2021), Company Limited by Guarantee (commonly used for community trusts), Charity (Charities Act 2009). Cloughjordan operates as a Co-operative + ecovillage development company structure — replicable model.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Ireland's open ownership regime can mislead non-EU buyers into expecting easy build-out. The binding constraint is rural planning — particularly in West Cork and parts of Connemara where one-off housing is heavily restricted. The practical pattern: purchase an existing dwelling and adjacent land (no planning needed to occupy existing house), then negotiate ancillary buildings via Section 5 declarations or careful CDP-compliant applications. Greenfield community projects almost always require pre-application engagement with the relevant Council planning department. Tailte Éireann title is generally clean but West-coast commonages can be complex.
