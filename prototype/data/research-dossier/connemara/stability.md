# Political Stability — West Cork / Connemara, Ireland

**Headline finding:** Ireland is one of the most institutionally stable democracies in the EU — V-Dem and INFORM Risk place it consistently in the top decile. The November 2024 general election produced a coalition between Fianna Fáil, Fine Gael, and Independents (formed January 2025) — change of arrangement, not regime. Conflict events in the Republic are essentially nil; Northern-Ireland-related risks do not project into West Cork or Connemara.

**Key data point (with vintage):** V-Dem Electoral Democracy Index for Ireland 2023: **0.86** (V-Dem v14, March 2024 release; top decile globally). UCDP conflict events in Connemara / West Cork 2019–2024: **zero** (UCDP GED v24.1). INFORM Risk 2024 for Ireland: **1.7/10** (lowest band; INFORM 2024). Median age in rural West counties 2022 Census: ~42 years.

**Supporting facts:**
- Ireland is an EU member (1973), Eurozone (1999) and parliamentary democracy under the 1937 Constitution. NATO-non-member but politically aligned.
- November 2024 general election: Fianna Fáil 48 seats, Fine Gael 38, Sinn Féin 39 — coalition formed January 2025 with Micheál Martin (FF) as Taoiseach; Simon Harris (FG) deputy; rotational arrangement.
- Civil-society response capacity in West Cork and Connemara is unusually dense: West Cork Development Partnership, Comhar na nOileán (island development cooperative), Údarás na Gaeltachta (Gaeltacht authority covering Connemara), Coillte and parks-service stewardship roles.
- Rural Ireland has near-zero violent-crime baseline; political-extremism polling has risen modestly through 2024 (post-immigration-protests cycle) but has not translated into institutional friction.
- Government continuity outlook: stable 5-year coalition expected through ~2030; next election cycle 2029–2030.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
For a 50–100 year horizon, Ireland's institutional stability is among the best in the world to anchor a community project. The political-risk vector that matters in practice is housing-policy churn at the planning level — Ireland's combination of restrictive rural planning (especially in Cork County Council and Galway County Council areas) and acute national housing shortage produces friction for new-build community projects. Connemara's Gaeltacht status means Údarás na Gaeltachta planning policy and Irish-language commitments are an additional integration layer worth understanding.
