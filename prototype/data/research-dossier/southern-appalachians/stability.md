# Stability — Southern Appalachians (Western North Carolina), United States

**Headline finding:** The Southern Appalachians record zero armed-conflict events in the UCDP Georeferenced Event Dataset (GED) v24.1 within a 200 km radius of Asheville (lng −82.6, lat 35.6). The United States has not experienced state-based armed conflict or organised non-state violence meeting UCDP's threshold (at least one direct death at a specific location) on its domestic territory in the modern dataset period. The region scores at the global floor for organised political violence risk. The relevant stability risks here are non-conflict in nature: political polarisation, rural economic stress, and extreme-weather disruption — all meaningful for a century-horizon settlement but categorically different from the conflict indicators this dimension measures.

**Key data point (with vintage):** UCDP GED v24.1 (Uppsala University, 2024 release, covering events to end-2023) armed-conflict events within ~200 km of Asheville, NC: **0 events**. The United States appears in the UCDP dataset only in relation to overseas military operations; domestic US territory registers no qualifying events in the dataset's full coverage period (1989–2023).

**Supporting facts:**
- UCDP GED v24.1 covers state-based armed conflict, non-state conflict, and one-sided violence with at least one direct fatality at a specified location and date. The dataset covers 1989–2023 globally (Uppsala University / UCDP, 2024).
- The 200 km radius from Asheville (lng −82.6, lat 35.6) encompasses western North Carolina, eastern Tennessee, northwest South Carolina, and southern Virginia — all stable US domestic territory with no qualifying events in any UCDP category.
- The Fund for Peace Fragile States Index (2024) ranks the United States at 0.9/12 for "Security Apparatus" sub-indicator — in the top quartile of the least-fragile states globally, consistent with UCDP null result.
- Non-conflict stability risks practitioners should independently assess: (a) political polarisation and county-level governance quality — Buncombe County (Asheville) voted 70% Democratic in 2024 while surrounding rural counties voted 75–80% Republican, a polarised context for land-use planning and community relations; (b) FEMA-designated flood zones following Helene (2024); (c) rural broadband and healthcare service availability in more remote WNC counties.
- US federal rule of law, property rights protections, and contract enforcement are among the strongest globally (World Bank Rule of Law Index: +1.6/2.5 for the US, 2023 percentile rank ~88th globally).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Political violence risk in the Southern Appalachians is negligible by any global comparator. The stability lens that matters for a century-horizon settlement here is not armed conflict but rather the region's cultural and political texture: the Asheville metro is a progressive, arts-and-wellness hub embedded in deeply conservative Appalachian rural counties. A regenerative community must navigate this bifurcation in practical terms — local political culture, building permit processes administered by county commissioners who may be sceptical of unconventional housing types, and community relations with neighbours who may hold different values about land use. This is not an instability risk in the UCDP sense, but it is a real social-environment factor that competent site selection must account for.
