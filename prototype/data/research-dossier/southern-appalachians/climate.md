# Climate — Southern Appalachians (Western North Carolina), United States

**Headline finding:** The Southern Appalachians sit in a subtropical highland climate (Köppen Cfb/Cfa transition) that is among the most thermally moderate in the eastern United States. Asheville's baseline mean annual temperature of approximately 13.5°C is held down by its elevation (~670 m); the trajectory under moderate emissions is a warming of roughly +2°C by 2041–2060, bringing the projected mid-century mean to ~15.5°C. That figure remains comfortable for a long-horizon settlement, but the region's summer extreme-heat day count is expected to jump sharply — from ~7 days above 31.8°C (89.3°F) per year around 1990 to ~45 days per year by 2050 — a change that practitioners must design for even if mean temperatures seem manageable.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Mean annual temperature projection 2041–2060 under SSP2-4.5: **~15.5°C** at the region centroid (lng −82.6, lat 35.6 — Asheville / Blue Ridge) derived by applying the NCA5 Southeast Chapter 22 mid-century warming envelope of +1.5–2.0°C to the NOAA 1991–2020 normals baseline of ~13.5°C for Asheville; consistent with WorldClim CMIP6 v2.1 downscaled bioclimatic data (2024 release) for this centroid and the CMIP6 ensemble reported in Davey Institute climate projections for the Southeast (2023).

**Supporting facts:**
- NOAA 1991–2020 climate normals baseline for Asheville Regional Airport: mean annual temperature ~13.5°C (56°F); mean annual precipitation ~940 mm (37 in) in the French Broad valley, rising to 1,300+ mm on west-facing slopes (NC Climate Office, 1991–2020 normals).
- NCA5 Chapter 22 (Southeast, 2023) projects the Southeast US will experience +1.5–2.0°C mean warming by mid-century under SSP2-4.5 relative to the 1986–2015 baseline; Appalachian highland zones track at the lower end of the warming range due to elevation buffering.
- Extreme heat days >31.8°C (89.3°F) projected to increase from ~7/yr (~1990 baseline) to ~45/yr by 2050 for Asheville (ClimateCheck, 2025 update, referencing NOAA/LOCA2 downscaled data).
- Annual precipitation projected to increase modestly from ~940 mm to ~1,215 mm by 2050 for the Asheville area — wetter future, not drier, in contrast to Mediterranean competitor regions (ClimateCheck, 2025).
- The region is sheltered from Gulf Coast tropical systems by mountain ridges, but funnelled river valleys can amplify flooding — Tropical Storm Helene (September 2024) caused catastrophic flooding in Buncombe and surrounding counties, an event that underscores extreme-precipitation tail risk.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The Southern Appalachians' headline climate advantage is its cool, moist baseline — rare among eastern North American locations at this latitude. The +2°C trajectory keeps the 2050 mean well below the heat-stress thresholds that cripple outdoor work in lowland Southeast US regions. However, the practitioner risk profile has two sharp edges. First, the extreme-heat day count is a nonlinear story: 45 days per year above 31.8°C means nearly two full calendar months of heat-stress risk for outdoor food production and construction. Passive-cooling design should account for this, not the mean temperature. Second, extreme precipitation events are intensifying: the Helene flooding of September 2024 was a stark reminder that highland watersheds can produce flash flood events of extraordinary speed and magnitude. Siting below ridge-crest elevation with attention to drainage topology is non-negotiable due diligence, not an optional refinement.
