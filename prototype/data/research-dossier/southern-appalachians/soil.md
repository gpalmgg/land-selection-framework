# Soil & Biodiversity — Southern Appalachians (Western North Carolina), United States

**Headline finding:** The Southern Appalachians are a globally recognised biodiversity hotspot — harbouring more tree species than all of northern Europe — sitting atop some of the richest forest soils in temperate North America. SoilGrids 2.0 reports topsoil soil organic carbon in the 35–60 g/kg range across most of the WNC mountain zone, with cove hardwood and mixed mesophytic forests at mid-elevations carrying the highest values. This is a fundamentally different soil story from degraded Mediterranean landscapes: practitioners here are inheriting accumulated organic matter, not rebuilding from near zero.

**Key data point (with vintage):** SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC, 2020 release) topsoil (0–5 cm) soil organic carbon for the Southern Appalachian mountain zone centred on Asheville (lat 35.6°N, lng −82.6°W): **~40–60 g/kg** for forested mid-elevation sites (600–1,200 m), consistent with USDA NRCS SSURGO data for oak-hickory and mixed mesophytic forest soils in Buncombe, Haywood, and Henderson counties, and with published research showing forest floor + mineral soil (0–30 cm) carbon stocks of 72–112 Mg C/ha in high-quality Southern Appalachian forest stands (ORNL / Garten et al., referenced in peer-reviewed literature).

**Supporting facts:**
- The Southern Appalachians are classified by Conservation International as one of the 25 global biodiversity hotspots, harbouring ~200 tree species, >400 vertebrate species, and extraordinary salamander and freshwater mussel diversity (Conservation International / Myers et al., 2000; NPS Blue Ridge Parkway biodiversity surveys).
- Dominant soils in WNC: Ultisols (red-yellow podzolics) on upland ridges; Inceptisols (Umbric and Typic) on steep slopes; highly productive Entisols and Mollisols on floodplain benches and cove bottoms. Cove soils are the highest-value agricultural ground: deep, well-drained, medium-to-high organic matter (USDA NRCS Web Soil Survey, 2024).
- The WNC Vitality Index classifies riverine / cove soils as carrying "medium to high levels of organic matter" and notes they are "some of the most productive in the world" despite covering less than 2% of the region's land area (WNC Vitality Index / NRCS data, 2022).
- High-elevation (>1,400 m) soils shift to frigid, organic-rich profiles with slower decomposition — SOC can exceed 80 g/kg — but these zones are typically under federal or state protection (Pisgah and Nantahala National Forests) and not available for land purchase.
- Forest floor carbon is high: Southern Appalachian cove hardwood plots carry 35–41 Mg C/ha in the forest floor layer alone before mineral soil is counted (Oak Ridge National Laboratory soil carbon synthesis, referenced in SRS/ORNL literature).
- USDA FIA (2022) confirms North Carolina's forest growth-to-removal ratio of 2.2 for hardwoods — forests are net carbon sinks, actively building soil organic matter.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The soil asset here is real and substantial, but it carries a critical siting bifurcation: cove and bottomland soils are exceptional agricultural ground and the correct target for food-production elements of a settlement; ridge and slope soils are thin, acidic Ultisols that drain fast, compact under disturbance, and support forest much better than cultivated fields. Any site-planning process must map cove vs. slope soil units before designing production areas — the difference in agricultural productivity can be an order of magnitude. The region's exceptional biodiversity also comes with regulatory consequence: Section 404 (Clean Water Act) jurisdictional wetland determinations apply to any excavation or fill within ephemeral streams, and WNC has abundant intermittent and ephemeral channels. Pre-purchase wetland delineation is standard professional practice for any site with stream features.
