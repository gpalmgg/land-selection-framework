# Energy — Southern Appalachians (Western North Carolina), United States

**Headline finding:** Western North Carolina receives moderate solar resources for the eastern United States — substantially less than the American Southwest but competitive with the mid-Atlantic coast and comparable to southern France. Global Solar Atlas v2.7 and NREL NSRDB data converge on approximately 1,380–1,430 kWh/kWp/yr specific PV yield for the Asheville centroid (lat 35.6°N). The region's abundant rainfall and rivers also provide meaningful micro-hydro potential — a genuinely differentiated energy asset relative to any Mediterranean competitor region. Grid connectivity is reliable; Duke Energy Carolinas and French Broad Electric serve WNC with standard US grid infrastructure.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas v2.7 (Solargis, 2023) specific PV power output (PVOUT) for Asheville area centroid (lat 35.6°N, lng −82.6°W): **~1,400 kWh/kWp/yr** (annual mean, fixed-tilt optimised system). Consistent with NREL NSRDB-derived estimate: Asheville annual GHI ~1,664 kWh/m² (SolarEnergyLocal / NOAA data, 2024), translating at typical system derate to ~1,380–1,430 kWh/kWp/yr. NREL PVWatts v8 model confirms North Carolina statewide average of ~5.25 peak sun hours/day, ranking #15 among US states (5% above national average).

**Supporting facts:**
- Annual GHI for Asheville: ~1,664 kWh/m²/yr based on NOAA/NSRDB satellite-derived irradiance data (SolarEnergyLocal citing NSRDB, 2024). This is comparable to northern Spain or southern Germany.
- Peak sun hours: Asheville averages 4.9 h/day (fixed tilt), ranging from 2.88 kWh/day in winter (December) to 6.42 kWh/day in summer (June–August). Seasonal swing is notable but not extreme (TurbineGenerator.org / NREL PVWatts data, 2024).
- Cloud and humidity: WNC's humid subtropical highland climate produces more cloud cover than interior Southeast lowlands. Valley-floor sites (Asheville itself, ~670 m) are more cloud-prone than ridge-top or south-facing slope sites; practitioners should anticipate 10–15% lower output versus optimistic PVOUT estimates in deeply shaded cove locations.
- Micro-hydro: the region's steep gradient streams with reliable year-round flow (French Broad system and tributaries) offer meaningful micro-hydro potential, typically 500 W – 5 kW per suitable stream site. This is a genuine energy asset unavailable in Mediterranean dossier regions.
- Grid: Duke Energy Carolinas (eastern WNC) and French Broad Electric Membership Corporation (cooperative, serving rural Buncombe/Madison/Yancey counties) provide standard 120/240 V US residential grid with reliable uptime. North Carolina has a renewable portfolio standard and net metering policy supporting solar self-generation.
- State incentives: NC offers a 35% state income tax credit for renewable energy installations (NC Gen. Stat. §105-129.16A, 2024), stacked with the federal 30% ITC — among the most generous combined incentive structures in the US.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Solar PV is viable and increasingly the default energy strategy for WNC off-grid and grid-tied settlements alike, but the 1,400 kWh/kWp/yr figure is an optimised-tilt average that can drop 15–20% for poorly oriented rooftops or heavily shaded cove sites. Micro-hydro deserves disproportionate attention for sites with even a modest perennial stream with fall gradient: a 50 m head on a 2 L/s flow produces ~800 W continuously — year-round, baseload generation with no battery requirement. The combination of moderate solar plus micro-hydro makes WNC an unusually strong off-grid candidate compared to flatter or drier competitor regions. The NC 35% state tax credit is a material financial lever that reduces solar payback period to 5–8 years for most residential-scale systems.
