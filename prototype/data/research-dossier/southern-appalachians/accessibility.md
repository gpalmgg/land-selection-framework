# Accessibility — Southern Appalachians (Western North Carolina), United States

**Headline finding:** Western North Carolina is a low-to-moderate density rural region, with a regional population density of approximately 47 persons/km² across the mountain counties — roughly one-quarter of the US national average. Buncombe County (Asheville hub) is significantly denser at ~120 persons/km², while surrounding rural counties (Haywood, Madison, Yancey, Mitchell, Avery) run 15–40 persons/km². The region is accessible: Asheville Regional Airport offers commercial flights to major hubs, I-40 connects east to Charlotte (2h) and west to Knoxville (1h), and US-19/23/74 thread the mountain valleys. Remoteness is available 20–40 minutes from the city but never more than 2 hours from a major airport.

**Key data point (with vintage):** JRC GHSL-POP 2020 release / US Census 2020: Regional (18-county WNC) population density: **~47 persons/km²** (WNC Vitality Index, 2022 compilation of Census 2020 data; GHSL R2023A consistent with this estimate). Buncombe County (Asheville metro core): ~120 persons/km² (Census QuickFacts 2020 estimate: 269,452 persons / 1,703 km² county area = ~158 persons/km² including urban core). Rural mountain counties average 15–45 persons/km².

**Supporting facts:**
- Western North Carolina 18-county region total population ~569,494 (Census 2020), covering ~12,100 km² — yielding regional average ~47 persons/km². This is well below the US national average of ~36 persons/mi² (14/km²), but WNC is notably more populated than comparably remote Appalachian areas because Asheville anchors a substantial urban-rural gradient.
- Asheville Regional Airport (AVL) serves ~1.5 million passengers/year; nonstop routes to Charlotte (CLT), Atlanta (ATL), Washington (DCA), Chicago (ORD), New York (JFK/LGA), and seasonal connections to 20+ additional cities (AVL, 2024 route data).
- Road connectivity: I-40 bisects Asheville; US-19/23/74 (the "Corridor of Flags" — four-lane highway to the coast) and US-64/74A serve the valleys. Mountain roads are steep and frost-susceptible in winter; rural parcel access via unpaved forest roads is common and requires high-clearance vehicles October–March.
- Healthcare: Asheville is a regional medical hub; Mission Hospital (HCA, 763 beds) provides Level II trauma care. Surrounding rural counties face healthcare access challenges typical of Appalachian America: thin primary-care coverage, long ambulance response times.
- Broadband: fibre optic available in Asheville metro; rural WNC remains a broadband desert in many townships, though state and federal investment (BEAD programme, 2024–2026) is expanding coverage rapidly. Starlink satellite internet functions well across the region (tested to 100–200 Mbps in clear-sky conditions).
- The USDA Rural-Urban Commuting Area (RUCA) codes classify most WNC counties as "rural" or "micropolitan" — qualifying for a wide range of federal rural development grants and USDA loan programmes.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The accessibility story for WNC is defined by the Asheville gradient: urban amenities and professional services are 20–60 minutes from almost any rural parcel in the region, but rural doesn't mean remote in the isolation sense. This is a genuine advantage over truly remote regions (Alaska, northern New England) and over European competitor regions where skilled trades and building-materials supply chains may be thinner. The practical friction is road quality in winter: ice and snow events occur 5–15 times per year at elevations above 600 m, and unpaved private roads can become impassable. Settlement design should anticipate year-round accessibility requirements and budget accordingly for road grading and winter maintenance. The healthcare deficit in rural WNC is real and should inform community planning — telemedicine infrastructure and rotating-practitioner agreements with Asheville providers are worth building into a settlement's operating model.
