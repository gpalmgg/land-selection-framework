# Stability — Oaxaca highlands, Mexico

**Headline finding:** Oaxaca is not a zero-violence region. It ranks second-highest in Mexico for violence targeting political figures, and between 2022 and 2023 organised criminal violence events tripled in the state as CJNG, Sinaloa Cartel, Gulf Cartel, and Zetas competed for territorial control. UCDP GED v24.1 records Mexico as an active non-state and one-sided conflict zone; Oaxaca state has a documented history of both cartel-related events and land/electoral-dispute killings. That said, the Sierra Norte and highland interior are qualitatively different from the Isthmus corridor and Pacific coast routes where cartel activity concentrates. Practitioners must not assume highland = safe, but the risk profile is meaningfully lower than Guerrero, Sinaloa, or Tamaulipas.

**Key data point (with vintage):** ACLED political violence events targeting political figures in Oaxaca state 2018–2024: **20 events in 2018; declining to 15 in 2021; 5 in January–April 2024** (ACLED, 2024 report on Mexico land/elections violence). ACLED reports Oaxaca held the second-highest rate of targeting of political officials across all Mexican states over the 2018–2024 period. Approximately 270 active land conflicts recorded in Oaxaca state as of 2024. *Note: UCDP GED v24.1 sub-basin georeferenced event count within 200 km of Oaxaca City [−96.7, 17.1] was not retrieved directly in this pipeline — the UCDP dataset must be queried directly. ACLED data is used as the primary proxy. Confidence for the exact UCDP event count: LOW — treat as UNVERIFIED for dashboard purposes. Provide the ACLED figures as the sourced proxy.*

**Supporting facts:**
- UCDP v24.1 covers Mexico through December 2023 and classifies Mexico as having active non-state and one-sided violence (cartel-on-cartel and cartel-on-civilian); Oaxaca is within the documented coverage area.
- Between 2022 and 2023, organised criminal violence events tripled in Oaxaca; overall political violence rose 70% in the state (SIPAZ / ACLED composite reporting, 2023–2024).
- Cartel presence in Oaxaca: CJNG, Sinaloa, Gulf, and Zetas cells all documented; primary competition zones are the Isthmus of Tehuantepec (drug/migrant trafficking corridor) and Pacific coast zone — both peripheral to the Sierra Norte / Valles Centrales highland core.
- The highland municipal governance structure (usos y costumbres — indigenous customary law operating in ~418 of Oaxaca's 570 municipalities) provides a parallel community-security layer that has historically buffered some highland municipalities from cartel penetration, though this is not a guarantee.
- Felix Vicente Cruz, a municipal official protecting indigenous land rights in the Istmo region, was killed in April 2023 — illustrating that land-defence activism carries real risk in some Oaxaca areas.
- Human trafficking (forced labour) is a documented issue in southern Mexico including Oaxaca, particularly affecting indigenous communities; this is relevant context for practitioners hiring local labour.
- SIPAZ (International Service for Peace) maintains an active accompaniment programme in Oaxaca for conflict-affected communities.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The stability picture requires geographic specificity. Violence in Oaxaca is concentrated along two corridors: (1) the Isthmus of Tehuantepec, where cartel control of smuggling routes generates territorial conflict, and (2) land-dispute and electoral-violence nodes in Mixteca and some Valley municipalities with contested local governance. The Sierra Norte, governed substantially through the usos y costumbres system and with a strong track record of community-managed forest enterprises, presents a meaningfully different risk environment. Practitioners should conduct community-level due diligence — talk to SIPAZ, the Tribunal Agrario records, and municipal human rights commissions — before committing to any specific municipality. The aggregate Oaxaca-state figure substantially overstates the highland-interior risk while understating the Isthmus/coast risk.
