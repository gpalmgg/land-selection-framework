# Water — Oaxaca highlands, Mexico

**Headline finding:** The Oaxaca highlands present a deeply split water picture: the state as a whole belongs to the wetter southern Mexico hydrological regime — which Conagua's regional atlas classifies as comparatively water-abundant relative to northern Mexico — yet Oaxaca City itself sits atop an overexploited urban aquifer and its river (the Atoyac) is one of Mexico's most polluted. WRI Aqueduct 4.0 places the broader Oaxacan basin catchments in the low-to-medium water-stress band for 2050 under business-as-usual (SSP3-7.0), but this masks severe local urban stress that a rural settlement at distance from Oaxaca City would not experience in the same way. The practitioner distinction between urban aquifer depletion and rural surface-water availability is critical here.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0, 2050 business-as-usual water-stress score for southern Mexico hydrological basins (Pacífico Sur / Golfo Centro administrative regions): **low-to-medium stress category (score approximately 0.1–0.3 on the 0–1 scale)** based on S&P Global synthesis of Aqueduct projections published 2024. Oaxaca state is identified as having "low exposure to water stress (scores of 1–30 on the 0–100 risk scale)" through 2050 absent major demand shifts. *Note: Aqueduct sub-basin scores for the specific Rio Atoyac-Verde sub-basin were not retrievable without direct dataset query; the state-level synthesis figure is used here. Confidence: medium.*

**Supporting facts:**
- Mexico as a whole is one of only four countries (with India, Egypt, Turkey) projected to account for over half of GDP exposed to high water stress by 2050 (WRI Aqueduct 4.0, 2023); this national risk is concentrated in northern and central states, not the Oaxaca south.
- Southern Mexico holds 67% of the country's renewable water while accounting for only 17% of GDP — Oaxaca benefits structurally from this south-bias (Conagua, Statistics on Water in Mexico, 2017 edition).
- Oaxaca City urban supply: current demand 1,200 litres/second; actual supply ~400 l/s — a 67% deficit — with aquifer overexploitation acknowledged by SOAPA (state water authority) (The Oaxaca Post, 2023).
- Average annual precipitation at Oaxaca City: ~908 mm, bimodal wet season (May–October peak). Sierra Norte cloud-forest zones receive 1,200–2,500 mm/yr; the Pacific-facing slopes receive less.
- The Atoyac-Verde river system is contaminated by untreated municipal and agricultural discharge from valley settlements; surface water from this system requires treatment regardless of rural location.
- Conagua's Hydrological-Administrative Region Pacífico Sur covers Oaxaca's primary watersheds; published annual availability figures show positive renewable water balance at regional scale.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
A rural settlement in the Sierra Norte or in a valley at distance from Oaxaca City faces a fundamentally different water reality than the city's aquifer crisis. Cloud-forest catchments at elevation yield year-round spring flow; the practical constraints are seasonal storage (November–April dry season), waterborne contamination from upstream agriculture, and the absence of formal water rights in most comunal territories (water use is governed internally by ejido/comunal assembly rules, not by Conagua licences — which can be an advantage or a vulnerability depending on community relations). Any settlement must design for the dry-season storage gap from day one; gravity-fed spring systems with 90-day reservoir capacity are the baseline standard in this landscape.
