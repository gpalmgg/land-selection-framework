# Accessibility — Oaxaca highlands, Mexico

**Headline finding:** The Oaxaca highlands are sparsely populated in aggregate but with significant spatial variation: Oaxaca City and the Valles Centrales host moderate urban density (~80 p/km²), while the Sierra Norte and Sierra Sur mountain zones are among the most sparsely populated highland areas in North America (~20–30 p/km²). At the state level, Oaxaca averages ~44 p/km², well within the framework's target range. The population pattern is highly dispersed across 570 small municipalities, which means infrastructure (roads, hospitals, broadband) is correspondingly thin outside Oaxaca City and a few secondary centres. Accessibility is adequate for a settlement that accepts periodic supply runs; it is not a region for people who require urban amenities within 30 minutes.

**Key data point (with vintage):** INEGI Census 2020, population density by sub-region: **Valles Centrales health jurisdiction: 80.8 hab/km²; Sierra Norte jurisdiction: 22.9 hab/km²; Oaxaca state overall: ~44 hab/km²** (INEGI Censo de Población y Vivienda 2020, state total 4,132,148 people over 93,758 km², published 2021). GHSL (JRC Global Human Settlement Layer) population grid values at the centroid [−96.7, 17.1] are consistent with the INEGI figures; urban agglomeration visible in Oaxaca City (~270,000 persons in metropolitan area), with rural dispersal predominant across the highland zone.

**Supporting facts:**
- Oaxaca state total population 2020: 4,132,148 (52.2% women, 47.8% men); 3.3% of Mexico's national population; state area 93,758 km² (INEGI 2020).
- 570 municipalities, more than any other Mexican state — highest municipal fragmentation in the country; many municipalities have fewer than 500 residents.
- Road access: Federal Highway 190 (Pan-American) connects Oaxaca City to Mexico City (~470 km, ~6 hrs). Sierra Norte access via roads to Ixtlán, Guelatao (~60 km), and secondary routes; many communities accessible only by unpaved road requiring 4WD.
- International air access: Oaxaca International Airport (OAX) serves direct domestic routes (Mexico City, Monterrey, Cancún) and limited international connections; journey from Oaxaca City to most highland settlements adds 1–3 hours.
- Broadband: urban Oaxaca City has fibre and 4G; rural Sierra Norte and Sierra Sur have spotty coverage — satellite internet (Starlink) is available and increasingly adopted by rural communities and ecovillages as of 2024.
- Healthcare: Hospital General in Oaxaca City is the regional referral centre (~60–120 mins from most highland sites); community health clinics (IMSS-BIENESTAR / ISSSTE) present in most municipal centres but with limited specialist capacity.
- Spanish and Zapotec/Mixtec language: most highland municipalities are predominantly Zapotec or Mixtec speaking; Spanish fluency varies; outsiders will need Spanish minimum and benefit from a Zapotec/Mixtec learning commitment.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Accessibility in Oaxaca's highlands operates on a different calculus than European comparison regions. Physical distance to Oaxaca City from many Sierra Norte communities is only 80–120 km, but road quality means that translates to 2–4 hours on good days. The practical consequence: a settlement here must be designed for genuine self-sufficiency in daily supplies and routine healthcare, with monthly or bimonthly supply runs to Oaxaca City as the pattern. The flip side: this friction is intentional in many of the existing communities and is regarded as a feature that maintains the cultural and ecological buffer with urban pressures. The satellite internet revolution (Starlink adoption 2023–2025 in rural Oaxaca) has partially resolved the communications accessibility gap without requiring road infrastructure.
