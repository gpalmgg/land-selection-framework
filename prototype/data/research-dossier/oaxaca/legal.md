# Legal + Economic — Oaxaca highlands, Mexico

**Headline finding:** Oaxaca's land regime is the most legally complex in this framework and requires a dedicated section. Over half of the state's territory is held as ejido (federal communal grant) or comunidad agraria (indigenous communal title) — foreigners cannot directly purchase or hold either form. The path to legal occupancy as an outsider involves either (a) converting ejido parcels to dominio pleno (private title) via a community assembly vote and RAN registration, followed by fideicomiso bank trust if within the coastal/border restricted zone, or (b) long-term usufruct agreement with a comunal assembly. The Oaxaca highlands (Sierra Norte, Valles Centrales interior) are NOT within the coastal or border restricted zones and therefore do not require fideicomiso — direct freehold purchase is possible once a parcel is converted to dominio pleno. This is the dimension where competent Mexican agrarian legal counsel is non-negotiable, not optional.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Communal and ejido land coverage in Oaxaca: **1,602 legally constituted ejido and communal land bodies covering the majority of the state's rural territory** (RAN — Registro Agrario Nacional, current registry as cited in practitioner real-estate guidance, 2024). Oaxaca has the highest concentration of indigenous communal territory (bienes comunales) in Mexico. For comparison, approximately 50–70% of Mexico's national rural territory is held under ejido or comunal regime (Ley Agraria, 1992 / RAN statistics). Land prices for private-title rural parcels in highland Oaxaca: approximately USD 3,000–15,000/ha depending on access, improvements, and proximity to Oaxaca City; no systematic published price series equivalent to European Eurostat data was found. *Confidence on price range: LOW. Treat as indicative only.*

**Supporting facts:**

**Tenure types:**
- **Ejido**: Federal communal land grant originating from the post-Revolutionary land reform. Individual members (ejidatarios) hold usufruct rights over specific parcels; the ejido assembly (asamblea) holds collective title registered in the RAN. Land cannot be directly sold to foreigners.
- **Comunidad agraria / Bienes comunales**: Indigenous communal title, typically older than ejido (pre-Colonial origins recognised under Ley Agraria). Generally more resistant to privatisation than ejido — conversion to dominio pleno requires federal approval and is politically sensitive. Oaxaca has more comunidades agrarias than any other Mexican state, reflecting deep indigenous territorial tenure.
- **Dominio pleno (private title)**: An ejido parcel can be converted to private property through a three-step process: (1) the ejidatario requests the General Assembly; (2) a quorum vote approves; (3) the parcel is deregistered from RAN and title issued by a notary, then registered in the local Public Property Registry. Once converted, the parcel is ordinary private property subject to common law — can be sold to foreigners without restriction (outside the coastal/border restricted zone).
- **Comunal land usufruct acta**: An alternative used by some outsiders — the comunal assembly issues a use-right certificate (acta de posesión) recognising usufruct. This is less secure than dominio pleno; it is transferable only with assembly approval and does not constitute ownership under Mexican civil law.

**The foreigners restricted zone:**
- Mexican Constitution Article 27 defines a "zona restringida": all land within 50 km of any coastline and 100 km of any international border.
- The Oaxaca highlands (Sierra Norte, Valles Centrales, Sierra Sur interior) are located well inland of the Pacific coast; Oaxaca City is approximately 230 km from the nearest Pacific coast. **The highlands are outside the restricted zone.** Foreigners can hold freehold dominio-pleno title directly in their name — no fideicomiso required.
- Oaxaca's Pacific coast (Puerto Escondido, Huatulco, Puerto Ángel) IS within the restricted zone; all coastal land requires fideicomiso for foreign ownership.

**RAN (Registro Agrario Nacional):**
- The RAN is the federal registry for all ejido and comunal land transactions, certifications, and conversions. It is the authoritative source for title clarity on communal land; title searches must be conducted here for any parcel with possible ejidal history.
- RAN maintains the PROCEDE/FANAR certification records documenting the parcelisation and individualisation of ejido parcels — a critical step before dominio-pleno conversion.

**Practical purchase path for an outsider:**
1. Identify private-title (dominio pleno) parcels via the local Public Property Registry.
2. For ejido parcels: negotiate with the ejidatario and ejido assembly; commission a lawyer to manage the assembly vote and RAN deregistration; allow 6–18 months for conversion.
3. For comunal land: usufruct acta is the primary instrument; ownership is structurally unavailable — this is the correct regime for a settlement built on relationship with the host community rather than individual property.
4. Incorporate a Mexican SRL (Sociedad de Responsabilidad Limitada) or SA if multiple international founders want joint ownership — foreign shareholders can hold 100% of a Mexican company that owns private land.
5. **Prestanombre (using a Mexican national as a front owner) is explicitly illegal** under the Ley de Inversión Extranjera and carries criminal liability; do not use this structure.

**Indigenous autonomy layer:**
- 418 of Oaxaca's 570 municipalities operate under usos y costumbres (customary indigenous law) rather than standard Mexican party-electoral governance. This affects land-use decisions, community entry protocols, and dispute resolution. Usos-y-costumbres communities have the legal right to control access to their territory and admit or exclude newcomers — this is a right, not just a custom. Any settlement in such a municipality must negotiate entry with the community, not just with an individual land-seller.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The legal dimension in Oaxaca is the dimension where well-meaning outsiders most consistently fail. The failure modes are: (1) buying an ejido parcel that looks private but whose dominio-pleno conversion was never completed, leaving title in a grey zone; (2) entering into a usufruct agreement with an individual ejidatario without the assembly's recorded approval, creating a right that cannot be enforced; (3) assuming that a purchase from a willing seller in a usos-y-costumbres municipality creates rights against the community — it does not. The counterintuitive good news: when entry is negotiated properly with an ejido or comunal assembly, the resulting arrangement is often more durable and community-supported than a purely private purchase, because the community has collective interest in the settlement's success. This is a region where the legal dimension and the relational dimension are the same dimension.
