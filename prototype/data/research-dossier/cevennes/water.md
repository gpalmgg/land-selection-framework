# Water — Cévennes / Languedoc, France

**Headline finding:** The Cévennes sit at the interface between Atlantic-fed Massif Central drainage and Mediterranean-aridity Languedoc — a region of paradoxical water politics where total annual precipitation is high (1,000–2,000+ mm) but seasonal distribution and karst geology make summer-low-flow a structural problem. WRI Aqueduct flags the southern fringe as "medium to high" stress; the granite/schist uplands score lower.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 baseline water stress: **0.2–0.4 (low-medium)** in the granite-schist Cévennes uplands, rising to **0.6–0.8 (high to extremely high)** in the Mediterranean coastal Languedoc and the lower Hérault/Vidourle basins (Aqueduct 4.0 dataset, 2023 release).

**Supporting facts:**
- Annual precipitation 1,000–1,500 mm on the Cévennes ridges and 1,500–2,000+ mm on the Aigoual massif (one of the wettest spots in France); 600–800 mm on the Languedoc plain (Météo-France climatological normals).
- "Épisodes cévenols" can deliver 300–600 mm in 24 hours during autumn — exceptional recharge events that drive the hydrological regime, but with high evaporative loss during summer (Météo-France, climate analyses 2014–2024).
- Karst aquifers on the limestone causses (Méjean, Sauveterre) hold significant storage but with highly variable yield and quality; BRGM monitors aquifer levels through its national piezometric network of 1,860 boreholes.
- Drought-projection direction: BRGM groundwater reports flag declining baseline levels in southern France over the 2017–2024 period; persistent rainfall deficits affect limestone-basement aquifers of the wider Cévennes region (BRGM 2024–2025 monitoring updates).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Cévennes water-politics are notoriously contested — the region is a headwater for the Hérault, Cèze, Tarn, and other major drainages, and downstream agricultural and tourist demand drives abstraction conflicts. For a settlement, the practical reality is: surface water is abundant in winter and spring, then collapses to fraction of capacity for 3–4 months; groundwater varies enormously with bedrock (high-storage karst vs near-zero schist); and Parc national des Cévennes' biosphere reserve regulations constrain any large-scale abstraction or impoundment. The traditional Cevenol response is the *béalière* — gravity-fed terrace irrigation off small springs — and rehabilitating such systems is often more permitted and effective than drilling new boreholes. Roof-rainwater harvesting captures large volumes per square metre but storage sizing must bridge a longer summer than in Atlantic Europe. Practitioners should also pencil in flash-flood resilience for any low-lying structure — historic settlement patterns favour mid-slope siting for a reason.
