# Soil & Biodiversity — Cévennes / Languedoc, France

**Headline finding:** The Cévennes are a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve and Cultural Landscape, with extraordinary biodiversity tied to centuries of agropastoral land management on schist, granite, and limestone substrates. Soil organic carbon is heterogeneous — high on shaded north-facing schist with chestnut groves, modest on cleared causse limestone plateaux. Practitioners inherit a managed landscape, not a wild one.

**Key data point (with vintage):** SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC, 2020 release) reports topsoil (0–5 cm) soil organic carbon for the Cévennes in the **~30–60 g/kg** range under chestnut forest and Mediterranean oak, dropping to ~15–25 g/kg on cleared limestone causses. Stocks (0–30 cm) on forested schist sites typically 80–140 t/ha (ESDAC / European Soil Atlas).

**Supporting facts:**
- The Cévennes National Park was designated a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve in 1985 and the *Causses et Cévennes* agropastoral landscape inscribed as UNESCO World Heritage in 2011; biosphere status renewed for 10 years through 2029 (Parc national des Cévennes / UNESCO MAB).
- Habitat mosaic includes beech, oak, pine, fir forests, Mediterranean scrubland (*garrigue*), high-altitude grasslands, peat bogs, and limestone steppe — extraordinary altitudinal range from ~150 m to 1,699 m (Mont Lozère).
- Geological substrate is tripartite: granite massifs (Mont Lozère, Aigoual), schist mountains (Cévennes proper), and limestone causses (Méjean, Sauveterre); each carries distinct soil/biodiversity profile.
- Traditional agropastoralism — *transhumance* sheep grazing, chestnut groves, terraced *bancels* — remains a defining landscape force; abandonment of these practices is the dominant biodiversity threat per Parc national management assessments.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The Cévennes' UNESCO designation is double-edged for a settlement project: the biosphere/cultural-landscape status protects the framework of small-scale, traditional, low-impact land use that aligns naturally with regenerative settlement values — but it also constrains the *type* of intervention permitted. Building new structures, changing land use, or restoring abandoned terraces all involve Parc national consultation. The good news: the Park is actively pro-traditional-use, and a settlement that recovers chestnut groves, rehabilitates *bancels* (terrace beds), or revives transhumance routes is materially supported, not opposed. Soil rebuilding here is best done within the existing agropastoral framework — silvopastoral grazing, chestnut-grove understory management — rather than via greenfield conversion. The schist parent material acidifies easily, so liming or biochar may be needed for productive horticulture; the limestone causses are alkaline and shallow, suited to extensive grazing more than cropping.
