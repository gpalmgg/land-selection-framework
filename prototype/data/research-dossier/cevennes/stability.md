# Political Stability — Cévennes / Languedoc, France

**Headline finding:** France remains one of the EU's largest and most institutionally entrenched democracies but has experienced extended political turbulence since the dissolution of the Assemblée Nationale in June 2024 — three Prime Ministers in 12 months, no stable governing majority, and recurring fiscal-policy crises. The Cévennes themselves are quiet (zero conflict events) but national-level uncertainty propagates into agricultural policy, local-government budgets, and SAFER operations.

**Key data point (with vintage):** V-Dem Electoral Democracy Index for France 2023: **0.80** (V-Dem v14, March 2024 release); UCDP conflict events in Cévennes (Lozère, Gard, Ardèche, Hérault) 2019–2024: **zero** (UCDP GED v24.1). INFORM Risk 2024 for France: **2.7/10** (low-to-moderate; INFORM 2024). Note V-Dem has flagged moderate democratic-quality decline in France over the 2010s.

**Supporting facts:**
- France is a EU/NATO member operating under the Fifth Republic constitution (1958). President Emmanuel Macron's second term runs to May 2027.
- Macron called surprise legislative elections in June 2024 after Rassemblement National (RN) won the European Parliament vote; outcome was a hung Assemblée: Nouveau Front Populaire ~180 seats, Ensemble (presidential bloc) ~163, RN+allies ~143, LR ~47.
- PM cycle 2024–2025: Gabriel Attal (until July 2024), Michel Barnier (September–December 2024, fell on no-confidence motion over budget), François Bayrou (December 2024 onward), uncertain term.
- Civil-society infrastructure in the Cévennes is unusually thick relative to its population: long history of Protestant Camisard heritage, alternative-community movements from the 1968 generation onward; structured around associations under Loi 1901 framework — Mouvement des Alternatives Solidaires en Cévennes, regional Colibris networks, organic agriculture cooperatives.
- Local government continuity: communes and départements (Lozère, Gard, Ardèche, Hérault) operate independently of national-government instability with stable elected mayors; next mayoral elections March 2026.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
For long-horizon projects, France's institutional stability remains very high — but the practical implication of 2024–2025 budget paralysis is that some agri-policy and infrastructure-investment timelines have slipped. SAFER (the public-interest land authority) and CAP eco-scheme administration continued operating through political turbulence. Communal mayors and the Parc National des Cévennes are the practitioner-relevant institutions; both have shown long-term continuity. The Cévennes' specific cultural inheritance of post-1968 alternative-community settlement (the "néo-ruraux" wave) is a major social asset for newcomer integration.
