# Energy autonomy — Cévennes / Languedoc, France

**Headline finding:** The Cévennes-Languedoc transition offers strong solar resource (Mediterranean influence) plus genuinely usable micro-hydro on the Cevenol stream network. Wind is moderate inland, strong on coastal Languedoc ridges. The constraint set is regulatory — Parc national zoning and UNESCO biosphere status limit large-scale installations — and architectural rather than physical.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas long-term PVOUT for the Cévennes / Languedoc transition zone: **~1,450–1,650 kWh/kWp/yr** (Solargis / World Bank Global Solar Atlas, 2020 release, ~1 km resolution). Coastal Languedoc reaches ~1,700+ kWh/kWp/yr; Mont Lozère uplands run lower (~1,300–1,400) due to cloud cover.

**Supporting facts:**
- Global Wind Atlas (DTU / World Bank, v3.3, 2023) shows mean wind speed at 100 m of **5–7 m/s** in interior Cévennes valleys, rising to 8–10 m/s on Languedoc-coast ridges and the Aigoual summit — a Class 3–5 resource, locally excellent.
- Micro-hydro feasibility is genuinely strong: dense network of perennial Cevenol streams with substantial elevation drop (typically 200–500 m within a few km); historical *moulinages* (silk mills) and small mills demonstrate the resource. ADEME and the Agence de l'eau Rhône-Méditerranée support small-scale hydro within ecological-flow constraints.
- France's *autoconsommation* framework (Décret 2017-676 and updates) supports individual and collective self-consumption; the *communautés énergétiques citoyennes* legal status (2019, 2023 updates) enables shared-storage and inter-household energy schemes.
- Parc national des Cévennes core zone restricts visible-from-distance installations; *zone d'adhésion* (peripheral zone) permits domestic-scale PV and micro-hydro subject to architectural review.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Energy autonomy in the Cévennes is technically straightforward but regulatorily nuanced. A practitioner on a south-facing slope can plausibly run a household entirely on 5–10 kWp PV plus battery plus a small micro-hydro from a permanent spring — and the Mediterranean-influenced solar regime supports this even in moderately cloudy winters. The dominant constraint is *visibility*: PV arrays, wind turbines, and infrastructure must integrate aesthetically into the cultural landscape per Parc rules. Slate-tile-matched PV, ground-mounted screens behind chestnut groves, and discreet small-hydro intakes are the accepted patterns. Practitioners should engage the Parc's *architecte conseil* early; what looks like permission-as-formality is often the determining factor between an approved and rejected project. Wood-fuelled heating is culturally and ecologically defensible if sourced from local coppice (chestnut, oak) under sustainable yield.
