# Climate — Cévennes / Languedoc, France

**Headline finding:** The Cévennes-Languedoc transition zone is projected to warm faster than the French national average — Météo-France's reference trajectory targets +2.7°C nationally by 2050 vs 1900, with southern Mediterranean France running above this. Practitioners site here for elevation-mediated cooling and Atlantic-influenced moisture, not climate stability.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Mean annual temperature projection 2041–2060 under SSP2-4.5 / RCP4.5 for the Cévennes / Mediterranean foothills: **~13.5–15.5°C** depending on elevation (Météo-France DRIAS 2020 ensemble; consistent with WorldClim CMIP6 v2.1 downscaling, 2024). Coastal Languedoc projections run 17–18°C.

**Supporting facts:**
- Météo-France's national trajectory: +1.7–2.7°C by 2050 vs 1900 baseline under intermediate emissions, with southern France warming above this (Météo-France climate-future portal, 2024).
- Cévennes precipitation regime is famously bimodal — Mediterranean "épisodes cévenols" deliver >300 mm in a single autumn event (notable example: 2014), and maximum daily precipitation in autumn has increased ~30% since the mid-20th century (Météo-France climate analyses).
- Mont Lozère, Aigoual, and Méjean plateau provide elevation-mediated climate refugia within the wider south-of-France warming gradient; mean July maxima at 1,500 m remain ~22–24°C even under +2°C global warming.
- DRIAS regional climate ensemble projects summer-precipitation reductions of 15–25% by 2050 with intensified extreme-rainfall events (Météo-France DRIAS, 2024).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The Cévennes climate story is dual: a "Mediterranean drying" component on the lower-elevation southern slopes, and a "moderate-temperate continuation" component on the higher Lozère and Aigoual plateaux. A practitioner at 200 m and at 1,000 m is essentially in two different climate regimes within 20 km — siting elevation matters enormously here. The autumn-storm regime ("cévenol events") is a defining hazard: flash flooding on steep schist slopes, debris-flow risk, and infrastructure damage are realistic 5–10 year recurrence events. Practitioners must design surface-water management for these events as a primary architectural constraint, not an edge case. UNESCO Biosphere status doesn't constrain climate but does constrain land-use response — major drainage works or terrace-rebuilding require Parc national des Cévennes consultation.
