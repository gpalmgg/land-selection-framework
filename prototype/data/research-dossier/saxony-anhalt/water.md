# Water — Saxony-Anhalt, Germany

**Headline finding:** Saxony-Anhalt sits within Germany's *Mitteldeutsches Trockengebiet* — the central German rain-shadow zone — making it one of the driest regions in the country, with WRI Aqueduct scoring much of the central agricultural plain as "medium" baseline water stress and rising under projection. Surface water is dominated by the Elbe and Saale rivers; shallow groundwater is the binding constraint for off-grid settlement.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 baseline water stress for Saxony-Anhalt's central loess plain (Magdeburger Börde area): **0.4–0.6 (medium to high)**, rising into "high" by 2050 under BAU/SSP3-RCP7.0; the Harz foothills score lower (Aqueduct 4.0 dataset, 2023 release).

**Supporting facts:**
- Annual precipitation 480–600 mm in central lowlands — among the lowest in Germany — rising to ~900 mm in the Harz; rainfall concentrated in summer thunderstorms with significant evapotranspiration loss (DWD climatological normals).
- Major rivers: Elbe (transit), Saale, Mulde — provide regional surface-water supply but quality remains affected by historical mining and industrial legacy.
- The Harz mountains form an important regional aquifer recharge zone; lowland groundwater storage in loess and sandy aquifers is shallow and rechargeable but increasingly stressed by consecutive dry summers (2018, 2019, 2020, 2022).
- The German Environment Agency (UBA) documented widespread groundwater-level declines in central German Länder during the 2018–2022 drought cluster, with recovery still incomplete in 2024–2025 (UBA Water Resources reports).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Saxony-Anhalt's water reality is structurally tighter than its German-context reputation suggests. The combination of lowest-in-Germany precipitation, deep summer evaporative demand, and projected climate-drying means that practitioners cannot assume Central-European "water is fine" defaults here. A settlement should plan rainwater catchment sized for 6–9 month dry-summer bridges, prioritise deep-mulch and water-conservation horticulture, and verify groundwater rights and well yield before committing to a parcel. The shallow loess aquifers can support modest household demand (typically 0.5–2 L/s wells) but levels are dropping; longer-term resilience favours diversified supply (well + roof catchment + dedicated greywater recycling). The legal framework is well-developed — German *Wasserrechte*, *Brunnenanzeige*, and groundwater-protection regulations are fully codified — but rural-Saxony-Anhalt regulatory navigation requires fluent local engagement.
