# Soil & Biodiversity — Saxony-Anhalt, Germany

**Headline finding:** Saxony-Anhalt holds Germany's most extensive Chernozem (Schwarzerde / "black earth") soils — among the most fertile and carbon-rich agricultural soils in Europe. The agricultural use of this exceptional resource is intensive, biodiversity in the cropland landscape is depressed, but where Natura 2000 protections and floodplain mosaics persist (Elbe-Mulde-Saale river system) the ecological value remains substantial.

**Key data point (with vintage):** SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC, 2020 release) reports topsoil (0–5 cm) soil organic carbon for the Magdeburger Börde Chernozem zone at **~25–45 g/kg**, with stocks (0–30 cm) typically 80–150 t C/ha — higher than European arable averages; Bad Lauchstädt long-term experimental site Chernozems are documented benchmarks (Thünen Institute, 2018).

**Supporting facts:**
- Saxony-Anhalt has the largest German Chernozem area, developed on loess deposits; Central Germany contains Europe's westernmost widespread Chernozem/Phaeozem area (Pohl et al., *Quaternary Science Reviews*, 2023, PMC10036524).
- Dominant soil types beyond Chernozems: Luvisols and Cambisols on transitional loess; Histosols and Gleysols in the Elbe floodplain; Podzols and shallow Cambisols in the Harz uplands (BGR / ESDAC).
- Natura 2000 network in Saxony-Anhalt covers ~11% of the state (~225,000 ha), with the Elbe floodplain biosphere reserve (UNESCO MAB *Flusslandschaft Elbe*) as the most significant continental-river-floodplain protection in Germany.
- Studies show organic carbon content significantly higher in Natura 2000 cropland and grassland sites than non-protected equivalents (PMC6087710, 2018) — relevant for understanding the potential of agroecological restoration here.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Saxony-Anhalt's soil story is uniquely double-sided for a regenerative practitioner. The headline asset — Chernozem — is one of the most productive soils on the planet, holding deep carbon stocks and providing a baseline fertility unavailable in 9 of the other 9 regions in this study. The headline liability is that 100+ years of intensive arable cultivation has degraded this baseline relative to its native potential, and the surrounding ecological matrix is largely depleted. A practitioner siting on Chernozem inherits exceptional fertility but a depleted biological context — the opposite of, say, Transylvania, where the biodiversity remains but the soils are more modest. Regeneration here means rebuilding biodiversity *on* productive ground: hedgerow restoration, multi-strata agroforestry, return of perennial cover, intentional rewilding of marginal areas. ZEGG (in adjacent Brandenburg) and the Wendland and Werder agro-ecological networks demonstrate working precedents. Land prices remain low by Western European standards, particularly for marginal parcels with restoration potential.
