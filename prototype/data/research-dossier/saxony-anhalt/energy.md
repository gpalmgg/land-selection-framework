# Energy autonomy — Saxony-Anhalt, Germany

**Headline finding:** Saxony-Anhalt is one of Germany's strongest renewable-energy regions, branded as both "wind state" and "Solar Valley" — wind already supplies ~62% of the state's renewable electricity, and the regional grid (50Hertz zone) is among the most renewables-saturated in Europe. PV resource is moderate but improving in economics; biomass and small-hydro remain niche but legally well-supported.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas long-term PVOUT for Saxony-Anhalt: **~1,050–1,200 kWh/kWp/yr** (Solargis / World Bank Global Solar Atlas, 2020 release, ~1 km resolution). Lower than Iberia or southern France but supported by Germany's mature regulatory and grid environment.

**Supporting facts:**
- Wind-generated electricity holds a 62% share of Saxony-Anhalt's renewable energy mix; the state hosts the world's largest concentration of modern onshore wind turbines (Invest in Saxony-Anhalt, 2024–2025).
- Global Wind Atlas (DTU / World Bank, v3.3, 2023) shows mean wind speed at 100 m of ~6.5–8.5 m/s across the central plain, locally higher on the Altmark plateau and Magdeburger Börde — Class 3–4 resource, commercially developable.
- Saxony-Anhalt secured 438 MW in Germany's February 2026 Bundesnetzagentur onshore wind tender (third nationally after Lower Saxony and North Rhine-Westphalia) and 282 MW in large-scale PV — reflecting strong continued auction-driven build-out.
- Germany's *Mieterstrom* and *Energiegemeinschaft* / Energy Sharing legislation (EnWG amendments, 2023–2024) support community-energy and shared-renewable models; *Erneuerbare-Energien-Gesetz* (EEG 2023) provides 20-year feed-in-tariff guarantees for systems under 100 kW.
- Saxony-Anhalt has been historic "Solar Valley" — international investors have committed >€9 billion to PV manufacturing and deployment in the state since 1991.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Saxony-Anhalt is among the most settlement-friendly energy environments in Europe: very mature regulatory framework, strong grid, established renewable-community infrastructure, and economically active rural-renewable build-out. A practitioner here benefits from low electricity prices (renewables-heavy 50Hertz zone is increasingly competitive on wholesale price), simplified PV permitting for small systems, and a culturally normal pattern of private renewable investment. The wind resource is genuinely good — small-wind under 100 kW remains an option in suitable rural-zoning areas, though permitting has tightened in some communes. ZEGG (in adjacent Brandenburg, ~80 km from Magdeburg) and the surrounding ecovillage and intentional-community networks provide established peer infrastructure for energy-autonomy design — what is technically a single household effort elsewhere benefits here from documented case studies and active community sharing of design patterns. Biomass winter heating remains widely used; heat pumps are increasingly competitive on the renewables-saturated grid.
