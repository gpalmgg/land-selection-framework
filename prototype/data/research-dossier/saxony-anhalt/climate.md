# Climate — Saxony-Anhalt, Germany

**Headline finding:** Saxony-Anhalt's lowland continental climate is warming significantly — projected +1.8–2.5°C mean annual temperature by mid-century under SSP2-4.5 — with a pronounced drying trend in summer. The Harz foothills and northeast lowlands offer microclimate variability; the central loess plain is exposed to increasing drought stress and heatwave frequency.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Mean annual temperature projection 2041–2060 under SSP2-4.5 for Saxony-Anhalt: **~10.5–11.5°C**, rising from a 1991–2020 baseline of ~9.5°C (WorldClim CMIP6 v2.1, 2024; DWD regional climate projections, ongoing).

**Supporting facts:**
- Current Saxony-Anhalt climate is continental: mean July temperature ~18–19°C, mean January ~0–1°C; annual precipitation 480–600 mm in the central rain-shadow lowlands ("Mitteldeutsches Trockengebiet"), up to ~900 mm in the Harz mountains (DWD climatological normals).
- CMIP6 drought projections for central Europe (PMC11322161, 2024) show increased meteorological drought severity-duration under SSP2-4.5 and SSP5-8.5, particularly affecting eastern German loess regions.
- Saxony-Anhalt sits within Germany's primary inland-drought zone; 2018, 2019, 2020, and 2022 summers caused major agricultural impacts and groundwater drawdown documented by DWD and Umweltbundesamt (UBA).
- Tipping-point exposure: low for direct AMOC/Atlantic effects; high for European-summer-circulation regime changes that affect Central European drought frequency.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Saxony-Anhalt's climate trajectory is one of the more concerning in Germany — it sits in a continental rain-shadow zone that is already among the drier parts of the country, and projections sharpen that condition. For a settlement, the implication is that what *looks* like a temperate climate (cold winters, warm summers, moderate rain) is actually approaching a marginal-water regime by mid-century. Practitioners siting here should design assuming continued summer drying: drought-tolerant tree species selection, agroforestry-based water-conservation, and rainwater storage sized for multi-month dry spells become foundational rather than optional. The flip side: winters remain real (snow, frost) and require serious thermal envelope work in any building. ZEGG (Belzig) and other established intentional communities in adjacent Brandenburg/Sachsen demonstrate that the practical settlement model is workable — but design must absorb the drying trajectory.
