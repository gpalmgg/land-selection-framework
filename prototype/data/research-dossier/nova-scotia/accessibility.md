# Accessibility — Nova Scotia / Cape Breton, Canada

**Headline finding:** Nova Scotia's population density is 18.4 persons/km² province-wide (Statistics Canada 2021 Census), placing it in the "sparse but not remote" category — lower than most Western European comparators but substantially denser than the Canadian prairie and northern territories. The spatial distribution is highly uneven: the Halifax Regional Municipality accounts for ~47% of the provincial population at much higher densities, while rural mainland Nova Scotia and Cape Breton Island are genuinely low-density (5–15 persons/km² in most rural census divisions). This split means that accessibility to services is bimodal: within ~1 hour of Halifax you have a mid-sized city (population ~480,000) with an international airport, hospital network, and full urban services; in rural Cape Breton or the eastern mainland the nearest full-service hospital may be 60–90 minutes away on roads that ice in winter.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Nova Scotia provincial population density: **18.4 persons/km²** (Statistics Canada 2021 Census; DGUID 2021A000212; land area 52,824.71 km²; total population 969,383). Cape Breton Island overall density: approximately **38.7 persons/km²** (2024 estimate of 93,694 residents over the island's ~10,311 km² including municipal and rural divisions) — but the island's density is concentrated in the Cape Breton Regional Municipality (Sydney area); rural Cape Breton Highlands approach 2–5 persons/km².

**Supporting facts:**
- Halifax Stanfield International Airport: direct flights to Toronto, Montreal, Ottawa, New York, London (UK), Frankfurt, Dublin — international connectivity is materially better than many rural European ecovillage locations.
- Rural mainland Nova Scotia (Annapolis Valley, Pictou, Colchester, Cumberland counties): 10–20 persons/km² in most census subdivisions; small service towns (Truro, Kentville, Wolfville, Amherst) within 20–60 minutes of most rural parcels.
- Cape Breton Highlands and northern Cape Breton: 2–8 persons/km² in rural areas; Inverness County population ~17,000 across 2,770 km² = ~6.1 persons/km² (2021 Census). Sydney (Cape Breton Regional Municipality) is the main services hub for the island, population ~95,000 CMA.
- 30.7% of provincial population (297,879 persons) lives outside Census Metropolitan Areas and Census Agglomerations — genuinely rural (Statistics Canada 2021 Census).
- Road infrastructure: Trans-Canada Highway (Hwy 104) connects mainland Nova Scotia to New Brunswick and the rest of Canada; Causeway to Cape Breton is a single fixed link (Canso Causeway) — functionally a bridge that has occasionally been affected by storms but has no closure record in recent decades.
- Healthcare: QEII Health Sciences Centre in Halifax is the province's tertiary care hub; Cape Breton Regional Hospital in Sydney serves the island. Rural primary care access is a documented challenge — physician shortage in rural Nova Scotia is severe, with many communities relying on nurse practitioners and locum doctors.
- Internet: fibre availability is expanding under federal funding (Canada's Universal Broadband Fund); rural Cape Breton is partially underserved but provincial investment is ongoing. LTE (4G) is the near-universal rural fallback.
- JRC GHSL GHS-POP R2023A raster: consistent with Statistics Canada figures; Nova Scotia cells in the 10–50 persons/km² range outside HRM.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
For a regenerative settlement, Nova Scotia's accessibility profile offers a genuinely useful middle path: low enough density that large, affordable land parcels remain available within reasonable driving distance of services, but not so remote that healthcare, markets, and social infrastructure become survival challenges. The real access vulnerability is healthcare: anyone in a rural community with chronic conditions, mental health needs, or children requiring specialist care will find rural Nova Scotia harder than suburban Halifax. This is a critical planning consideration — cohousing and ecovillage projects anywhere in rural Canada need to plan explicitly for medical access, including evacuation routes and telemedicine infrastructure. Cape Breton's single-causeway connection to the mainland is also worth acknowledging as a resilience factor: under extreme weather or infrastructure failure, the island is effectively isolated for 12–48 hours. This is a known and manageable risk, not a disqualifier, but it shapes the infrastructure-self-sufficiency design calculus differently than mainland sites.
