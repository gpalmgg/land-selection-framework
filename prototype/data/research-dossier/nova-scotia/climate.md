# Climate — Nova Scotia / Cape Breton, Canada

**Headline finding:** Nova Scotia sits in a cool-temperate maritime zone (Köppen Dfb) with a current mean annual temperature of approximately 6.7°C (1991–2020 normal). Under SSP2-4.5, the province is projected to warm by roughly 2.6°C by mid-century — landing at approximately 9.3°C by 2041–2060 — which pushes it toward conditions that much of central Europe already experiences today. The trajectory is meaningful but not extreme: summers will lengthen, heating-degree-days will fall, and growing seasons will extend, but the maritime influence of the Gulf Stream and the North Atlantic will continue to moderate temperature swings and buffer the most extreme heat events projected for continental interiors.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Projected mean annual temperature 2041–2060 (SSP2-4.5 multi-model ensemble, WorldClim CMIP6 v2.1, 2024): **~9.3°C** (baseline 1991–2020 provincial mean ≈6.7°C + 2.6°C mid-century warming, derived from Nova Scotia Government climate assessment consistent with ClimateData.ca regional outputs; Halifax-centred values approximately 0.5°C warmer than province-wide mean).

**Supporting facts:**
- Provincial government projection (high-emissions scenario): +2.6°C by mid-century, +4.5°C by end of century; moderate-emissions (SSP2-4.5) trajectory runs roughly 30–40% lower at century-end but converges near +2.6°C at mid-century (Nova Scotia Climate Change website, 2024).
- Current baseline 1991–2020 mean: ~6.7°C province-wide; Halifax/Dartmouth average ~7.5°C; Cape Breton Highlands colder by 1–2°C due to elevation and northern exposure (Environment and Climate Change Canada normals).
- Annual precipitation ~1,300 mm province-wide; projected increase of ~10% by end of century with more falling as rain rather than snow (Nova Scotia climate assessment, 2023).
- Extreme-heat days (>30°C) remain rare relative to southern Canada; maritime buffering keeps the "feels-like" summer ceiling substantially lower than, for example, Ottawa or Montreal.
- Growing-season length currently 150–175 days in Annapolis Valley; projected +3–4 weeks by 2050 under moderate scenarios, meaningful for perennial food systems.
- Wildfire risk is rising: the 2023 Upper Tantallon wildfire near Halifax and the 2025 season were the province's most destructive in recorded history, linked to drier spring conditions anticipated to persist.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Nova Scotia's maritime climate is its greatest asset and the hardest thing to fully appreciate from European comparisons. The Atlantic Ocean regulates temperature year-round — winters are cold but not Manitoba-cold, summers are genuinely pleasant rather than oppressively hot. The 2041–2060 projection of ~9.3°C mean annual temperature places it in roughly the same thermal envelope as Brittany or coastal Galicia today, which is entirely livable for temperate agroecology. The real climate risks are not heat but rather (1) intensifying nor'easters and ice storms disrupting winter infrastructure, (2) sea-level rise and storm-surge risk for coastal land (not relevant for inland/highland parcels), (3) emerging wildfire risk linked to forestry-altered landscape, and (4) drought becoming a more regular feature of late summer in the drier inland valleys. A practitioner siting a settlement should weight inland, elevated, river-adjacent parcels over coastal low-land parcels — the former get the temperature benefits without the storm surge.
