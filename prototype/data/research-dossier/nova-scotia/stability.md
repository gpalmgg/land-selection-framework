# Stability — Nova Scotia / Cape Breton, Canada

**Headline finding:** Canada and specifically Nova Scotia present one of the most stable geopolitical environments of any candidate region in this framework. UCDP GED v24.1 records zero organised-violence events in Nova Scotia or anywhere within 200 km of its territory in the entire post-1989 period covered by the dataset — the region is structurally outside any organised-conflict geography. Canada ranks 14th globally on the Global Peace Index 2025 (score 1.491), 14th on the EIU Democracy Index 2024, and holds a World Bank Political Stability and Absence of Violence score of +0.60 in 2024 (well above the global mean of −0.07). The principal stability risks at a 50–100 year horizon are institutional rather than military: rising political polarisation, federal–provincial governance friction, and Canada–US trade tensions introduced in 2025 under tariff escalation.

**Key data point (with vintage):** UCDP Georeferenced Event Dataset (GED) v24.1 (2024 release), organised-violence events within 200 km of Nova Scotia: **0 events** (entire coverage period 1989–2023). Canada has no entries in the UCDP GED state-based or non-state conflict datasets.

**Supporting facts:**
- Global Peace Index 2025: Canada ranked 14th globally (score 1.491, Institute for Economics and Peace); classed as "most peaceful country in the Central and North America region" (Vision of Humanity / IEP, June 2025).
- EIU Democracy Index 2024: Canada ranked 14th out of 167 states, scoring as a "Full Democracy" (score ≥8.0); decline from 5th place in 2020 attributed to political polarisation and provincial–federal tensions, not institutional collapse.
- World Bank Political Stability and Absence of Violence index (2024): Canada **+0.60** (percentile ~78th globally; VDEM/WB, 2024 release).
- Freedom House 2025: Canada rated "Free" — political rights 1/7, civil liberties 1/7 (highest freedom scores).
- Canada–US trade tensions (2025 tariffs under Trump administration) represent a new economic-stability headwind; Nova Scotia's trade exposure to the US is significant (lobster, blueberries, forestry products), creating rural economic volatility. This is an economic, not a conflict, risk.
- Atlantic Canada has no history of separatist political movements comparable to Quebec's independence movement; Nova Scotia is one of Canada's most federally integrated provinces.
- No significant domestic terrorism incidents in Nova Scotia in the modern period; the April 2020 Nova Scotia mass shooting (Portapique) was the country's worst modern mass-casualty event and prompted significant RCMP reform, but it was a singular criminal event, not organised political violence.
- Indigenous land rights are an active and unresolved political dimension: Mi'kmaq treaty rights to fisheries and unceded territory remain subject to federal and provincial negotiation; this affects commercial land-use permissions on some parcels but is not a conflict-scale instability risk.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Nova Scotia's stability profile is close to the theoretical ceiling for this framework. The 50–100 year risk horizon that matters for a regenerative settlement does include some legitimate concerns: (1) Canadian federal fiscal sustainability under an aging demographic will stress public services over decades; (2) the province's heavy dependence on US export markets creates periodic economic shocks; (3) Indigenous treaty rights resolution will likely expand over time and could intersect with land-use planning in contested territories — practitioners should undertake a treaty-territory review (Mi'kmaq Rights Initiative and provincial Crown land consultation process) before purchasing, particularly in rural Cape Breton. None of these are instability scenarios in the UCDP sense — they are governance-evolution dynamics that a well-embedded community navigates through relationship rather than physical security.
