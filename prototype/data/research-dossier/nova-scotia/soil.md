# Soil — Nova Scotia / Cape Breton, Canada

**Headline finding:** Nova Scotia's soils are dominated by acidic, podzolised profiles over glacially-worked parent material — typically thin, low-pH, and naturally low in base cations. SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC) topsoil SOC values for the Nova Scotia interior and Annapolis Valley fall in the range of approximately 25–55 g/kg (0–30 cm), with forested podzols holding higher stocks than cultivated valley soils where decades of tillage have oxidised organic matter. The Annapolis Valley is the agricultural exception: Canada Land Inventory Class 2 soils there represent some of the best farmland east of Quebec, carrying higher organic matter than the provincial average, though still at the low end of European fertile-soil benchmarks. The trajectory for cultivated land is negative — 83% of Atlantic Canada cropland experienced moderate-to-large decreases in soil organic carbon through 2011 — but cover-cropping, composting, and reduced-tillage adoption are beginning to reverse localised trends.

**Key data point (with vintage):** SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC, 2020 training dataset, global product) estimated topsoil SOC (0–30 cm) for Nova Scotia: **~25–55 g/kg**, with the lower end typical of cultivated valley soils and the upper end in undisturbed mixed-forest podzols and wetland/peat margins. Annapolis Valley cultivated soils likely in the 20–35 g/kg range based on provincial soil survey data (Soil Landscapes of Canada v3.2, Agriculture and Agri-Food Canada, 2013). *Note: no sub-provincial SoilGrids tile values were directly extractable at the time of writing; range is cross-referenced from Soil Landscapes of Canada SLC survey units and published Atlantic Canada soil-science literature.*

**Supporting facts:**
- Provincial soil classification is dominated by Humo-Ferric Podzols and Ferro-Humic Podzols — strongly leached profiles typical of cool, wet climates over ancient crystalline bedrock.
- Canada Land Inventory: Nova Scotia has no Class 1 soils; Class 2 (highest available) is found only in the Annapolis Valley and very limited areas of Cumberland County. Classes 2–4 cover ~29% of provincial land area.
- Annapolis Valley parent material is largely reworked glacial till and glacio-lacustrine deposits on top of Triassic sandstones and basalts — producing heavier, more nutrient-retentive textures than the dominant thin glacial till elsewhere in the province.
- Soil pH across most of Nova Scotia ranges from 4.5–6.0 — lime amendment is almost always required for cultivation; Annapolis Valley soils are somewhat higher (pH 5.5–6.5 in cultivated fields).
- 83% of Atlantic Canada cropland showed moderate to large SOC decreases between approximately 1990–2011 (Agriculture and Agri-Food Canada monitoring data cited in Saskatchewan OER Soil Science chapter, 2022).
- Wetland and peatland soils in Cape Breton Highlands and eastern Nova Scotia hold very high organic carbon stocks but are ecologically protected and effectively unavailable for cultivation.
- Soil Landscapes of Canada (SLC v3.2) provides the national-scale soil unit mapping at 1:1M resolution; detailed provincial soil surveys exist for the Annapolis Valley (NS22 survey) at 1:50,000 scale.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Nova Scotia's soil reality requires a longer regenerative timeline than warmer-climate comparators. Podzolised profiles are genuinely low in plant-available nutrients and need sustained organic inputs — compost, biochar, cover-cropping, mulching — before productivity matches Annapolis Valley benchmarks. The upside is that the soil capital ceiling is high: cool-moist climates are excellent for building SOC once tillage is reduced and organic inputs are continuous, because decomposition rates are lower than in warmer climates. Practitioners should plan for a 5–10 year soil-building phase before achieving the fertility comparable to productive European regen farms. Cape Breton soils are generally thinner and more acidic than mainland Nova Scotia; the Bras d'Or Lake watershed includes some heavier glaciomarine deposits with better moisture retention. For any land purchase, a detailed soil test (pH, SOC, base cation saturation, phosphorus) is essential before committing to a productive land use plan — baseline conditions vary enormously at the parcel scale. Agriculture and Agri-Food Canada's detailed Annapolis Valley survey report (NS22) is free to download and invaluable for that region.
