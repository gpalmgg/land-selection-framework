# Energy — Nova Scotia / Cape Breton, Canada

**Headline finding:** Nova Scotia's solar resource is moderate by global standards — ranking well below Mediterranean and southern-continental comparators but comparable to northern Germany or southern Scandinavia, which have built substantial solar industries under similar irradiance. Global Solar Atlas v2.7 places the province in the 1,050–1,150 kWh/kWp/yr range for fixed-tilt optimised systems, with the south shore and Cape Breton Island near the lower end and the Annapolis Valley and southern mainland near the upper end. The more compelling energy story for Nova Scotia is wind: the province has excellent Class 4–5 wind resources, particularly on the Atlantic coast and Cape Breton Highlands, and is aggressively building a grid-scale wind + battery + solar system targeting 80% renewable electricity by 2030. A new settlement's grid-tied energy footprint is becoming steadily cleaner; off-grid viability leans heavily on wind + battery rather than solar-dominant configurations.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas v2.7 PV power output (PVOUT) for Nova Scotia: **~1,050–1,150 kWh/kWp/yr** (optimally tilted, free-standing PV system). Provincial solar yield calculator (Natural Resources Canada / CanmetENERGY methodology): Halifax ~1,275 kWh/100kWp = 1,275 kWh/kWp/yr; south shore (Bridgewater) ~1,253 kWh/kWp/yr; north shore (New Glasgow) ~1,188 kWh/kWp/yr; Cape Breton (Baddeck) ~1,190 kWh/kWp/yr (SolarCalculator.ca, NRCan methodology, accessed 2025). *Note: the SolarCalculator values appear calibrated to Canadian horizontal irradiance data and may be slightly higher than the World Bank Global Solar Atlas figures for the same coordinates; both sources used here for triangulation.*

**Supporting facts:**
- Optimal panel tilt angle is 34–39° (latitude-matched) for Nova Scotia latitudes (44–47°N); east- or west-facing adjustments reduce yield by approximately 10–15% relative to south-facing optimum.
- Provincial electricity target: 80% renewable by 2030 (Nova Scotia *Environmental Goals and Climate Change Reduction Act*, 2021); full coal phase-out by 2030.
- Current renewable mix (2023): ~29.4% of 9 TWh total generation; coal still contributing ~47% down from ~63% in 2010 (Canada Energy Regulator, 2024).
- Nova Scotia Power is installing 150 MW of grid-scale battery storage (White Rock, Bridgewater, Waverley — three 50 MW projects online before end of 2026) plus 1,000 MW additional onshore wind and 300 MW solar by 2030.
- Net-metering programme: residential and commercial up to 1 MW; residential net-metering (≤100 kW) credits surplus at the retail rate; commercial net-metering programme approved 2023 (UARB decision).
- Wind resource: Cape Breton Highlands and Atlantic coast sites achieve mean wind speeds of 7–9 m/s at 80 m hub height — Class 4–5, highly viable for small-scale turbines and community wind.
- Off-grid diesel remains common in remote Cape Breton communities; electrification and propane are the realistic alternatives given grid extension costs.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
For a regenerative settlement, Nova Scotia's energy equation looks like this: solar covers summer loads comfortably and contributes meaningfully in spring/autumn, but winter solar is thin (Halifax gets ~1.5 peak sun hours/day in December) and must be backed by either wind, wood-gasification, or grid connection. A grid-tied system makes financial and reliability sense given Nova Scotia Power's approved net-metering program and the rapidly improving grid carbon intensity. Off-grid systems in Nova Scotia should be designed around wind-primary, solar-secondary, battery-buffered architectures — not the solar-dominant paradigm common in southern Europe. Thermal solar for domestic hot water performs well in summer and shoulder seasons and reduces electricity demand year-round. The 2030 coal phase-out is material: if Nova Scotia Power achieves its target, a grid-tied settlement will have one of the cleanest large-province grids in Canada by 2035. The land-use intensity of the province's planned wind build is also relevant to site selection — large wind lease areas are appearing across mainland Nova Scotia, which can affect rural land values and visual landscape quality.
