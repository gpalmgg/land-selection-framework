# Legal + Economic — Nova Scotia / Cape Breton, Canada

**Headline finding:** Nova Scotia's land regime is unusually complex for international buyers in 2025: a federal residential-property ban (extended to January 2027) blocks non-Canadians from purchasing residential property within Census Metropolitan Areas and Census Agglomerations, but rural land — which includes the vast majority of settlement-scale parcels — sits outside these zones and is largely unaffected. A provincial Non-Resident Deed Transfer Tax of 10% (as of April 2025, doubled from 5%) applies to residential properties with ≤3 dwelling units purchased by non-Nova-Scotia residents, but has a key exemption for vacant agricultural or undeveloped land in many contexts. Agricultural land prices remain among the lowest in Canada at ~C$3,900–4,100/acre (2024), and Nova Scotia's Provincial Nominee Program (NSNP) offers credible legal-immigration pathways — though federal PNP quota cuts in 2025 have tightened the pipeline materially.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Agricultural land value in Nova Scotia: **C$3,913/acre** (2023, Statistics Canada farmland price series); FCC 2024 Farmland Values Report recorded a 5.3% appreciation in 2024, implying approximately **C$4,120/acre** in late 2024 (Farm Credit Canada, 2024 report published March 2025). First-half 2025 appreciation rate: +1.0% — decelerating relative to the rest of Canada (RealAgriculture / FCC H1 2025 release). For context: the Atlantic Provinces remain among Canada's most affordable farmland markets, with Saskatchewan averaging ~C$1,600/acre and Ontario ~C$14,000/acre in the same period.

**Supporting facts:**
- **Federal foreign-buyer ban** (*Prohibition on the Purchase of Residential Property by Non-Canadians Act*, effective January 2023, extended to January 1, 2027): applies only within Census Metropolitan Areas (CMAs) and Census Agglomerations (CAs). Rural Nova Scotia and most of Cape Breton Island fall outside designated CMAs/CAs — the federal ban does not apply to the majority of rural settlement parcels.
- **Vacant land exemption (federal, 2024 amendment)**: non-Canadians may purchase vacant land zoned residential or mixed-use after 2024 amendments; vacant agricultural land is effectively unrestricted at federal level.
- **Non-Resident Provincial Deed Transfer Tax (NS)**: 10% on residential properties (≤3 dwelling units) purchased by individuals not residing in Nova Scotia, effective for agreements signed after March 31, 2025 (doubled from 5%); a 6-month residency-establishment exemption applies (prove Nova Scotia residency within 6 months of closing); agricultural land and commercial/multi-unit (4+ units) exempt from this tax.
- **Nova Scotia Nominee Program (NSNP)**: 10 immigration streams including Express Entry-aligned streams (Demand, Experience, Labour Market Priorities), Entrepreneur stream, and Physician stream. In 2025, federal PNP allocations were cut ~50% (from ~6,300 to ~3,150 nominations for NS); program is prioritising healthcare, construction, and people already in-province.
- **Collective ownership**: Nova Scotia law recognises cooperative corporations (*Cooperatives Act*, SNS 1998, c.4), non-profit associations, and land trusts; no provincial barrier to group land ownership structures. Community land trusts (CLTs) are an emerging model.
- **Agricultural land use**: no RAN/REN-equivalent "agricultural reserve" system in Nova Scotia (unlike Quebec's zone agricole protégée); farmland can typically be re-purposed or built on subject to municipal zoning, but municipalities vary — some protect agricultural designation at zoning level.
- **Building permits**: issued by Nova Scotia's 49 municipalities and the provincial Department of Municipal Affairs for unincorporated areas. Rural land in unincorporated NS uses provincial land-use by-laws, which are less restrictive than most municipal zoning codes.
- **Land trust model**: Nova Scotia has active conservation land trusts (e.g., Nature Trust of Nova Scotia, Annapolis Royal & Area Land Trust) that sometimes partner with regen projects on conservation easements and affordability mechanisms.
- **Cape Breton specifics**: no additional foreign-ownership restriction specific to Cape Breton; same federal/provincial rules apply. Cape Breton land prices are generally lower than Annapolis Valley — rural parcels in Inverness and Victoria counties often sell for C$500–2,000/acre for undeveloped land, though this varies enormously by road access and water.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The headline immigration story — "Nova Scotia is actively immigration-friendly" — requires qualification in 2025. It was true through 2024: the NSNP was one of Canada's more welcoming PNPs with generous allocation. The federal 50% PNP quota cut imposed for 2025–2027 under the Liberals' population-reduction platform has materially changed the pipeline; getting a nomination in 2025–2026 requires either already being in Nova Scotia on a work permit or being in a high-demand occupation. For a group of international co-founders hoping to settle together, the most realistic pathway is: one or two members secure a work permit and eventually a provincial nomination, establish residency, and then the group purchases land together — with non-resident members holding equity through a corporation or cooperative until they achieve PR. The 10% provincial deed transfer tax on residential property is avoidable by establishing residency within 6 months or structuring the initial purchase as vacant/agricultural land (not a dwelling). The broader economic story is favourable: C$4,100/acre farmland, low construction costs relative to central Canada, a provincial government actively courting newcomers with skills, and federal rural-broadband investment all create a supportive context. The single biggest legal trap for international groups is the federal-tax treatment of property held by a corporation with non-resident beneficial owners — Canadian non-resident withholding tax rules and the *Underused Housing Tax* (UHT) apply to certain non-resident-owned residential properties and require specialist Canadian tax advice before structuring ownership.
