# Water — Nova Scotia / Cape Breton, Canada

**Headline finding:** Nova Scotia is one of the most water-abundant jurisdictions in the Northern Hemisphere at the landscape scale. Annual precipitation of ~1,300 mm, a dense network of lakes and rivers covering ~4% of the land surface, and an average runoff rate of ~70% mean that WRI Aqueduct 4.0 scores the province firmly in the "low" water-stress category (<0.10) under both baseline and 2050 BAU projections. The practical water challenge in Nova Scotia is not quantity but quality (groundwater nitrate contamination in agricultural zones, acidification in forested catchments) and, increasingly, episodic late-summer drought as climate-driven evapotranspiration rises faster than the modest projected precipitation gains.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 baseline water stress for Nova Scotia: **<0.10 (low)** across virtually all watersheds (Aqueduct 4.0 dataset, 2023 release). Future 2050 BAU projection likewise remains in the "low" band — the province does not appear in any high-stress country or sub-national ranking in the Aqueduct 4.0 current/future country-ranking tables.

**Supporting facts:**
- Total annual precipitation province-wide: approximately 1,300 mm, with higher totals on the Atlantic (windward) coast and lower in the sheltered Annapolis Valley (~1,100 mm); distributed across roughly 200+ rain-days per year (Environment Canada 1991–2020 normals).
- Average runoff ~70% of precipitation; result of thin glacially-derived soils over impermeable Precambrian and Paleozoic bedrock that delivers rapid recharge to surface streams and lakes.
- Approximately 4% of Nova Scotia's land surface is open freshwater — one of the highest ratios in the country.
- Groundwater: 42% of Nova Scotians rely on private wells; dominant aquifer type is fractured bedrock with moderate yields (typically 1–10 L/s). Shallow overburden aquifers exist in valley lowlands (Annapolis, Musquodoboit, Shubenacadie).
- Primary quality risks: agricultural nitrate contamination in Annapolis Valley lowlands, manganese and iron in some overburden aquifers, and historic-era acid deposition affecting lake pH in central Nova Scotia.
- Emerging episodic drought: 2024 drought events triggered mandatory water conservation orders in multiple rural municipalities (Annapolis County, Pictou County) — a new phenomenon for a province historically indifferent to summer water supply.
- Sea-level rise adds saltwater-intrusion risk to coastal shallow aquifers; inland parcels above 10 m elevation are not materially exposed.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
A regenerative settlement in Nova Scotia is unlikely to face the water-quantity constraints that dominate Iberian and Mediterranean site selection. Rainwater harvesting from a 200 m² roof returns approximately 260,000 L/year at provincial average precipitation — adequate for a household with generous garden use. Surface water rights in Nova Scotia are administered under the *Water Resources Protection Act* (2019, consolidated) and its predecessor *Water Act*; taking water from surface sources requires a provincial licence for volumes exceeding 23,000 L/day (roughly a small-farm threshold). Well drilling is regulated but not restricted in non-coastal areas. The more material water-planning question for practitioners is wastewater: Nova Scotia's fractured-bedrock and thin-soil terrain creates real challenges for conventional septic systems; engineered constructed wetlands and composting systems are permittable but require Environmental Service Agreement approvals. The 2024 drought episodes are a forward-looking signal worth watching — if precipitation patterns shift toward concentrated winter events with dry late summers, storage infrastructure becomes a design element rather than an afterthought.
