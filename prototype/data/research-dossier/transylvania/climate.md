# Climate — Transylvania, Romania

**Headline finding:** Transylvania's elevated Carpathian-basin climate is warming substantially — projected +2.5–3.0°C summer warming by 2050 over the 1961–1990 baseline — but remains markedly cooler and wetter than Iberian or Mediterranean comparators. The region's elevation, forest cover, and continental setting provide partial buffering.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Mean annual temperature projection 2041–2060 under SSP2-4.5 for the Transylvanian Plateau: **~10.5–11.5°C**, a rise of ~2.0°C over the 1991–2020 reference. Summer warming dominates, at +2.5–3.0°C by 2021–2050 vs 1961–1990 across most Carpathian areas (Romanian Academy / RegCM3 + ALADIN + PROMES regional climate ensemble, Springer *Climate of the Romanian Carpathians*, 2014; updated CMIP6 projections via World Bank CCKP, 2024).

**Supporting facts:**
- Current annual mean temperature for the Transylvanian Plateau is 8–10°C depending on elevation (300–900 m basin); winters reach −10 to −15°C minima (ANM climatological normals, 1991–2020).
- Annual precipitation 600–800 mm in lowlands, 1,000–1,400 mm in surrounding Carpathian ranges; winter precipitation projected to decline, autumn to increase (RegCM3 ensemble, 2014).
- High-elevation Carpathian valleys are flagged in EEA reports as continental-European climate refugia for cool-temperate biodiversity through 2050.
- Heatwave frequency in Romanian lowlands has roughly tripled since the 1990s; Transylvanian uplands buffered but trending similarly (ANM 2023 climate report).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Transylvania is one of the rare European regions where the practitioner inherits *both* substantial climate-adaptation runway and a still-intact traditional rural economy. The warming trajectory matters — summers will get noticeably hotter, growing seasons extend by 2–4 weeks — but the starting point is cool enough that "hotter and drier" still means viable rain-fed agriculture, working forests, and uncooled architecture. Continental-climate winters require serious heating infrastructure (typically wood-fuelled in the existing rural housing stock). AMOC-shift sensitivity is low: Transylvania sits in a continental regime less dependent on Atlantic circulation than the Iberian or Irish west coasts. The bigger climate risk here is changing forest-pathogen dynamics and bark-beetle outbreaks in spruce monocultures, not heat stress on humans.
