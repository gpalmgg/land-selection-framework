# Energy autonomy — Transylvania, Romania

**Headline finding:** Transylvania's renewable resource profile is moderate-across-the-board: solar PV is acceptable but not exceptional, wind is locally good on Carpathian ridgelines but mixed in basins, and small/micro-hydro is genuinely strong thanks to the dense Carpathian stream network. Biomass (sustainably harvested firewood) remains a culturally established backbone for winter heating.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas long-term PVOUT for the Transylvanian Plateau: **~1,250–1,350 kWh/kWp/yr** (Solargis / World Bank Global Solar Atlas, 2020 release, ~1 km resolution). Lower than southern Romania's lowland sites (~1,400+) due to elevation cloud cover, but competitive with central Europe.

**Supporting facts:**
- Global Wind Atlas (DTU / World Bank, v3.3, 2023) shows mean wind speed at 100 m of ~5–7 m/s across the Transylvanian Plateau, with isolated Carpathian ridge sites reaching 7–9 m/s — a Class 3 resource on average, Class 4 on selected ridges.
- Romania's installed renewable capacity has shifted markedly toward solar and wind since 2020, supported by EU-backed contract-for-difference auctions and a national net-metering scheme (ANRE / Romanian Energy Regulatory Authority, 2024).
- Micro-hydro is broadly feasible: Carpathian stream network includes hundreds of historic mill sites; ANRE permitting for systems under 1 MW has been streamlined since 2022. Annual flow on most Transylvanian tributaries supports run-of-river systems through 8–10 months.
- Romanian *prosumator* legislation (Law 220/2008, amended through 2023) permits net-metering for systems up to 400 kW and energy communities with simplified registration.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
A settlement in Transylvania can plausibly build a working hybrid: 5–10 kWp rooftop PV for shoulder-season and summer, a small wind turbine if the site has ridge exposure, and either a micro-hydro tap on a small perennial stream or a substantial firewood stack for the December–March heating window. Continental-climate winter demand is real — a household here typically uses 5–10 tonnes of firewood/year for heat and hot water in older stock — and electrification of heat is constrained by relatively low average winter PV yield. The biomass story is the underappreciated one: Carpathian forests carry centuries of sustainable-yield harvesting if managed within annual increment limits, but illegal logging and large-scale industrial export are ongoing concerns that erode the long-term resource. A settlement-scale firewood operation tied to local forest stewardship is both technically and culturally aligned with the regional baseline.
