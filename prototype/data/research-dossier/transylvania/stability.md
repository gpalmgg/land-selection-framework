# Political Stability — Transylvania, Romania

**Headline finding:** Romania is a stable EU/NATO member, but its 2024–2025 political cycle has been turbulent: the December 2024 presidential election was annulled by the Constitutional Court over Russian interference allegations, with re-runs in 2025 producing Nicușor Dan. Conflict events are nil; institutional resilience held under stress. Transylvania, with its long-standing Hungarian minority structures and Saxon heritage, remains one of Romania's more politically pluralistic regions.

**Key data point (with vintage):** V-Dem Electoral Democracy Index for Romania 2023: **0.71** (V-Dem v14, March 2024 release) — mid-band for EU members, declining slightly through the past decade. UCDP conflict events in Transylvania 2019–2024: **zero** (UCDP GED v24.1). INFORM Risk 2024 for Romania: **3.0/10** (low-to-moderate risk; INFORM 2024).

**Supporting facts:**
- Romania joined the EU in 2007 and Schengen (air/sea, March 2024; full land Schengen January 2025) and is a NATO frontline member since 2004.
- December 2024 first-round presidential vote was annulled in an unprecedented Constitutional Court decision; the May 2025 re-run was won by independent reformist Nicușor Dan against far-right George Simion. Institutional process held throughout.
- Far-right AUR and SOS parties have grown materially since 2020 (~30% combined in 2024 parliamentary elections); ethnic-Hungarian UDMR remains a coalition fixture and dampens nationalist friction in Transylvania.
- Civil-society response capacity in Transylvania is strong by Romanian standards: Saxon village heritage NGOs (Mihai Eminescu Trust, founded 1987), the Hungarian-minority cultural network in Harghita and Covasna counties, and a thick Cluj-based urban civic scene.
- Government continuity: parliamentary system, PSD–PNL grand-coalition arrangements have been common since 2021; next legislative elections scheduled 2028.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
For long-horizon projects, Romania's stability story is one of resilient institutions tested in 2024–2025 and emerging functional, not a placid baseline. Transylvania benefits from cross-cutting Hungarian, Saxon, and Romanian community structures that buffer national political shocks. The Mihai Eminescu Trust's work in the Saxon villages (Viscri, Mălâncrav and others) is a 30-year case study in foreign-led heritage and land stewardship under Romanian law — worth direct study by any practitioner.
