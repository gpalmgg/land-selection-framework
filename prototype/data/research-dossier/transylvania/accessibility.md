# Accessibility + Demographics — Transylvania, Romania

**Headline finding:** Transylvania is depopulating but unevenly — Cluj-Napoca is one of the EU's fastest-growing secondary cities while many rural villages have lost 30–50% of population since 1990. Service access is workable through the Cluj/Sibiu/Brașov/Târgu Mureș network; the rural backcountry is materially poorer in infrastructure than Western Europe equivalents.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Centru and Nord-Vest NUTS-II regions (which together cover Transylvania) population density 2022 Census: **~70 inhabitants/km²** (INS — Recensământul Populației și Locuințelor 2021/2022 results, published 2023). Romania national rural population trajectory 2011–2021: **−9.6%** (INS Census 2021). Median age in rural Transylvania 2022: ~46 years; in Cluj-Napoca 2022: ~38 years.

**Supporting facts:**
- Tertiary hospitals: Cluj-Napoca (Cluj County Emergency Hospital, Institutul Inimii), Târgu Mureș (SCJU Mureș), Sibiu, Brașov, Oradea. Most Transylvanian settlements are within 60–100 km of one of these.
- Airports: Cluj-Napoca International (CLJ), Sibiu International (SBZ), Târgu Mureș (TGM), Oradea (OMR). Cluj has direct flights to most major European hubs as of 2024.
- Road density: backbone improving — A1 (Bucharest–Sibiu–Nădlac) and A3 motorway gradually extending; many rural roads remain unpaved or in poor condition. National Highway DN1 (Brașov–Sibiu) is the busy spine.
- Internet coverage: Romania has among the highest residential broadband speeds in the EU, including in rural areas where smaller ISPs deploy aggressively. ANCOM 2024 reported >85% of rural households with fixed broadband.
- Electrification: ~100% grid; rural microgeneration regulated under ANRE; net-metering small-scale solar uptake accelerated 2022–2024.
- Demographic trend: emigration outflow (~3–4 million Romanians live abroad) has been the dominant story; net inbound *return migration* increased modestly post-2020; Saxon-village foreign-resident pockets in Sibiu and Brașov counties grew through 2023–2024.
- Schools: rural school consolidation continues. Romanian-language schools dominate; Hungarian-language schools widespread in Harghita and Covasna; German-language Lyceum continues in Sibiu, Sighișoara, and Mediaș.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Transylvania's accessibility map is dominated by a few well-connected city-regions (Cluj, Sibiu, Brașov) and large stretches of thinly-served rural backcountry. The Saxon village belt (Sibiu–Sighișoara–Brașov triangle) combines reasonable proximity to hospitals/airports with the architectural and ecological qualities that draw practitioners. Internet quality is consistently better than apparent rural-development indicators suggest — Romanian fibre/mobile coverage is genuinely strong. Healthcare quality varies sharply between urban tertiary centres (very good in Cluj) and rural family-medicine clinics (often understaffed and underequipped).
