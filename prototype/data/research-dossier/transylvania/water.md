# Water — Transylvania, Romania

**Headline finding:** Transylvania sits at the headwater origin of much of Romania's river system — most major Romanian rivers spring from the Carpathians — which gives it strong local availability of clean surface water and shallow groundwater, despite Romania ranking as relatively water-poor at national scale. WRI Aqueduct flags most of the region as "low to medium" baseline water stress.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 baseline water stress for most of the Transylvanian Plateau: **<0.2 to 0.4 (low to low-medium)**, with the surrounding Carpathian ranges scoring lower still (Aqueduct 4.0 dataset, 2023 release).

**Supporting facts:**
- Romania's national groundwater resources are estimated at 9.6 billion m³/year; Transylvania holds a disproportionate share thanks to Carpathian recharge (ICPDR Romania country profile, 2022).
- Major rivers originating in or flowing through Transylvania: Mureș (761 km in Romania), Olt (615 km), Someș (376 km). Most carry perennial flow and serve as both ecological and water-supply backbone (ANM / ICPDR, 2022).
- Romania per-capita renewable water resource: ~1,700 m³/inhabitant/year — relatively low for Europe — but distribution is uneven; Transylvania is comfortably above the national mean (WISE Freshwater EU profile, 2023).
- Drought-projection direction: summer-precipitation reductions of ~10–15% projected for the Transylvanian Plateau by 2050 under SSP2-4.5; winter snowpack contraction reduces spring-melt runoff (ICPDR Climate Adaptation Strategy, 2018).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
For a settlement, Transylvania offers one of Europe's better combinations: abundant near-surface water, robust spring/shallow-well yields, and a perennial river network that doesn't depend on Mediterranean autumn-storm recharge. Traditional rural water access in the region is dominated by hand-dug wells (often 3–8 m deep) and natural springs, both of which remain reliable in most communes. The binding constraints are *quality* (legacy agricultural and mining contamination in specific micro-watersheds, particularly downstream of former Communist-era industrial sites) rather than quantity, and *infrastructure* (rural piped supply remains patchy outside major communes). A practitioner here can realistically design around a combination of spring/well + roof rainwater + greywater recycling without engineering heroics. Verify legacy contamination via the regional ANM groundwater monitoring network before committing to a parcel.
