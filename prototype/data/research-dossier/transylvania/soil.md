# Soil & Biodiversity — Transylvania, Romania

**Headline finding:** Transylvania is one of Europe's most intact agro-ecological landscapes — a mosaic of mature beech-oak forest, semi-natural hay meadows, and traditional wood-pastures with internationally significant biodiversity. SoilGrids data shows healthy mid-range topsoil organic carbon, and the absence of 20th-century industrial-scale farming has preserved soil structure that's been lost almost everywhere else in the EU.

**Key data point (with vintage):** SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC, 2020 release) reports topsoil (0–5 cm) soil organic carbon for the Transylvanian Plateau in the **~25–45 g/kg** range under cropland and **40–70 g/kg** under intact forest and hay meadow. Stocks (0–30 cm) in protected grasslands measure substantially higher than EU averages (Diaz et al., *Science of the Total Environment*, 2018, PMC6087710).

**Supporting facts:**
- Hârtibaciu-Târnava Mare and surrounding Saxon villages form one of Europe's most species-rich landscapes, with documented plant-species richness of >50 species/m² in traditional hay meadows — values exceeded almost nowhere else on the continent (WWF Transylvania biodiversity assessment, 2010s).
- Seven Natura 2000 sites in Transylvania cover ~270,000 ha; the region has the highest density of *wood pastures* in Europe (EU Environment, May 2025).
- Carpathian forests surrounding Transylvania hold ~60% of Europe's brown bears and ~40% of its wolves — the continent's last large-carnivore stronghold (LIFE Carpathia / EU LIFE Programme, 2018–ongoing).
- Dominant soils: Chernozems and Luvisols in the lowland plateau; Cambisols and Andic soils on volcanic substrates of the Apuseni and Călimani ranges; Histosols and gleysols in valley wetlands (ESDAC Soil Atlas of Europe, 2020).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Transylvania is one of the few European regions where the practitioner-relevant nuance is largely the inverse of elsewhere: the soil and biodiversity baseline is high *because* industrial agriculture never fully took hold. The Communist collectivisation era was followed by post-1989 fragmentation, leaving small holdings, mixed land use, and a still-living traditional management knowledge base. The risk going forward isn't degradation from current use but rapid abandonment of hay-meadow management as the rural population ages — which collapses the *biodiversity*, even though the SOC stays put. A settlement here can plausibly inherit a working ecosystem rather than rebuild one. The corollary: due-diligence shifts from "is this restorable?" toward "can we *continue* the existing management cycle?" — a different practitioner skill set, closer to ecological stewardship than reclamation.
