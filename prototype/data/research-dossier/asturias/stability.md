# Political Stability — Asturias, Spain

**Headline finding:** Asturias sits inside Spain's broader stable democratic envelope (see Galicia stability.md for V-Dem/INFORM context). The Principado de Asturias has had alternating PSOE-led and PP-led regional governments since 1982 — long-run institutional continuity with regular party rotation. The current PSOE-led government under Adrián Barbón (since 2019, re-elected 2023) is administratively stable. Conflict events are nil; the practical risk is policy churn around demographic-decline ("España vaciada") interventions.

**Key data point (with vintage):** V-Dem Electoral Democracy Index for Spain 2023: **0.83** (V-Dem v14, March 2024). UCDP conflict events in Asturias 2019–2024: **zero** (UCDP GED v24.1). INFORM Risk 2024 for Spain: **2.6/10** (low risk). Asturias regional population trajectory 2011–2023: **−6.4%** (INE / SADEI 2023) — among Spain's steepest declines.

**Supporting facts:**
- Asturias is one of Spain's 17 autonomous communities under the 1978 Constitution and the 1981 *Estatuto de Autonomía del Principado de Asturias*. Devolved powers include health, education, agriculture, environment, planning.
- Junta General del Principado de Asturias (regional parliament) — PSOE-led coalition (PSOE + IU/Convocatoria por Asturias) since 2019; Adrián Barbón as Presidente del Principado.
- 2023 regional election results returned PSOE first, PP second; coalition continued.
- Civil-society infrastructure: extensive village association ("vecinos") network; ASTURIES.com, regional environmental coalitions (Coordinadora Ecoloxista d'Asturies), agricultural cooperative network (Central Lechera Asturiana).
- Asturias has a unique post-industrial layer — mining-region transition through the 1990s-2010s created a thick layer of regional-government social spending that continues to shape rural policy.
- Government continuity: Asturian institutional stability across decades is strong; next regional elections 2027.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
For long-horizon projects, Asturias offers a quietly stable backdrop with a distinctive policy concern: the *España vaciada* depopulation question has produced sustained government attention to rural-revitalisation programmes (national *Estrategia Nacional Frente al Reto Demográfico* and regional equivalents). This translates into concrete support — abandoned-village restoration grants, return-to-rural incentives, fiscal advantages in low-density municipalities. The political-cultural texture is also distinctive: Asturias has its own language (Asturian / *bable*), a strong working-class and post-industrial identity, and relatively low immigration historically — newcomer integration requires patience.
