# Energy autonomy — Asturias, Spain

**Headline finding:** Asturias is hydropower- and wind-dominated, with relatively weak solar resource by Iberian standards but a strong Cantabrian-coast wind regime and dense small-hydro potential on Atlantic-flowing rivers. The region is also home to Europe's largest planned green-hydrogen development (HyDeal Asturias), reflecting strong regional commitment to renewable transition.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas long-term PVOUT for Asturias: **~1,150–1,300 kWh/kWp/yr** (Solargis / World Bank Global Solar Atlas, 2020 release, ~1 km resolution). Somewhat lower than Galicia, comparable to southwest UK; substantially below Iberian-interior values.

**Supporting facts:**
- Asturias renewable electricity generation reached 43.5% of regional energy mix in 2024, with hydroelectric power alone supplying 23.6% (Red Eléctrica de España regional reports / Gobierno del Principado de Asturias, 2024).
- Global Wind Atlas (DTU / World Bank, v3.3, 2023) shows mean wind speed at 100 m of **7.5–10 m/s** on Cantabrian-coast ridges and the Cordillera Cantábrica passes — Class 4–5 resource, strongly developable.
- Major pumped-hydro storage in Asturias: Grandas de Salime (247 MW / 3,810 MWh, expanded with €45 million state support, 2025); La Barca project (354 MW / 4,739 MWh, awarded Boralmac II funding). Reflects regional importance of hydro storage at scale.
- HyDeal Asturias — slated to become the world's largest giga-scale renewable hydrogen project, supplying renewable hydrogen for green steel, ammonia, and fertilisers (Invest in Spain, 2022 announcement; under development).
- Spain's *autoconsumo* legislation (Real Decreto 244/2019) and *comunidades energéticas* framework support collective self-consumption — same regulatory environment as Galicia.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
A practitioner in Asturias is energy-environment-similar to Galicia: PV-secondary, wind-primary in exposed locations, micro-hydro genuinely workable. The differentiator is the strength of the regional hydro tradition — many small parcels carry historical mill rights (*aprovechamientos hidráulicos*) that can be modernised under simplified permitting if the original water right is documented. Verify *aprovechamientos* on the parcel deed and via Confederación Hidrográfica del Cantábrico records before assuming feasibility. For new installations, the wind permitting environment is more rural-permissive than densely-populated Galicia — small-wind under 100 kW faces fewer community objections. Wood-fuelled winter heating remains widely used and culturally normal; the same Passivhaus + heat-pump direction as the rest of Atlantic Europe is the canonical low-impact stack.
