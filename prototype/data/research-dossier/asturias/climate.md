# Climate — Asturias, Spain

**Headline finding:** Asturias' wet Atlantic-temperate climate is warming more slowly than the Iberian interior, with AEMET projecting roughly +1.5–2.0°C mean annual temperature by mid-century under intermediate scenarios. The Cantabrian coast remains buffered against extreme heat by Atlantic maritime influence; the precipitation regime shifts toward fewer but more intense rainfall events.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Mean annual temperature projection 2041–2060 under SSP2-4.5 for coastal Asturias: **~14.5–15.5°C**, rising from a 1991–2020 baseline of ~14.0°C (WorldClim CMIP6 v2.1, 2024; AEMET regional downscaling for Atlantic Spain, 2023).

**Supporting facts:**
- Current mean annual temperature ~14°C on the coast and lowlands, ~10–12°C in the Cordillera Cantábrica interior; mean annual precipitation 1,000+ mm coastally, 1,500–2,000+ mm on north-facing Cordillera slopes (AEMET climatological normals 1991–2020).
- Spanish national-scale projections show rainfall declines of 14–20% by 2050 under intermediate scenarios, with the Mediterranean and southeast worst-affected; Asturias' Atlantic regime moderates this trajectory (Land 14(1):85, 2025).
- The Cantabrian mountains hold pockets identified as climate-refugia for cool-temperate species in IPCC AR6 WGII Mediterranean assessments; relict beech forests above 1,000 m are particular conservation interest.
- AMOC-shift sensitivity: high — Asturias' mild winters depend on Gulf Stream-warmed Atlantic air. A substantial weakening could cool the region against the global warming trend.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Asturias is climatologically similar to Galicia next door, with slightly more orographic precipitation and slightly cooler summers on average. The practitioner-relevant story is largely the same: a relatively buffered temperate climate that drifts rather than transforms, with serious winter wet conditions and stormy Atlantic systems being the dominant design constraints rather than heat or drought. Compared to Galicia, Asturias has a more abrupt coast-to-mountain gradient — the Cordillera Cantábrica rises directly from the sea — which gives practitioners a steeper altitudinal lever for siting if they want to escape coastal humidity or rural-coastal land prices. The AMOC tail risk applies here as in Connemara and Galicia — worth scenario-planning, not forecasting. Practitioners at higher altitudes (>700 m) should also factor winter snow and isolation into infrastructure planning.
