# Water — Asturias, Spain

**Headline finding:** Asturias is among the most water-abundant regions in Iberia, with 1,000–2,000+ mm annual precipitation, dense Cantabrian-mountain stream networks, and low WRI Aqueduct baseline stress. Quality issues (legacy mining, intensive livestock) dominate over quantity concerns. Similar profile to Galicia, with somewhat more reliable summer flow due to orographic recharge.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 baseline water stress for Asturias: **<0.2 (low)** across virtually the entire region (Aqueduct 4.0 dataset, 2023 release). Projected 2050 trajectory remains low.

**Supporting facts:**
- Mean annual precipitation ~1,000 mm coastally; 1,500–2,000+ mm on the Cantabrian Cordillera (Picos de Europa) north-facing slopes; precipitation distributed across all seasons with autumn peak (AEMET climatological normals 1991–2020).
- Major rivers: Nalón (largest), Navia, Sella, Narcea — all carry perennial flow; spring discharge is reliable and high-quality where uncontaminated; many small rivers and streams support traditional micro-hydro and water-mill infrastructure.
- Spain's Third Cycle Hydrological Plans for the Cantabrian basin (2022–2027) tighten abstraction controls and emphasise ecological-flow protection (Demarcación Hidrográfica del Cantábrico Occidental).
- Legacy mining contamination (coal, mercury, fluorite, lead) affects specific sub-basins (Aller, Caudal, Nalón mid-reach); the Spanish IGME/CSIC and Confederación Hidrográfica del Cantábrico monitor and progressively remediate these areas.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The water reality in Asturias closely parallels Galicia: abundant supply, modest groundwater storage in fractured-bedrock aquifers, and household-scale resilience dominated by quality rather than quantity. Where Asturias differs is in the *legacy industrial* dimension — large parts of the central Asturian valleys carry 19th–20th century mining and steel heritage, which means due-diligence on water-source contamination is materially more important than in Galicia or the Cévennes. Practitioners siting on a parcel with historical mining within the catchment should commission a full water-quality panel (heavy metals, sulfates, hydrocarbons) before committing. Boundary-area parcels in the eastern Picos de Europa or rural-western Asturias are typically cleaner and more comparable to Galician baseline. Roof rainwater harvesting yields substantial volumes; the standard wastewater pattern is reed-bed or willow-coppice constructed wetlands, well-suited to the climate and widely permitted.
