# Soil & Biodiversity — Asturias, Spain

**Headline finding:** Asturias holds some of the highest soil organic carbon stocks in southern Europe under its Atlantic-temperate forest, scrub, and pasture mosaic. The Picos de Europa massif and Cordillera Cantábrica anchor major Natura 2000 sites and one of the largest national parks in Spain. The dominant biodiversity threat is rural abandonment — the loss of active grazing management — not intensification.

**Key data point (with vintage):** SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC, 2020 release) reports topsoil (0–5 cm) soil organic carbon for Asturias in the **~40–90 g/kg** range under forest and scrub; native Atlantic oak/beech forest soils above udic-regime thresholds carry stocks averaging **~260 t C/ha (0–30 cm)** — matching Galicia (Calvo de Anta et al., *Spanish Journal of Soil Science*, 2015).

**Supporting facts:**
- Dominant soil types: Cambisols, Umbrisols, Leptosols on schist, slate, limestone, and quartzite; Histosols in mountain wetlands; acidic on silicate substrates (pH 4.5–5.5), alkaline on limestone Picos (ESDAC European Soil Atlas).
- Parque Nacional de los Picos de Europa (64,660 ha, straddling Asturias / Cantabria / Castilla y León) is Spain's second-oldest national park; ~33% of Asturias is within Natura 2000 designations (Gobierno del Principado de Asturias, 2024).
- Picos de Europa region has lost ~70% of its sheep, goat, and cattle since 1997, with severe biodiversity consequences from loss of pasture management (EU Natura 2000 Awards 2020; Pro-Biodiversidad initiative).
- Wolf populations have rebounded — Asturias documented 3,256 confirmed livestock predation losses to wolves in 2023 — reflecting both successful conservation and accelerating land-management trade-offs (Gobierno del Principado de Asturias, 2024).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Asturias presents a curious paradox: extremely high baseline ecological value, with low population density and cheap land prices (post-mining and post-pastoral depopulation), but a landscape that *needs* active management to maintain its biodiversity. The practitioner here can plausibly acquire land at low cost, but inherits both the opportunity (intact ecosystems) and the responsibility (active extensive grazing or coppice management to prevent scrub encroachment). The wolf rebound is real and changes the calculus for any livestock operation — predator-protection infrastructure (livestock guardian dogs, electric fencing, night corrals) is now a working necessity in much of the region. Compared to Galicia: Asturias has more pronounced topography, more dramatic biodiversity peaks, more legacy industrial contamination in specific valleys, and a more acute rural-abandonment dynamic. The Pro-Biodiversidad shepherd-as-conservator model is a directly applicable template for regenerative settlement.
