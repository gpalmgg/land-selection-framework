# Water — Cascadia (Western Oregon / Washington), United States

**Headline finding:** Western Oregon and Washington sit in WRI Aqueduct's lowest water-stress bands for present-day conditions — the Willamette, Umpqua, and coastal basins receive abundant winter precipitation and maintain high baseflows through most of the year. The meaningful stress signal is seasonal: the July–September dry season is real, snowpack decline under SSP2-4.5 will reduce summer streamflows over coming decades, and summer agricultural demand is already creating localised stress in irrigation-intensive sub-basins. For a settlement designed around winter rainwater storage and summer-modest demand, the water story is genuinely positive by global comparison.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 baseline water-stress score for the Willamette Basin (HUC-level, covering the region centroid at −123.0, 44.5): **0.1–0.2 (low)** for the western-slope basins draining to the Pacific. Projected 2050 business-as-usual (SSP3-RCP7.0) score: **0.2–0.3 (low to medium)** — well below the "high" threshold of 0.4 (WRI Aqueduct 4.0, 2023 release).

**Supporting facts:**
- Willamette River mean annual discharge at Salem gauging station: ~850 m³/s (USGS National Water Information System, 1871–2024 period of record), making it one of the most water-abundant river basins per km² on the continent.
- USGS Oregon StreamStats (2024) shows the Willamette and its tributaries historically classify as Class 3–5 perennial streams with reliable baseflow through September even in dry years.
- Oregon Water Resources Department (OWRD) reports high groundwater availability throughout the Willamette Valley: alluvial aquifers typically yield 100–800 gallons per minute in bored wells (OWRD Groundwater Report, 2022).
- NCA5 (2023) projects Cascade snowpack decline of 25–40% by 2050 under SSP2-4.5, reducing summer streamflow in snowmelt-fed sub-tributaries — a trajectory concern for irrigated agriculture but modest for a rain-harvest-supplemented small settlement.
- Portland Water Bureau (2024) reports average municipal water use ~130 gallons/person/day and maintains Bull Run watershed reserves sufficient for >90-day drought-buffer at current demand levels.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Water rights in Oregon operate under a prior-appropriation doctrine ("first in time, first in right"), which matters critically for any settlement drawing surface water or large-volume groundwater. Historic water rights on farm properties may be appurtenant to the land — check carefully before purchase, as they are valuable and transferable. Domestic use exemptions exist (up to 15,000 gallons/day for household and non-commercial uses under ORS 537.545) but have been increasingly scrutinised in drought years. For a small intentional community, a well-permitted domestic well plus greywater recycling and rainwater-harvest cisterns is the path of least regulatory friction; surface-water permits for irrigation are slower and more contested. The long-term story is still strongly positive: even under worst-case snowpack loss projections, the maritime precipitation belt — not snowmelt — is the dominant winter recharge mechanism in western Cascadia.
