# Accessibility + Demographics — Cascadia (Western Oregon / Washington), United States

**Headline finding:** The Willamette Valley and western Coast Range present an unusual accessibility profile: genuinely rural land (with the quietness, land cost moderation, and ecological quality that entails) within 30–90 minutes of Portland, Eugene, or Salem — cities with international airports, research universities, and full-service medical infrastructure. Population density at the rural-fringe parcels most likely to host a settlement is 10–40 persons/km² — sparse enough for meaningful land-holding at reasonable cost, dense enough that basic services remain accessible. The demographic trajectory is positive: the region is net-attracting younger, educated migrants from California and across the US, reversing the rural-depopulation pattern seen in European peer regions.

**Key data point (with vintage):** JRC Global Human Settlement Layer (GHSL) R2023 population grid, persons/km² for the western Oregon region centroid area: **~55 persons/km²** averaged across the broader Willamette Valley zone (lat 44.0–45.5, lng −124.0–−122.5). This reflects a mixture of urban cores (Salem ~2,000/km², Eugene ~1,800/km²) and rural hinterland (5–30/km²). Cross-check: US Census Bureau 2020 population density for Marion County (Salem): 89/km²; Benton County (Corvallis): 49/km²; Lane County (Eugene): 33/km² — consistent with GHSL regional average.

**Supporting facts:**
- Portland International Airport (PDX): direct flights to most major US cities and international hubs; ~60–90 min drive from northern Willamette Valley settlements. Eugene Airport (EUG): smaller, with connections to major hubs via Seattle and San Francisco, ~30 min from southern valley communities.
- Major medical centres: Oregon Health & Science University (Portland), PeaceHealth Sacred Heart (Eugene), Salem Health — all within 30–90 min of most Willamette Valley rural parcels. Cascades East and Samaritan Health regional hospitals cover the mid-valley.
- Broadband: Oregon Broadband Office (2024) reports 85% of Oregon households have access to broadband at ≥25 Mbps; fibre rollout under BEAD Programme (federal, 2024–2027) is actively expanding rural coverage. Starlink is widely used in the Coast Range. Rural parcels vary significantly — confirm parcel-level connectivity before committing.
- Oregon's population grew 10.6% 2010–2020 (US Census 2020), driven primarily by net in-migration from California; Lane, Benton, and Linn counties all recorded positive net migration 2015–2022 (Portland State University Population Research Center, 2023).
- Median age in Oregon 2022: 39.7 years (US Census ACS 2022) — younger than EU peer regions (Alentejo median ~50 years) and with a significantly younger in-migration stream.
- School access: Oregon's rural school districts have maintained more capacity than European peer regions — K-12 schools in Philomath, Cottage Grove, Brownsville, Monroe serve small farming communities; homeschooling and cooperative-learning networks are active and legally accessible (Oregon homeschool statutes ORS 339.035).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The accessibility story for Cascadia is the inverse of most rural regen regions: you do not have to choose between deep-rural land and access to the infrastructure a multi-generational community needs. The Willamette Valley's long agricultural corridor means that hospitals, universities, airports, farm-supply stores, and co-operative food networks are all within the daily-life orbit of a rural settlement. The practical constraints are different: land cost (addressed in legal.md) is high; housing construction costs in Oregon have risen sharply (2022–2025 construction cost index up ~30% per NAHB); and the permitting system, while not hostile, is active — building a substantial settlement will require engagement with county planning departments, sometimes environmental review, and careful attention to agricultural land-use restrictions (EFU zoning — see legal.md). The net-in-migration demographic trend means that unlike European rural regions, you are not building into an ageing, shrinking community — you are building into an arriving one.
