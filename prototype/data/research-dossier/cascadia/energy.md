# Energy Autonomy — Cascadia (Western Oregon / Washington), United States

**Headline finding:** Western Oregon sits in one of the weakest solar-PV resource zones in the contiguous United States — prolonged overcast winters produce PVOUT figures well below European Mediterranean peers and even below much of Central Europe. The compensating advantages are substantial: the Pacific Northwest's grid is predominantly hydroelectric (low-carbon, low-cost), wind resources are high on ridgelines and coastal exposures, and Oregon and Washington have aggressive net-metering and community solar policies. A settlement here should plan for a hybrid portfolio — PV for summer autonomy, grid or wind backup for winter — rather than the PV-dominant stack that works in Alentejo.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas v2.7 (Solargis / World Bank, 2022 release) specific PV yield (PVOUT) for the region centroid (−123.0, 44.5): **~1,100–1,200 kWh/kWp/yr** for optimally tilted fixed-mount south-facing systems. This value is consistent with NREL NSRDB (National Solar Radiation Database, 2020–2022 TMY data) which reports Global Horizontal Irradiance of ~1,100 kWh/m²/yr at this location, translating to PVOUT in the 1,050–1,150 kWh/kWp/yr range for standard crystalline-silicon systems.

**Supporting facts:**
- For comparison, Portland, OR (lat 45.5°N) averages ~142 sunny days/yr vs Lisbon's ~290; the 5–6 month overcast season (November–March) generates roughly 30–40% of annual PV output in the Pacific Northwest (NREL PVWatts Calculator, 2024 data).
- NREL Global Wind Atlas equivalent: mean wind speed at 80 m hub height across Cascade foothills and Coast Range is 5.5–8.0 m/s (Class 3–5 resource), and ridgeline sites in the Oregon Coast Range and Cascades can reach 8–10 m/s (Class 6) — materially better than PV as a winter-generation resource (Global Wind Atlas v3.3, 2023).
- Pacific Northwest grid: approximately 60–65% of Oregon's in-state generation is hydroelectric; Oregon's average retail electricity price was 11.1 cents/kWh in 2023 (EIA Electric Power Annual, 2024) — among the lowest in the contiguous US and sharply below European averages.
- Oregon net-metering policy (ORS 757.300 et seq., updated 2023): residential systems up to 25 kW receive retail-rate credit; community solar projects up to 2 MW are eligible under the Community Solar Programme (Oregon PUC, 2024). Washington has similar provisions (RCW 80.60).
- Wood-gasification and biomass options are locally available given the timber resource, but regulatory and air-quality constraints (Oregon DEQ) increasingly limit open-burn systems.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The honest planning scenario for a Willamette Valley or Coast Range settlement is: size PV for summer self-sufficiency (easy — long days, low cloud cover), accept grid supplementation or a small wind turbine for November–March (or a propane/wood backup if off-grid is a hard requirement), and do not try to size PV panels to cover winter demand because the array would be absurdly large and underutilised in summer. The grid here is unusually clean by US standards (Oregon's Renewable Portfolio Standard mandates 50% clean energy by 2040, 100% by 2050 under HB 2021), so grid-tied operation does not compromise a regenerative mission the way it would in a coal-dominated region. For truly off-grid communities, a PV + battery + wind turbine (small Bergey or similar) + woodgas or propane backup is the realistic stack; expect higher capital cost per kWh than southern European peers but far lower maintenance burden on the thermal side.
