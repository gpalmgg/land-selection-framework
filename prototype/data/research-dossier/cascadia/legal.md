# Legal + Economic — Cascadia (Western Oregon / Washington), United States

**Headline finding:** The United States imposes no nationality restrictions on agricultural land ownership — any foreign national or entity can purchase rural land in Oregon or Washington. The legal complexity is not in acquisition but in land-use: Oregon's unique statewide land-use planning system (Senate Bill 100, 1973) designates most rural land as Exclusive Farm Use (EFU), which severely restricts non-agricultural structures and alternative community configurations. Land prices in western Oregon are among the highest for agricultural land in the Pacific Northwest — driven by the regen-culture demand premium, the climate-haven migration trend, and California capital — and are rising. The dominant legal challenge for an intentional community is not ownership but permitted use.

**Key data point (with vintage):** USDA NASS Land Values Summary (August 2024): Oregon farm real estate (all farm land including buildings) average value: **$2,380/acre ($5,880/ha)**. Willamette Valley irrigated cropland specifically: **$8,000–$16,000/acre ($19,760–$39,530/ha)** for prime Willamette silt loam (Oregon NASS, 2024; confirmed by Farmer Mac Oregon 2024 Cropland Values report). Unimproved timber/foothills land (Coast Range, Cascade foothills): **$1,500–$4,000/acre ($3,700–$9,880/ha)**. Comparable parcels actively used by regen/intentional communities typically trade at the upper band of the unimproved range or above, reflecting the culture premium.

**Supporting facts:**
- No foreign-ownership restriction: US federal law (AFIDA — Agricultural Foreign Investment Disclosure Act, 7 USC 3501) requires disclosure of foreign agricultural land acquisitions but does not restrict them. Oregon has no additional state-level foreign-land-ownership restriction as of 2025.
- Oregon SB 100 (1973) created the statewide land-use planning framework. Most rural land outside Urban Growth Boundaries (UGBs) is zoned Exclusive Farm Use (EFU) or Forest Conservation (F-1/F-2). EFU zoning limits non-agricultural residential development to one farm dwelling; additional dwellings for farm workers may be allowed; intentional community configurations often require a Conditional Use Permit (CUP) or a zone exception before county planning commissions.
- Oregon Department of Land Conservation and Development (DLCD) goal 3 (Agricultural Lands) and goal 4 (Forest Lands) are the binding constraints. Non-agricultural primary uses on EFU land — such as an education centre, event venue, or additional dwellings — require explicit county land-use approval and can be appealed by neighbours or state agencies (DLCD, ORS chapter 215).
- Intentional communities with existing legal status (some 30+ years old) generally hold their status through grandfathered Conditional Use Permits. New projects face a more scrutinised approval environment in most western Oregon counties (personal communications in FIC Oregon network, 2023–2024).
- Washington state has similar agricultural land-use zones (WAC 365-196) but county-level implementation is more variable than Oregon's uniform statewide system — some Washington counties (Lewis, Thurston, Pacific) have been more permissive toward alternative rural housing than Oregon.
- Property tax: EFU-designated land in Oregon qualifies for special assessment at agricultural use value (typically 70–90% lower than market value under ORS 308A.056) — a significant carrying-cost benefit for a community holding land in farm use.
- Collective-ownership structures available: LLC (most common for rural land), Oregon Land Trust, 501(c)(3) nonprofit, community land trust (CLT), or limited-equity cooperative — all recognised under Oregon law. A CLT model decouples land value from individual equity and aligns most closely with long-horizon community stability.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The single most important legal reality for a new intentional community in Oregon is the EFU zoning system. A community planning to have 6 families and a shared educational or food-production facility on a 40-acre parcel in the Willamette Valley foothills will almost certainly need a Conditional Use Permit, possible zone exception, and multi-year county planning commission engagement. This is navigable — it has been done many times — but it requires Oregon land-use planning expertise (hire a land-use attorney familiar with ORS 215 before purchasing, not after). The cost-per-hectare story is equally important: even at the low end of the Cascade-foothill range ($3,700/ha), western Oregon land costs 3–5× the Alentejo baseline ($8,000–$20,000/ha equivalent for comparable quality), and the Willamette Valley prime ground ($20,000–$40,000/ha) is in a completely different cost tier. The community land trust model is not just ethically attractive here; it is practically necessary for most groups that lack substantial capital reserves, as it allows land to be decoupled from market appreciation and held in permanent agricultural and community use.
