# Climate — Cascadia (Western Oregon / Washington), United States

**Headline finding:** The Willamette Valley and broader western Cascadia coast sits in a temperate-maritime climate (Köppen Csb/Cfb) that will warm measurably by mid-century but remains one of the most liveable corners of North America for a long-horizon settlement: mild winters, no extreme heat under current baselines, and the highest projected warming still leaves it far below the summer extremes of most competitor regions. The meaningful trajectory risk is drought-intensification in the long dry season (July–September) and a gradually rising wildfire smoke-exposure period.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Mean annual temperature projection 2041–2060 under SSP2-4.5: **~12.5°C** at the region centroid (lng −123.0, lat 44.5 — central Willamette Valley) derived from WorldClim CMIP6 v2.1 downscaled bioclimatic variables (multi-model ensemble, 2024 release). Consistent with NCA5 (US Fifth National Climate Assessment, 2023) projections of +1.5–2.0°C warming for the Pacific Northwest by mid-century relative to 1986–2015 baselines.

**Supporting facts:**
- Current 1970–2000 WorldClim baseline mean annual temperature for this centroid: ~11.0°C; the 2041–2060 SSP2-4.5 projection represents approximately +1.5°C of warming above the historical mean.
- NCA5 (2023) projects the Pacific Northwest will experience the fewest very-hot days (>35°C) of any contiguous US region through 2060 under moderate emissions — western Cascadia's marine airflow buffers inland heat (NCA5 Chapter 24, Pacific Northwest).
- Mean annual precipitation at this centroid: ~1,050–1,200 mm/yr (PRISM 1991–2020), strongly winter-dominant; July and August average <15 mm each, creating a de facto 2-month dry season that lengthens under SSP2-4.5.
- The 2021 Pacific Northwest heat dome event (Portland hit 46.7°C) is the documented upper bound of extreme-event risk — attribution studies indicate such events become 2–3× more likely by 2060 under SSP2-4.5 (Philip et al., World Weather Attribution, 2021).
- Wildfire smoke intrusions from eastern Oregon/Washington and northern California are already a late-summer air-quality issue (AQI >150 days increasing; EPA AirNow historical data, 2015–2024).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Cascadia's climate trajectory is more favourable than almost any other assessed region at this latitude, but two practitioner-relevant risks deserve serious weight. First, the decadal wildfire smoke trend: western-slope communities now routinely lose 10–20 late-summer days per year to unhealthy air quality, and this trend is projected to worsen. Outdoor-labour-intensive settlement models (food production, construction) must incorporate adaptation plans — sealed air spaces, HEPA filtration, seasonal work scheduling. Second, the heat-dome tail risk: passive-cooling architecture that performs in 2025 may fail in a 2050 heat dome. Building for the 46°C scenario rather than the 28°C average is not paranoia; it is learning from 2021. The fundamental climate story, however, remains one of relative resilience: mild, wet winters; moderate summers; no drought stress in the low-cost-of-water sense that burdens Alentejo or Mediterranean Iberia.
