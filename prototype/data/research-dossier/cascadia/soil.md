# Soil & Biodiversity — Cascadia (Western Oregon / Washington), United States

**Headline finding:** The Willamette Valley holds some of the highest-fertility agricultural soils in the Pacific Northwest — deep Mollisols and Alfisols formed on Missoula Flood sediments, with topsoil organic carbon stocks that are genuinely high by temperate-maritime standards. The catch is that decades of intensive row-crop agriculture, particularly grass-seed and wheat monoculture, have degraded these SOC reserves on cultivated parcels. A settlement purchasing ex-agricultural Willamette Valley ground is inheriting degraded but highly recoverable soil capital — the biological potential is far above what the current numbers suggest.

**Key data point (with vintage):** SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC, 2020 release) reports topsoil (0–5 cm) soil organic carbon for the Willamette Valley centroid (−123.0, 44.5) in the range **~40–60 g/kg**, with higher values (70–90 g/kg) on undisturbed or pasture-maintained slopes and lower values (20–35 g/kg) on intensively-tilled cultivated bottomlands. Cross-check: USDA NRCS SSURGO (2023) reports Willamette silt loam (the benchmark series) at 26–46 g/kg organic matter in the A-horizon, consistent with SoilGrids estimates.

**Supporting facts:**
- Dominant soil orders in the Willamette Valley: Mollisols (Willamette series — the Oregon state soil), Alfisols, and Inceptisols on hillslopes; benchmark soil Willamette silt loam is rated "prime farmland" by USDA and supports the highest-value row-crop production in the Pacific Northwest (USDA NRCS Web Soil Survey, 2023).
- Forest Service and ODA data show Cascade-foothills soils (forest-to-farm transition zones where intentional communities often locate) carry SOC 60–120 g/kg under undisturbed or lightly grazed conditions, declining to 20–45 g/kg under 40+ years of conventional tillage (ODA Soil Health Assessment Programme, 2022).
- Oregon Department of Agriculture (ODA) Soil Health Initiative (2022–2025) identifies the Willamette Valley as a priority region for no-till transition, cover-cropping and compost application — measurable SOC gains of 1–3 g/kg/yr documented in demonstration sites.
- Biodiversity context: western Cascadia is a globally significant biodiversity hotspot — the Pacific temperate rainforest is one of the largest remaining temperate rainforest zones on Earth, with intact salmon-river corridors, old-growth conifer understories, and high endemism in invertebrates and fungi (Conservation Biology, 2021).
- Volcanic parent material (Cascade tephras) provides trace mineral richness and rapid soil development potential; Willamette loess on flood terraces has favourable cation exchange capacity (20–30 cmolc/kg) for high productivity.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Soil purchase decisions in western Oregon bifurcate sharply by land-use history: valley bottomlands with 40+ years of grass-seed cultivation may have SOC 20–30 g/kg and compacted subsoil pans that require multi-year remediation; hillslope and former-timber parcels can carry 60–100 g/kg SOC under minimal tillage history. A practitioner should request a USDA NRCS custom soil survey and field-verify compaction depth before committing to any high-value bottomland parcel. The upside is real: high rainfall, long growing season, fertile parent material, and strong local composting and cover-crop culture mean SOC rebuilds faster here than in any Mediterranean peer region — 5–10-year timelines to substantially improved soil biology are achievable with appropriate management.
