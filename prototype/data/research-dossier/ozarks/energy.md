# Energy Autonomy — Ozarks (Arkansas / southern Missouri), United States

**Headline finding:** The Ozarks deliver a solid mid-tier solar resource for the continental US — meaningfully better than the US Northeast or Pacific Northwest, and sufficient for a self-reliant community PV system, though well below the US Southwest and considerably below southern Europe. Annual global horizontal irradiance (GHI) of ~1,920 kWh/m² translates to PVOUT in the range of **1,400–1,500 kWh/kWp/yr**, competitive with central France or northern Spain. Wind is modest (Class 2–3 at most sites). Micro-hydro is site-dependent but genuinely possible on year-round flowing Ozark streams and springs. Grid interconnection is available but thin in the most rural counties; off-grid PV plus battery is already the predominant model for new homestead builds across the region.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Horizontal Irradiance for Ozark, AR: **~1,920 kWh/m²/yr** (5.27 kWh/m²/day annual average), per NREL National Solar Radiation Database (NSRDB), accessed via Solar Energy Local / NREL data (2024). Estimated PVOUT (PV system yield, fixed-tilt optimised, standard losses): **~1,400–1,480 kWh/kWp/yr**, consistent with NREL PVWatts estimates for the 36°N latitude band in this GHI range (NREL NSRDB / PVWatts v6, 2024). An 8 kW system in central Arkansas yields approximately 11,200 kWh/yr (~1,400 kWh/kWp).

**Supporting facts:**
- Peak months: July–August at 5.98–6.14 kWh/m²/day GHI; lowest production in December at 4.13 kWh/m²/day — a seasonal variation of ~1.5:1, manageable with modest battery storage (NSRDB / Solar Energy Local, 2024).
- Arkansas averages 4.5–5.1 peak sun hours/day across the state; the Ozarks sit in the 4.7–5.3 range, slightly lower than the southwestern corner of the state (NREL data per multiple sources, 2024).
- Wind: modest resource at most Ozark ridgeline sites. The Global Wind Atlas shows mean wind speeds at 100 m of approximately 5–6 m/s on exposed ridges — marginal for utility-scale wind but potentially viable for small turbines (1–10 kW) at well-exposed sites. Not a primary energy source for most parcels.
- Micro-hydro: the Ozarks' karst system supports perennial springs and stream baseflows year-round, unlike Mediterranean climate analogues. Many homesteads already use small Pelton or turbine wheels on spring-fed runs; feasibility is highly site-specific but genuinely common in the region.
- Arkansas net-metering policy (Act 827 of 2019): utilities must offer net metering for systems up to 1,000 kW; excess credits roll over monthly and are settled annually. Community solar is available but limited in rural co-op territory.
- Rural electric co-ops (e.g., Carroll Electric, North Arkansas Electric) serve most of the Ozark backcountry; grid reliability is lower than urban areas; outages from ice storms are a meaningful winter consideration. Off-grid is economically competitive for parcels >1 mile from an existing line.
- Arkansas has no state-level solar tax credit (as of 2025), but federal 30% ITC applies; property tax exemption for solar installations varies by county.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The Ozarks' energy profile is well-matched to off-grid or hybrid settlement design. The GHI supports a ~5 kWp rooftop system generating 7,000–7,500 kWh/yr — sufficient for a highly efficient household. The practical challenge relative to the European candidates is winter: ice storms can interrupt solar generation for 1–3 weeks, and propane backup heating remains near-universal in the region. A resilient stack is: PV (7–15 kWp) + lithium-iron-phosphate storage (20–40 kWh) + propane or wood-gasifier winter backup + spring-run micro-hydro where feasible. The cost of this stack has fallen sharply; 2024 installed PV costs in rural Arkansas run $2.50–3.50/W for typical residential systems, making energy autonomy financially accessible alongside the region's low land costs.
