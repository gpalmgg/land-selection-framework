# Climate — Ozarks (Arkansas / southern Missouri), United States

**Headline finding:** The Ozarks currently sit in a humid continental–humid subtropical transition zone, with a mild four-season climate considerably more temperate than the deep South. Under SSP2-4.5 mid-century projections, mean annual temperature is expected to rise by approximately 2–2.5°C above the 1991–2020 baseline, pushing the region firmly into humid subtropical territory. The primary concern is not extreme heat on the level of the Gulf Coast, but a lengthening of the summer heat season, increasing frequency of days above 35°C, and intensifying rain events punctuating longer dry intervals — a shift that challenges traditional farming cycles while favouring drought-tolerant polyculture.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Current mean annual temperature across the central Ozark Plateau (elevation ~300–600 m): **~14–16°C** (57–61°F) based on 1991–2020 NOAA Climate Normals for representative stations (Harrison, Mountain Home, Fayetteville, AR). Projected increase under SSP2-4.5 by 2041–2060: **+2.0–2.5°C** above 1991–2020 baseline, yielding a projected mid-century mean of approximately **16–18°C**, consistent with NCA5 Southeast Chapter 22 regional envelope and the KSMU/Missouri State University synthesis of NOAA projections for the Ozarks region (2025).

**Supporting facts:**
- Current annual precipitation: ~1,280 mm (50 in) averaged across northern Arkansas; the 1991–2020 NOAA normal for Franklin County NWS station is 50.5 in/yr, with rain distributed year-round but with a secondary dry period in late summer (NWS Tulsa NOAA climatology, 2022).
- Current climate at Ozark, AR (elev. 150 m): mean annual 15.3°C (59.5°F); 55 days/yr above 32°C (90°F); 74 frost days/yr; snowfall ~5 in/yr — transitional between Cfa (humid subtropical) and Cfb (temperate oceanic) on upland terrain (NWS Tulsa climatology, 1981–2010).
- NOAA Arkansas State Climate Summary (2022) reports that statewide mean temperature has risen ~0.3°C (0.5°F) since the early 1900s; recent decades (post-1970) show acceleration consistent with global trends.
- NCA5 projects the Southeast (which includes Arkansas) will experience increasing extreme-heat days, with days above 38°C (100°F) roughly doubling under intermediate scenarios by mid-century (NCA5 Ch. 22, 2023).
- NOAA projects winter and spring precipitation to increase while summer precipitation may decrease across the Ozarks, resulting in higher-intensity but shorter rain events and longer dry spells (KSMU/Missouri State Univ., 2025).
- Elevation provides meaningful thermal buffering: ridgeline and plateau sites at 500–700 m run 3–5°C cooler than valley floors in summer, extending the comfortable working season and reducing peak cooling demand.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The Ozarks' climate trajectory is more moderate than much of the US South, but the direction of change is toward more heat stress and weather volatility, not away from it. The practical design challenge for a multi-decade settlement is managing the intensification of the existing dry-spell / flood cycle: more runoff from intense events, longer inter-storm dry periods, and diminishing summer baseflow in smaller streams. This argues strongly for earthworks (swales, ponds, keyline) and perennial deep-rooted species mixes. The upside relative to European comparable regions: abundant annual precipitation (50 in) spread across the year means absolute water availability is not the crisis it is in Alentejo or Galicia, just the management of timing and infiltration.
