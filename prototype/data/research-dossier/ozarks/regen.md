# Regenerative Ecosystem — Ozarks (Arkansas / southern Missouri), United States

**Headline finding:** The Ozarks carry a deep and authentic homestead/off-grid culture but a sparse formal regenerative institution layer. There is no established ecovillage within the core Ozark Plateau that approaches the scale or visibility of Dancing Rabbit Ecovillage (northeast Missouri, ~280 acres, 70 residents, founded 1997) — and Dancing Rabbit is ~450 km north of the Ozark Plateau centre, geographically outside the region. Within 100 km of the central Ozarks, the ic.org and GEN directories list only a handful of established intentional communities, with most being small-scale homestead collectives or formation-stage projects. What exists instead is a rich substrate of individual homesteaders, permaculture practitioners, and back-to-the-land families — the cultural precursor to formal institutions, active on platforms like Permies.com but not yet crystallised into the kind of networked regen infrastructure found in Portugal or Transylvania.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Foundation for Intentional Community (ic.org) directory — estimated **3–8 established intentional communities** listed within ~150 km of the Ozark Plateau centre (36.3°N, 92.8°W), across Arkansas and southern Missouri, as of 2024–2025. GEN North America directory: **no established ecovillages verified** within 100 km of the core Ozark Plateau. Dancing Rabbit Ecovillage (Rutledge, MO — 280 acres, 70 residents, founded 1997) is the region's most prominent regen community but is located ~450 km north in northeastern Missouri farmland, outside the Ozark Plateau geological boundary (ic.org directory, accessed 2025; Dancing Rabbit Ecovillage website, 2024).

**Supporting facts:**
- Permies.com (the largest English-language permaculture forum) has an active "Ozarks" sub-forum with numerous posts about homestead projects, land searches, and skill-sharing — indicating a real grassroots network not yet captured in formal directories.
- Multiple formation-stage communities and permaculture farms have posted on Permies.com (2019–2024) seeking members for the Arkansas and Missouri Ozarks: northwest Arkansas (Huntsville area), north-central Arkansas, southern Missouri — but most are early-stage, unverified as established, or lack publicly documented membership or acreage.
- Dancing Rabbit Ecovillage (the region's flagship): 280 acres, ~70 members, income-sharing and cooperative models, 20+ years of operation; neighbours Sandhill Farm (income-sharing commune) and Red Earth (homestead land trust), forming a small cluster in Rutledge, MO. Though geographically distant from the Ozark Plateau, this cluster demonstrates that intentional community in Missouri farm-country is viable and durable (DR website, Wikipedia, 2024).
- No active PEPAC/EU funding or equivalent US federal regen-agriculture fund specifically targets Ozark intentional communities; the relevant US programmes (USDA RCPP, EQIP, CSP) are available to all farms and do not require community-scale operations.
- The University of Arkansas Extension Service (UAEX) and the Kerr Center for Sustainable Agriculture (Poteau, OK — ~150 km west) run agricultural education with a sustainable/regenerative framing, providing local knowledge infrastructure.
- Ozark Regional Land Trust (ORLT) is active in southern Missouri; The Nature Conservancy runs the Ozark Highlands Karst Program for conservation easements — providing land-protection mechanisms that can complement settlement goals.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The Ozarks' regen ecosystem is "pre-institutional": the cultural ingredients are present (deep homestead ethic, distrust of industrial agriculture, long growing seasons, cheap land enabling experimentation) but the formal infrastructure — intentional community legal frameworks, regen-ag funding programs, shared education nodes — is thin compared to the European candidates in this slate. This means a new project here is pioneering rather than joining; it would need to build relationships from scratch rather than tap an existing support network. The upside of low formal density is low competition for land and no "ecovillage scene" that attracts the wrong crowd. The Permies.com community and regional homestead networks would be the primary social fabric for any incoming project, and those networks have proven durable and practically skilled.
