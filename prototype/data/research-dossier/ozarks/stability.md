# Political Stability — Ozarks (Arkansas / southern Missouri), United States

**Headline finding:** The United States is a constitutional democracy with continuous institutional governance since 1789, and the Ozarks region specifically has recorded zero organised armed conflict events in the UCDP dataset across the entire post-Cold War period. At the national level, democratic-health indices show a measurable trend of erosion since 2016 — Freedom House scores the US at 77/100 in 2026, down from 90+ pre-2016; V-Dem reclassified the US from "liberal democracy" to "electoral democracy" in 2025. For a 50–100 year settlement horizon, this national institutional trajectory warrants honest acknowledgement: the US is not Portugal or Germany in terms of democratic stability trend, but the structural durability of federal, state, and local government — courts, property rights, constitutional framework — remains robust by global standards. The Ozarks itself is a deep-red rural political region with strong property-rights culture and minimal appetite for government intervention, which cuts both ways: very high tolerance for off-grid and alternative lifestyles, but limited progressive policy infrastructure.

**Key data point (with vintage):** UCDP Georeferenced Event Dataset (GED) v24.1 — confirmed organised violence events within 200 km of Ozark Plateau centre (36.3°N, 92.8°W), 2000–2023: **0 events** (UCDP GED v24.1, Uppsala University, 2024). Freedom House "Freedom in the World" 2026 score for the United States: **77/100** (down from 83 in 2024, 86 in 2020; Freedom House, 2026). V-Dem Liberal Democracy Index 2024: **0.748** (down from 0.771 in 2023; V-Dem v14, 2024).

**Supporting facts:**
- The Ozarks region has no history of organised armed conflict, civil war activity, or significant civil unrest in the modern era; the last relevant historical conflict in the area was guerrilla activity during the US Civil War (1861–1865).
- FEMA National Risk Index (v1.20, December 2025) scores Arkansas counties for composite natural hazard risk — Ozark-region counties (Newton, Searcy, Stone, Marion) score in the medium-to-relatively-low range compared to national distribution; primary hazards are tornado, severe storm, and flood, not conflict.
- The US maintains continuous federal rule of law, functioning judiciary, and constitutionally protected private property rights. Freehold land title in Arkansas is among the most secure in the world for a non-EU jurisdiction.
- Arkansas is politically conservative (Republican trifecta since 2015); state policy has consistently favoured low regulation, low taxes, and strong property rights — creating a permissive environment for rural self-sufficiency while limiting progressive policy investment.
- National-level democratic erosion concerns (V-Dem, Freedom House, Pew Research Centre 2026 reports) are relevant to long-horizon planning: polarisation, pressure on electoral institutions, and reduced rule-of-law scores are factual trends. These represent risk to the national political environment, not immediate conflict risk in the rural Ozarks.
- INFORM Global Risk Index 2025 for the United States: ~3.2/10 (low-risk band overall; INFORM GRI, 2025 release), primarily driven by natural hazard exposure, not political or conflict risk.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
For a community planning 50–100 years, the relevant stability distinction is between immediate conflict risk (negligible in the Ozarks by any global standard) and long-term institutional trajectory (genuinely mixed). The US retains deep structural resilience — property law, contract law, federal courts — that is likely more durable than particular administrations. The Ozarks' specific political culture of autonomy and low government footprint is a practical asset for alternative communities: local sheriffs and county governments in Newton, Searcy, and Stone counties have historically been indifferent to homestead and intentional-community arrangements that would face planning friction in European jurisdictions. The risk is not confiscation or state violence; it is the slow erosion of healthcare access, climate policy, and social infrastructure in a deeply rural region already underserved by those systems.
