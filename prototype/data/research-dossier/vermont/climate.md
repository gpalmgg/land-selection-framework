# Climate — Vermont / Upper New England, United States

**Headline finding:** Vermont sits in a mid-latitude continental climate zone (Dfb / humid continental, cool summer) whose trajectory under moderate emissions is unambiguous warming — roughly +1.7–2.5°C above the 1991–2020 baseline by mid-century — while remaining wet, green, and free of the heat-stress extremes that threaten Southern European regen sites. The region's climate risk profile is not heat death but compressed seasonality, more intense precipitation events, and ice-storm/ice-out shifts that alter agroforestry windows. Winters are cold and snowy; the question for 2050 practitioners is whether Zone 4/5 planting assumptions still hold.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Projected mean annual temperature 2041–2060 under SSP2-4.5: **~9.0°C** — derived from Vermont's observed 1991–2020 baseline of 7.21°C (NOAA NCEI normals, 2021) plus the Vermont State Climate Summaries 2022 (NOAA/CISESS) intermediate-scenario mid-century warming range of +1.7–2.5°C, consistent with NCA5 Chapter 21 (2023) which projects the Northeast to warm by ~3°C when global warming reaches 2°C. WorldClim CMIP6 v2.1 (2024) downscaled rasters at 2.5-arc-minute resolution corroborate this range for the centroid coordinate [-72.7, 44.0]; the multi-model ensemble mid-point for BIO1 (2041–2060, SSP2-4.5) for this cell is approximately 8.8–9.2°C.

**Supporting facts:**
- Vermont's 1991–2020 statewide mean annual temperature: **7.21°C** (44.98°F); Montpelier station mean annual high/low: 13°C / 1°C (normals via currentresults.com, sourced from NOAA NCEI).
- Vermont Climate Assessment (Gund Institute for Environment, UVM, 2021): projects annual average temperatures to increase by at least 2°F (~1.1°C) through 2050 on the lower-emissions pathway, and 4–10°F (+2.2–5.5°C) on the higher pathway.
- NCA5 Northeast chapter (2023): Northeast is the fastest-warming region in the contiguous US; heat-index values above 100°F (38°C) projected to triple by mid-century under an intermediate scenario — relevant for summer labour conditions.
- Precipitation trend: Vermont is getting wetter on an annual basis (+9% since 1900), with increases concentrated in winter/spring; summer drought spells may intensify even as annual totals rise.
- The hard-frost season (below-0°C nights) is shortening by roughly 2–3 days per decade in northern Vermont; maple-syrup season is shifting earlier and compressing.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Vermont's warming is real but moderate relative to the Mediterranean and Continental European candidates in this framework. A settlement built for 2025 conditions — passive solar design, well-insulated timber-frame structures, Zone 5 perennial selection — will remain viable through 2060 with minor adaptation, not the wholesale redesign that Alentejo practitioners face. The more critical risk vector is precipitation intensity: the 2011 Tropical Storm Irene and 2023 flooding events demonstrated that Vermont's rivers and valley bottomlands can flash-flood catastrophically. Site selection should prioritise ridge and upland positions or well-drained valley margins rather than river-bottom farmland, however fertile. Cold winters remain an energy-cost reality — passive solar plus super-insulation and wood-heat backup are non-optional, not optional retrofits.
