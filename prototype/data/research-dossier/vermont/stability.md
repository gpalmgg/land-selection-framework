# Stability — Vermont / Upper New England, United States

**Headline finding:** Vermont and the broader Upper New England region record zero armed-conflict events in any relevant geopolitical dataset. The United States has not experienced state-based armed conflict on its soil in the modern data period; Vermont specifically has no history of organised political violence, civil conflict, or mass-casualty non-state events that UCDP GED codes as organised violence. The region's stability profile is structurally high — federal constitutional continuity, strong state-level democratic institutions, and deep civic participation culture (Vermont's town-meeting governance is one of the oldest functioning deliberative-democracy systems in the US). The primary long-run stability risks are economic (rural depopulation, healthcare access, infrastructure maintenance in a small, sparsely populated state) rather than security.

**Key data point (with vintage):** UCDP Georeferenced Event Dataset (GED) v25.1 (Uppsala University, 2025, covering events through 2024): **0 armed-conflict events** within a 200 km radius of central Vermont [-72.7°, 44.0°]. The dataset codes three types of organised violence (state-based conflict, non-state conflict, one-sided violence against civilians); none are recorded in New England or the broader northeastern United States in any year covered by the dataset.

**Supporting facts:**
- Vermont has not experienced any political violence events coded by UCDP in its entire dataset coverage from 1989 onwards.
- The broader northeastern US region (within 200 km radius — covering Vermont, New Hampshire, Massachusetts, upstate New York, Quebec border) has no UCDP GED events.
- Vermont's political culture is distinctive within the US: high civic participation, strong town-meeting tradition (annual direct democracy at municipal level since colonial era), low partisan polarisation by US state standards.
- US federal constitutional stability: Vermont is one of the original states; federal institutions have maintained continuity including through the US Civil War (Vermont sent proportionally more soldiers than any other Union state).
- Crime rates: Vermont consistently ranks among the 5 safest US states by violent crime rate. FBI UCR / Vermont Crime Information Center data (2023): Vermont violent crime rate 1.5 per 1,000 residents — well below the national average of 3.7 per 1,000.
- Economic stability risks: Vermont's population is ageing and the rural workforce is thin; the state relies heavily on property taxes and has a modest industrial base. These are long-run rural-development challenges, not security risks.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
For a long-horizon settlement (50–100 year frame), stability risk in Vermont is essentially nil on the security dimension. The relevant risk register is: (1) federal policy shifts affecting land-use law, taxation, or conservation programmes — real but slow-moving and litigable; (2) economic marginalisation of rural Vermont, which affects services, healthcare access, and labour availability; (3) climate-induced infrastructure stress (flooding, ice storms) that could disrupt utilities and road access. None of these approach the level of risk that a conflict-adjacent or institutionally fragile context would present. US legal systems including property rights, contract law, and environmental review are mature and well-litigated in Vermont — providing a stable regulatory environment for long-term land stewardship.
