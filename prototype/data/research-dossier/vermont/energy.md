# Energy — Vermont / Upper New England, United States

**Headline finding:** Vermont has moderate solar resource by global standards — sufficient for meaningful grid-tied and off-grid PV installations, but well below the yield achievable in Southern Europe, the US Southwest, or tropical regions. The state compensates with strong wind potential on ridges and summits, abundant biomass (78% forested), and a mature small-scale renewable energy policy environment including net metering, group net metering, and the Standard Offer programme. Vermont was the first US state to achieve more than 20% in-state electricity from solar; energy self-sufficiency at the settlement scale is achievable, but cold-climate heating loads require a thermal strategy beyond PV alone.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas v2.7 (World Bank / Solargis, 2023) estimated photovoltaic power output (PVOUT) for central Vermont (centroid ~44.0°N, -72.7°W): **~1,200–1,300 kWh/kWp/yr** — consistent with GHI observations for Montpelier (05602 zip) of 3.61 kWh/m²/day annual average (~1,318 kWh/m²/yr, Solar Energy Local / NREL NSRDB, 2024), and corroborated by a documented 15 kW Vermont installation producing 19,480 kWh/yr (~1,299 kWh/kWp). Note: the Global Solar Atlas interactive PVOUT raster could not be directly queried in this session; the value above is triangulated from NREL NSRDB-derived GHI data and documented Vermont system performance. Confidence: medium. Direct raster extraction via globalsolaratlas.info/map is recommended for project-level use.

**Supporting facts:**
- Vermont statewide average: ~4.1 peak sun hours/day, translating to ~1,150–1,350 kWh/kWp/yr depending on system tilt, orientation, and inverter losses.
- Seasonal distribution is strongly skewed: June–August deliver ~5.4–5.5 kWh/m²/day; December–January deliver ~1.5–2.0 kWh/m²/day, creating a 3:1 summer-to-winter irradiance ratio. Battery storage or grid interconnection is essential for winter energy balance.
- Vermont's Renewable Energy Standard (RES) requires 75% renewable electricity by 2032 and 100% by 2032 for the emissions-free portion; the state's grid is among the lowest-carbon in the US Northeast (approx. 75–80% renewable mix as of 2023).
- Net metering is available for systems up to 500 kW; group net metering allows community-scale generation to benefit multiple accounts — well-suited to intentional community models.
- Wood heat: Vermont has the highest per-capita residential wood-heat penetration of any US state; modern biomass (wood pellet boilers, gasification) is a mature technology here and provides resilient cold-weather backup that solar cannot reliably supply.
- Wind: ridge-top wind potential is significant (Class 4–5 on exposed ridges); however, Vermont Act 250 environmental review applies to commercial wind installations and several projects have been contested on landscape grounds.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The energy equation in Vermont is dominated by winter heating, not electricity. A standard Vermont farmhouse uses 2,000–4,000 litres of fuel oil or equivalent annually; a well-insulated new build with passive solar can reduce this by 60–80%, but the cold-climate baseline is a persistent cost and energy intensity. For a regen settlement, the optimal strategy is: super-insulated passive-solar buildings (target Passive House or equivalent) + south-facing grid-tied PV array + wood pellet or biomass boiler backup + possibly small hydro if streamflow permits. The grid is a genuine asset — Vermont's low-carbon grid means that importing winter electricity carries a low emissions penalty. Off-grid is achievable in summer but technically demanding and expensive in winter; most Vermont intentional communities remain grid-connected.
