# Regenerative Ecosystem — Vermont / Upper New England, United States

**Headline finding:** Vermont has the most concentrated and culturally embedded back-to-land and regenerative agriculture ecosystem of any US state relative to its population. The 1970s counterculture back-to-the-land migration (Vermont received an estimated 35,000+ new rural settlers 1968–1975) seeded a durable infrastructure of intentional communities, small organic farms, permaculture education centres, and land trusts that has persisted and professionalised across five decades. Within a 100 km radius of central Vermont, at least 20–30 documented intentional communities, ecovillages, and cohousing projects are active (ic.org + Ecovillage Tours Vermont count, 2024), alongside multiple permaculture education centres, a mature organic-farming support network (NOFA-VT), and the longest-running Centre for Sustainable Agriculture in the US.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Intentional communities and ecovillages within ~100 km of central Vermont: **~25–35 active documented communities** — based on: Vermont ic.org directory (9–10 Vermont-only entries, 2024), Ecovillage Tours Vermont (8 featured sites, 2025), Vermont Public / Brave Little State survey (50–60 communities statewide across all intentional-living types, 2023), plus documented communities in adjacent New Hampshire, Massachusetts, and upstate New York within the 100 km radius (RiverSong Cohousing NH, Pioneer Valley Cohousing MA, Sirius Community MA among them). Precise GEN Global Ecovillage Network count for the radius: **UNVERIFIED at the sub-regional level** — GEN North America does not publish a georeferenced directory that could be queried for this radius programmatically. Vermont ic.org count alone is confirmed at 9 entries (2024).

**Supporting facts:**
- Named Vermont communities (documented 2023–2025): Cobb Hill Cohousing (Hartland), Headwaters Ecovillage (Cabot), Living Tree Alliance (Moretown), Burlington Cohousing (Burlington), White Pine Cohousing (Montpelier), Heartbeet Lifesharing (Hardwick), Bristol Village Cohousing (Bristol), Earthseed Ecovillage (in development), Rah Rah Village (southern VT), ReTribe (Underhill, 80 acres at base of Mt. Mansfield). Source: ic.org and Ecovillage Tours Vermont, 2024–2025.
- NOFA-VT (Northeast Organic Farming Association of Vermont) — one of the strongest state-level organic farming associations in the US; runs an annual winter conference, apprenticeship programme, technical assistance, and soil health advocacy.
- Vermont has the highest per-capita rate of certified organic farms of any US state east of the Mississippi; certified organic land in Vermont has roughly doubled since 2008.
- Ben Falk / Whole Systems Design (Moretown, VT): internationally cited permaculture design practice and education centre; the "Resilient Farm and Homestead" book (2013) shaped a generation of regen practitioners globally.
- Vermont Land Trust (founded 1977): has conserved over 700 farms and 640,000 acres; provides direct support for agricultural land access including resale-price-restricted farmland for beginning farmers.
- Funding programmes accessible to regen communities: USDA NRCS Environmental Quality Incentives Program (EQIP), USDA Conservation Stewardship Program (CSP), Vermont Housing and Conservation Board (VHCB) farmland grants, USDA Beginning Farmer/Rancher grants.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Vermont's regen ecosystem is unusual in that it is not primarily aspirational — it has 50 years of operational history, including communities that failed and communities that adapted and survived. This means practitioners arriving here find real-world learning: NOFA-VT maintains records of what worked in Vermont's climate and soils, the Vermont Land Trust has structured templates for conservation easements and farm transfer, and communities like Cobb Hill and Heartbeet have navigated the full lifecycle of intentional community governance. The ecosystem is dense enough that newcomers can plug in at multiple entry points (apprenticeships, farm-shares, PDC courses, town meetings) before committing land capital. The risk is that this same ecosystem has generated high cultural expectations — "doing it right" in Vermont carries social accountability that can be demanding for outsiders. Community integration is easier here than in culturally closed regions but requires genuine engagement with existing networks rather than arriving with a fully-formed outside vision.
