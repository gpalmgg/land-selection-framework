# Water — Vermont / Upper New England, United States

**Headline finding:** Vermont sits in one of the most water-abundant zones in North America. Annual precipitation averages 1,000–1,200 mm statewide, exceeding evapotranspiration in almost all years, resulting in perennially positive streamflow surpluses. WRI Aqueduct 4.0 classifies northeastern US basins in the lowest water-stress category globally — a direct contrast to the Western US and Southern Europe. Even under 2050 business-as-usual projections, the region is not expected to enter medium water stress. The practitioner challenge is not scarcity but management of abundant, sometimes excess, water.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 (2023), 2050 business-as-usual (SSP3/RCP7.0) water-stress score for the Connecticut River and Champlain basin (HydroSHEDS level-6 sub-basins covering central Vermont): **"Low" category, score < 0.1** — indicating withdrawal-to-availability ratios well below 10%, consistent with Aqueduct's published national-level characterisation that northeastern US states "do not have a significant water risk when looking at averaged water stress factors" (WRI, 2023). USGS streamflow monitoring at Vermont gauges shows above-median flows in most years (USGS WaterWatch, 2024).

**Supporting facts:**
- Vermont receives 1,000–1,200 mm precipitation per year distributed across all seasons; the state has no dry season (Dfb classification).
- Sleepers River Research Watershed (northeastern Vermont, USGS-monitored since 1958): annual streamflow closely tracks precipitation minus evapotranspiration, consistently positive — water availability is "rarely a problem in forests of the humid, temperate northeastern US" (USGS FS-166-99, 1999).
- Historic drought events (2000, 2016, 2020, 2022) were brief, largely summer-fall seasonal deficits, not multi-year hydrological droughts of the kind recorded in the US Southwest or Mediterranean.
- Vermont's Agency of Natural Resources regulates in-stream flows via the Streamflow Protection Program; groundwater wells are generally productive across most of the state, recharged by abundant precipitation and permeable glacial deposits.
- Climate projections for the Northeast include increased annual precipitation (+5–10% by 2050 under intermediate scenarios, NCA5 Chapter 21), though summer droughts may lengthen slightly — still within manageable storage design parameters.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Vermont's water abundance is genuine and defensible as a long-term asset. For site selection, the practical risk is the reverse of scarcity: bottomland flooding, high water tables limiting root systems and septic/composting systems, and spring mud seasons that can immobilise vehicles and construction for 4–8 weeks. Settlements that assume dry Mediterranean drainage conditions will fail; those designed for a wet, freeze-thaw cycle — with proper French drains, raised beds, swales draining to woodlands rather than impounding, and gravity-fed spring or artesian sources — will have reliable water at very low infrastructure cost. Spring and well development is lightly regulated in Vermont; rainwater harvesting is legal and common.
