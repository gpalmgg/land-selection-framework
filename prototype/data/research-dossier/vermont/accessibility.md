# Accessibility — Vermont / Upper New England, United States

**Headline finding:** Vermont is genuinely rural by US standards — the second least-populated state in the US after Wyoming — but it is not remote in the global sense. Burlington (Vermont's largest city, pop. ~45,000) has an international airport with connections to major hubs; Montpelier (the state capital, pop. ~8,000) is reachable by Amtrak from New York City in 5–6 hours. The rural landscape has reliable road access to most settled land parcels, high-speed broadband penetration approaching 90% (as of 2024), and is within 3–4 hours of Boston (pop. 4.9M) and 4–5 hours of New York City (pop. 18.8M). For a regen settlement requiring selective urban connection — markets, airports, specialist healthcare, universities — Vermont is accessible enough to sustain an outward-facing community.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Vermont statewide population density: **27 persons/km²** (69.8 persons/sq mi) — US Census Bureau, 2020 Decennial Census (total population 643,077; land area 24,906 km²). This is the 49th lowest state density in the US, comparable to rural New South Wales (Australia) or the Rhodope mountains of Bulgaria. Highly rural counties (Essex: 9 persons/km²; Orleans: 15 persons/km²) contrast with the Chittenden County Burlington corridor (~122 persons/km²). The GFW / GHSL-equivalent rural population density in the agricultural/forested interior of central Vermont is approximately **5–15 persons/km²**.

**Supporting facts:**
- Burlington International Airport (BTV): daily direct flights to major US hubs (Boston, New York JFK/LGA, Philadelphia, Washington DC, Chicago, Charlotte, Denver); no direct transatlantic routes — Europe requires a connection.
- Amtrak Vermonter (New York Penn Station to St Albans, VT) and Amtrak Ethan Allen Express (New York to Rutland) provide rail access without car travel; frequency is 1 train/day per line, limiting utility.
- Interstate highways: I-89 (Burlington–Montpelier–Concord NH–Boston) and I-91 (Springfield MA–White River Junction–St Johnsbury–Canadian border) provide fast N-S and E-W access.
- Vermont is the only state in the continental US without a McDonald's (as of 2024) — a cultural data point reflecting the deliberate character of the state's ruralism and its resistance to certain forms of commercial development.
- Broadband: Vermont has invested heavily in rural fibre deployment through the Vermont Community Broadband Board; ~89% of Vermont addresses have access to 100/20 Mbps or faster as of 2024 (Vermont Agency of Digital Services).
- Healthcare: major medical centres in Burlington (UVM Medical Center), Dartmouth-Hitchcock in Lebanon, NH (90 min from central VT), and Springfield, MA (2 hrs) — adequate for specialist care but rural primary care is undersupplied, a known Vermont structural issue.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Vermont's rural character is by design and by culture — the state has resisted big-box retail, franchise development, and suburban sprawl more successfully than almost any other US state, aided by Act 250 land-use controls. For a regen settlement, this means a high-quality rural environment is preserved but also that basic urban infrastructure (specialty goods, certain services) requires planning and travel. The internet connectivity situation has improved dramatically since 2020; remote-work compatible bandwidth is now available on most settled rural parcels. The real accessibility constraint is healthcare: Vermont's rural areas face primary care physician shortages, and a large settlement will need to factor healthcare access into site selection and budget — either proximity to Burlington or a shared community healthcare model.
