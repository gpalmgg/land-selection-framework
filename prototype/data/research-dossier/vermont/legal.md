# Legal + Economic — Vermont / Upper New England, United States

**Headline finding:** Vermont's legal regime is uniquely favourable for long-duration regen land stewardship among all US states — and arguably globally competitive as a framework for intentional communities. Three interlocking pillars define it: (1) the Current Use / Use Value Appraisal Program, which dramatically reduces property tax on agricultural and forest land enrolled in productive stewardship, locking in affordability over time; (2) Act 250, Vermont's 1970 land-use law, which controls sprawl and large-scale development and has preserved the rural landscape that makes Vermont attractive; and (3) strong land-trust infrastructure, providing legal vehicles (conservation easements, limited-equity farm transfers) for multigenerational land protection. Farm real estate costs are high relative to Southern Europe but low relative to the US Northeast corridor; forest land remains affordable in absolute terms.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Vermont farm real estate average value: **$4,350/acre** (~€4,000/acre at 2024 exchange rates; or ~$10,750/ha) — USDA NASS Land Values 2024 Summary (August 2024, reporting on 2024 survey data). Forested/recreational rural land: **$500–$2,000/acre** ($1,235–$4,940/ha) for remote parcels with limited road access; buildable rural land with utilities: **$5,000–$20,000/acre** ($12,355–$49,420/ha). Under the Current Use Program, enrolled agricultural land is assessed for tax purposes at **$483/acre** and forest land at **$188/acre** (Vermont Department of Taxes, FY2024) — dramatically below market value, reducing annual property tax burden to a fraction of the cost for unenrolled land.

**Supporting facts:**
- **Current Use Program (Use Value Appraisal, UVA):** Enacted 1978 under Vermont Statutes Annotated 32 V.S.A. § 3751–3787. Qualifies land that is (a) forest land ≥25 contiguous acres under an approved forest management plan, or (b) agricultural land ≥25 contiguous acres actively farmed, or (c) conservation land under certain conditions. As of 2024: ~19,600 enrolled parcels, ~16,000 unique landowners, 2.5 million acres enrolled — approximately one-third of Vermont's total land area. Annual FY2024 use values: agricultural land $483/acre, forest land $188/acre (vs. fair-market taxable values of $4,000–$10,000+/acre). Withdrawal from the programme triggers a land-use change tax equal to 10% of fair market value (payable on withdrawal); this penalty discourages development and locks enrolled land into productive use. Source: Vermont Department of Taxes; Vermont Legislature FY2025 overview.
- **Act 250 (10 V.S.A. Chapter 151):** Vermont's landmark 1970 land-use and development control law, administered by the Land Use Review Board (reformed 2024 under Act 181). Triggers: any development project involving (a) >10 acres, (b) >10 housing lots/units within 5 years, (c) any development above 2,500 feet elevation, or (d) commercial/industrial development on >1 acre in unzoned towns. Reviews projects against 10 criteria covering air/water quality, soils, flood risk, wildlife habitat, school capacity, local services, aesthetics, primary agricultural soils, forest fragmentation (new Criterion 8C, effective 12/31/2026), energy, and consistency with municipal/regional plans. Act 250 has successfully limited sprawl for 55 years; its primary effect for regen settlements is positive (preserving rural character) but it adds process time (3–12 months) and consultant costs ($5,000–$50,000) for any development meeting the thresholds. Creating a multi-household intentional community on >10 acres typically requires an Act 250 permit.
- **Foreign/non-resident ownership:** No citizenship or residency requirement to own land in Vermont (or any US state, with minor exceptions for agricultural land under AFIDA — Agricultural Foreign Investment Disclosure Act requires disclosure, not prohibition). Non-US buyers must comply with FIRPTA withholding rules on sale but face no restriction on purchase. Non-immigrant visa holders may purchase land; however, building and residing on it full-time requires legal residency status.
- **Land trust infrastructure:** Vermont Land Trust (VLT, founded 1977) and Vermont Housing and Conservation Board (VHCB) provide conservation easements, farmland purchase programmes, and beginning-farmer land access. VLT has conserved over 11% of Vermont's land area.
- **Collective ownership structures recognised:** Limited Liability Company (LLC), Limited Liability Partnership (LLP), Community Land Trust (CLT) under Vermont Statutes, Nonprofit Corporation, or simple tenancy-in-common with a recorded co-ownership agreement. Cooperative housing (under 11 V.S.A. Chapter 15) is well-established and used by Burlington Cohousing and others.
- **Zoning:** Vermont has no uniform statewide zoning; municipalities adopt their own zoning bylaws. In unzoned towns (about 50 out of 255 towns), Act 250 is the primary development control. Municipal zoning can be more or less permissive than Act 250 — always verify at the town level before purchase.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The single most important legal move for a long-duration regen settlement in Vermont is enrolling land in the Current Use Program immediately on purchase — this cuts annual property tax by 80–90% on qualifying agricultural and forest acreage. The 10% withdrawal penalty is a powerful commitment device: once enrolled, the land is effectively locked into productive use in perpetuity unless the community chooses to pay the penalty. Combined with a conservation easement held by VLT or a local land trust, this creates a two-layer legal structure that protects the land's regen character across ownership transitions. The Act 250 permit process is real cost and time but also provides environmental review that can build community credibility and neighbours' trust — the public hearing process gives the community an opportunity to demonstrate its stewardship intentions. For non-US practitioners: Vermont's property law is English-common-law derived, well-documented, and litigated — legal due diligence (title search, survey, municipal plan check) is straightforward and costs $2,000–$5,000 at a Vermont real estate attorney.
