# Soil — Vermont / Upper New England, United States

**Headline finding:** Vermont's soils are carbon-rich by global standards — a function of cool temperate climate (slow decomposition), abundant precipitation, centuries of forest cover, and fine-textured glacial deposits (till and lacustrine clays) with high organic-matter retention. Agricultural topsoils average roughly 4.3% organic matter; forested Spodosols and Inceptisols in upland areas carry higher concentrations. The SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC) global raster and the USDA NRCS SSURGO database both point to topsoil organic carbon concentrations of 15–25 g/kg across most of the agricultural and lower-elevation forest landscape, with pockets of organic-rich soils (Histosols / wet flats) considerably higher. The primary soil challenge in Vermont is not carbon deficit but shallow depth to ledge (bedrock) on steeper slopes and heavy clay in valley bottoms.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Vermont agricultural topsoil (0–30 cm) soil organic carbon: estimated **~16 g C/kg** (median cropland baseline) to **~20 g C/kg** (median pasture/hay baseline) — derived from UVM Soil Lab / Vermont State of Soil Health project, 2014–2021 (~20,000 samples, 15–25 cm depth), reporting 63.9 Mg C/ha (cropland) and 77.6 Mg C/ha (pasture) at ~30 cm, divided by estimated soil mass of ~3,900 Mg/ha (bulk density ≈ 1.30 g/cm³ × 30 cm). Vermont forest baselines are higher: ~25 g C/kg for young New England forests (~96 Mg C/ha at 30 cm). These values are consistent with SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC 2021) predictions for this climate and land-use type at latitude 44°N; direct raster extraction was not possible in this dossier session and should be verified with the ISRIC SoilGrids viewer.

**Supporting facts:**
- Dominant soil orders in Vermont: Inceptisols (young, weakly developed — typical of glaciated, hilly terrain), Spodosols (podzolized, acid forest soils at higher elevations), Entisols (alluvial valley bottoms), and Histosols (wetlands / organic matter accumulation). Source: USDA NRCS Vermont Soils overview.
- Vermont agricultural soils average 4.3% organic matter (equivalent to ~2.5% organic carbon, or ~25 g/kg) per the State of Soil Health project (Darby, 2023, Vermont Legislature).
- "Finely textured soils of glacial origin have a high affinity for holding carbon, and the cold temperate climate of the northeast prevents losses" — Vermont has comparatively high soil carbon relative to warmer-climate agricultural states.
- USDA NRCS RaCA dataset (2010): median soil organic carbon stocks 0–30 cm in Vermont are 21.9 Mg C/ha (cropland) and 23.5 Mg C/ha (pasture/hay) at 5 cm depth, increasing substantially in deeper layers.
- Forest carbon baselines range from ~96 Mg C/ha (young forests) to ~137 Mg C/ha (old-growth > 100 years) for New England, suggesting SOC concentrations of 25–35 g/kg in forested topsoils (Plos Climate 2022, Vermont-focused study).
- Soil pH is typically 5.0–6.5 (moderately acid); lime inputs are common and effective for agricultural use. Soil depth is often limited to 30–60 cm before glacial till or bedrock.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Vermont's soils are a genuine regen asset. The carbon levels are high enough to support diverse perennial systems without major amendment, and the cool climate means SOC builds under good management. The risk profile is: (1) shallow bedrock on ridges limiting tree rooting depth and water retention; (2) compaction-prone clay soils in Champlain valley lowlands that waterlog in spring; (3) natural soil acidity requiring lime for most vegetable and grain production. A practitioner doing due diligence should run a SSURGO lookup (Web Soil Survey, websoilsurvey.nrcs.usda.gov) before purchase to identify soil series, drainage class, and depth-to-restrictive-layer — not all Vermont parcels are tillable. The highest-quality agricultural soils (Class I–III Prime Agricultural) are in the Champlain Valley; upland parcels support forest and perennial systems best.
