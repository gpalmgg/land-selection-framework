# Energy autonomy — Rural Estonia (Saaremaa / Southeast), Estonia

**Headline finding:** Estonia's renewable resource is wind-dominated, with substantial Baltic offshore potential anchored near Saaremaa, plus emerging utility-scale onshore wind and solar across the mainland. PV is genuinely weak in winter at this latitude but acceptable over the full year. Biomass (firewood, agricultural biogas) remains a cornerstone of rural heating; the national target is 100% renewable electricity by 2030.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas long-term PVOUT for Estonia: **~950–1,050 kWh/kWp/yr** (Solargis / World Bank Global Solar Atlas, 2020 release, ~1 km resolution). Saaremaa achieves the upper end (~1,050 kWh/kWp/yr) due to clearer Baltic-coast skies; northern mainland slightly lower. Among the lowest annual yields in Europe.

**Supporting facts:**
- Global Wind Atlas (DTU / World Bank, v3.3, 2023) shows mean wind speed at 100 m of **7.5–9.5 m/s** on Saaremaa and the western Estonian islands, declining to ~6–7 m/s in the interior mainland — Class 4–5 resource on the islands.
- Tuul Energy (Deep Wind Offshore) is developing a ~1 GW offshore wind project west of Saaremaa (combined area ~252 km²), planned as one of the largest Baltic offshore wind farms.
- Sopi-Tootsi (mainland Estonia) is set to become the Baltic's largest combined wind/solar farm — 38 turbines + 112,000 PV panels, projected to produce ~770 GWh/year (~10% of national electricity demand).
- Estonia targets 100% renewable electricity by 2030 (Invest in Estonia, 2024–2025); the *väiketootja* (small producer) framework supports household and community renewable installations with net-metering up to 15 kW domestic, ~50 kW community.
- Saaremaa hosts Estonia's first post-independence agricultural-biogas CHP, in Valjala parish, demonstrating practical small-scale biogas integration with rural livestock operations.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Energy autonomy in rural Estonia requires careful design around extreme winter PV degradation: a 5 kWp array generates plenty in May–August but contributes near-zero in December–February. The realistic stack for a settlement is PV (summer) + small wind (winter, where siting permits) + battery + biomass winter heating + heat pump. Saaremaa is materially better for wind than the southeast mainland — IEC Class I conditions support reliable small-wind under 50 kW where local planning allows. The southeast (Võrumaa, Põlvamaa) faces stronger forest-cover wind shadowing but offers excellent biomass resource from sustainably-harvested local firewood, plus an established culture of wood-fuelled smoke saunas (UNESCO-listed Setu-Võro smoke sauna tradition). District-level renewable cooperatives are an emerging model. Permitting is fast by EU standards — Estonia is the European leader in e-government, and renewable-energy applications process digitally and quickly.
