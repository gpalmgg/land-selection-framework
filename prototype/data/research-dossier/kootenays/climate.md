# Climate — BC Interior — the Kootenays, Canada

**Headline finding:** The Kootenays are warming faster than the global mean — typical of interior mountain regions — but the altitudinal geography of the West Kootenay provides genuine climate-selection leverage. Valley settlements around Nelson (~540 m) are projected to reach mean annual temperatures 2.5–3.5°C above 1960s baselines by the 2050s, pushing toward a warmer, drier summer regime, while the region's elevation gradient means practitioners can site at 700–1,200 m to stay in a cooler, more temperate envelope. The headline risk is not heat but hydrological volatility: shrinking glacial contributions and increasingly severe summer drought episodes interspersed with higher-intensity precipitation events.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Projected mean annual temperature at valley-floor elevations (Nelson area, ~540 m) by the 2050s under intermediate emissions: **~9.5–10.5°C** (up from a 1981–2010 baseline of ~7.0°C for Nelson), representing a warming of **+2.5–3.5°C** relative to 1960s norms. This is consistent with the BC provincial 50th-percentile CMIP6 warming of +1.22°C under SSP2-4.5 for 2041–2060 averaged across the full province (including cool north), and with regional Columbia Basin projections showing 2.5–3.5°C of additional warming by the 2050s relative to the 1960s baseline. (Sources: ClimateNA / AdaptWest ensemble for CMIP6 SSP2-4.5 2041–2060; Nelson Climate Profile, Nelson Public Library 2022; ClimateData.ca CMIP6 provincial tabulations 2024.)

**Supporting facts:**
- Nelson's recorded mean annual temperature 1981–2010 baseline: approximately 7.0°C (Environment and Climate Change Canada climate normals station 1118430, Nelson).
- Columbia Basin and Boundary communities are projected to see average annual temperatures 2.5–3.5°C warmer than the 1960s by the 2050s (Nelson Climate Profile, September 2022).
- BC province-wide CMIP6 SSP2-4.5 50th-percentile warming for 2041–2060: +1.22°C relative to the 1981–2010 baseline — the Kootenay Interior warms faster than this provincial mean due to the continental interior amplification pattern (ClimateData.ca CMIP6 tabulations, 2024).
- The 2023 wildfire season burned more than 2.84 million hectares across BC — more than double any prior year on record — driven partly by record May heat and early snowmelt; the Kootenays experienced Drought Level 5 conditions (highest category) and evacuation orders in the Aylwin and Komonko Creek area (BC Wildfire Service, 2023).
- Glacial retreat in the Columbia Basin has already passed "peak water" for August glacier-melt contribution; declining glacier contributions will reduce summer baseflow in coming decades (University of British Columbia study, 2020, cited in Columbia Basin Trust reporting).
- Winter conditions remain continental-cold with reliable snowpack: Nelson averages ~230 cm of annual snowfall. Heating season runs approximately 5 months (November–March).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The Kootenay climate trajectory contains both an asset and a liability that practitioners must think about together. The asset: current summers (June–August) remain genuinely temperate by global regen-settlement standards — hot days exist but sustained extreme heat above 35°C is infrequent in the valley floors, and mid-elevation sites (800–1,100 m) offer a further buffer. The liability: summer drought intensity is increasing. The 2021 heat dome, 2023 mega-fire season, and multi-year glacier retreat all point toward a future where July–August water stress becomes a planning constraint, not just a nuisance. Communities must design for cistern capacity, greywater reuse, and spring-fed or deep-well water sources that hold through the dry season. The altitudinal gradient is the region's most valuable climate-adaptation lever: a settlement at 900 m today experiences roughly what the valley floor experienced in the 1970s — projecting that forward gives practitioners a meaningful "climate lag." The flip side is a serious winter energy burden (5-month heating season, snow-load engineering, road access).
