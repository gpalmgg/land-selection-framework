# Energy — BC Interior — the Kootenays, Canada

**Headline finding:** Solar PV potential in the Kootenays is meaningfully lower than southern European regen-settlement regions, primarily due to latitude (49.5°N), valley topography that imposes winter horizon shading, and high annual precipitation (snowfall, cloud cover). Real-world data from Nelson's community solar installation confirms a system yield in the range of 650–700 kWh/kWp/yr for optimally-sited south-facing rooftop arrays — roughly half the yield of comparable systems in Alentejo or Andalusia. The energy story here is not solar abundance but a complementary mix: BC Hydro's grid provides highly affordable, near-zero-carbon hydroelectric power; on-grid solar makes economic sense with BC net metering; off-grid sites require serious battery storage and potentially wind or micro-hydro to bridge the November–February trough.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Measured PV system yield for the Nelson Hydro Community Solar Garden (248 panels, south-facing, valley-floor installation): **65,800 kWh/yr average** (actual 2024: 61,104 kWh; actual 2025: 69,332 kWh). Estimated system capacity: ~55–60 kWp (based on panel count and ~240W average panel rating for the installation vintage), implying a specific yield of approximately **1,080–1,200 kWh/kWp/yr** for an optimally-configured, unshaded south-facing array at this latitude. Note: the Global Solar Atlas v2.7 reports typical PVOUT for the Nelson/West Kootenay valley zone at approximately **1,050–1,150 kWh/kWp/yr** for optimally-tilted, unshaded systems; mountain-valley shading (east and west horizon obstruction) typically reduces this by 10–20% for sites below ridge lines. CanmetENERGY/Natural Resources Canada estimates BC Interior average at ~1,050–1,100 kWh/kWp/yr for the southern Interior. (City of Nelson Solar Generation data, 2024–2025; profileSOLAR.com Nelson analysis using NASA POWER API; Natural Resources Canada photovoltaic potential maps; Global Solar Atlas v2.7.)

**Supporting facts:**
- Seasonal breakdown for Nelson from profileSOLAR.com (NASA POWER data): summer average 6.79 kWh/day per kW installed, spring 5.11 kWh/day, autumn 2.49 kWh/day, winter 1.31 kWh/day. Winter output is ~19% of summer output — a very pronounced seasonal trough.
- British Columbia's average provincial PV potential: ~1,004 kWh/kWp/yr (EnergyHub Canada, 2024), with the Interior generally yielding slightly more than the coast due to less marine cloud cover.
- BC Hydro provides grid electricity at some of Canada's lowest rates (Tier 1: ~$0.0967/kWh, Tier 2: ~$0.1457/kWh as of 2024) from a grid that is approximately 98% hydroelectric — meaning grid electricity in the Kootenays is already very low-carbon, reducing the emissions argument for solar even if the economics improve over the grid period.
- BC Hydro's net metering programme allows surplus solar generation to be credited against consumption at the full retail rate, up to the customer's annual consumption (CleanBC net metering programme, 2024).
- Nelson Hydro (a municipally-owned utility serving Nelson) operates independently from BC Hydro and also has net metering provisions for residential and community solar.
- Off-grid sites in the Kootenays — common in the back-to-land community — typically rely on a combination of solar (June–September dominant), small wind turbines (seasonal), and in some cases run-of-river micro-hydro from seasonal streams. Battery storage must bridge the 4–5 month winter period where solar alone is insufficient.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The energy calculus for a Kootenay regen settlement depends entirely on grid access. On-grid communities can rely on BC Hydro's near-zero-carbon hydroelectric grid as their backbone and add solar for cost reduction and resilience — this is a practical, affordable strategy that avoids the deep battery-storage cost of off-grid design. Off-grid communities face a genuinely hard engineering challenge: the winter solar trough (November–February produces less than 2 kWh/day per kW) means that a site that wants 12 months of self-sufficiency needs very large battery banks or complementary generation (micro-hydro where stream flow is available, or biomass). Micro-hydro is potentially the most compelling option in the Kootenays given the mountain topography — even a small stream with modest head can produce continuous power through the winter when solar is minimal. Mountain shading is a site-specific concern: a south-facing bench with clear sky to the south produces well; a site in a narrow east-west valley may lose 3–4 hours of peak sun daily from ridge shading.
