# Soil — BC Interior — the Kootenays, Canada

**Headline finding:** Kootenay valley soils are functional but characteristically limited: coarse-textured, well-drained, and low in natural moisture-holding capacity, with organic carbon content moderate for a mountain-forest context but insufficient for high-intensity row-crop agriculture without amendment. The region's steep topography concentrates the best soils on narrow valley benches and river terraces; only about 20,000 hectares of the 86,000+ hectares classified as Class 1–4 agricultural land are actually in agricultural use. For regenerative settlement, the soil story is: workable, improvable, but irrigation-dependent in summer — and highly variable across sites.

**Key data point (with vintage):** SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC, 2020) reports topsoil soil organic carbon (SOC) for the Kootenay valley zone (representative coordinates ~49.5°N, 117.0°W) at approximately **20–40 g/kg** for 0–30 cm depth in cultivated/grassland valley soils, and **40–80 g/kg** in forested mineral soils at similar elevations. These figures are consistent with British Columbia's Soil Landscapes of Canada (SLC) v3.2 characterisation of Kootenay Brunisolic and Luvisolic soils as having moderate-to-good organic matter in the A horizon but limited sub-surface carbon storage due to coarse textures and low clay content. (SoilGrids 2.0, ISRIC 2020; SLC v3.2, Agriculture and Agri-Food Canada, published 2013, still current reference for BC classification; UBC MLWS West Kootenay Agricultural Potential report, 2014.)

**Supporting facts:**
- West Kootenay soils are described as well-draining with low moisture-holding capacity, attributed to coarse texture, low clay content, and moderate organic matter — moisture retention is the primary limitation to agricultural use (UBC MLWS Agricultural Potential of the West Kootenay, 2014).
- Approximately 86,000 ha in the West Kootenay are classified Class 1–4 (capable of supporting fruit, vegetables, and grains); an additional 113,000 ha in Class 5 can support forage, pasture, orchards, and niche crops. However, only ~20,000 ha (~17% of Class 1–4 land) is actively in agricultural use (UBC MLWS, 2014).
- Topography and stoniness limit field-scale mechanised cropping, but the soils are well-suited to small-plot mixed vegetables, orchards, and niche crops such as grapes in climatically suitable lower-elevation sites (BC Ministry of Agriculture, Kootenay region overview).
- Irrigation has the potential to significantly increase productivity; rising temperatures are expected to increase irrigation requirements further, making water access and soil water-holding capacity co-limiting factors (UBC MLWS, 2014).
- The BC Kootenay Soil Building Network (KBFA) documents active cover-cropping and compost-integration practices on Kootenay farms, demonstrating that SOC can be built systematically in the region's coarse-textured soils over 5–10 year timescales.
- Soil Landscapes of Canada v3.2 classifies much of the West Kootenay valley floor as Brunisolic (Grey Brown Luvisol / Dystric Brunisol) — moderate fertility, pH typically 5.5–6.5, suitable for vegetable and fruit production with lime amendment on acid sites.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Kootenay soils reward investment and punish neglect differently than European loam-dominant soils. The coarse texture means water and nutrients drain quickly — a community that builds deep mulch systems, adds compost aggressively, and installs drip irrigation can turn a marginal bench site into a productive market garden within 3–5 years. A community that relies on broadcast irrigation and no organic matter will exhaust the soil's capacity quickly. The strong forest-soil interface is an asset: fallen-wood decomposition, mycelial networks, and leaf litter accumulate rapidly in logged clearings, giving any site that has recovered from disturbance a biological head start. The practical constraint for community-scale food production is land geometry: usable flat or gently-sloping valley land is scarce, fragmented, and already in ALR protection, which means large consolidated growing blocks are hard to assemble without paying a premium.
