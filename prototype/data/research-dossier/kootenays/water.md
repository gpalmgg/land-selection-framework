# Water — BC Interior — the Kootenays, Canada

**Headline finding:** The Kootenays sit within the Columbia River basin — one of Canada's largest and historically most water-abundant river systems — and current baseline water stress is low to very low by global standards. However, the trajectory is toward tightening: glacial contributions to summer baseflow are already declining, severe drought episodes (Level 4–5 on BC's five-tier scale) are occurring with increasing frequency, and summer peak-demand stress is emerging even in a region that receives ~600–900 mm of annual precipitation. The water picture here is not scarcity today; it is reliability risk by 2040–2060.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 baseline annual water-stress score for the Kootenay/Columbia River sub-basin: **low** (score category 0–1 on the 0–5 ordinal Aqueduct scale, corresponding to <10% withdrawal-to-availability ratio). The Kootenay River watershed covers 50,298 km² and drains into the Columbia system; baseline surface water availability is high. (WRI Aqueduct 4.0, 2023 release, PCR-GLOBWB 2 model, HydroSHEDS level 6 catchment; provincial context: BC Drought Information Portal, 2024.)

**Supporting facts:**
- The Kootenay River is one of the ten major tributaries of the Columbia River, draining 50,298 km² — one of the largest sub-basins in the Columbia system (Columbia River Basin hydrology, USGS/EC compilation).
- In 2023, the East Kootenay basin experienced 162 days at provincial Drought Level 4 or 5 (where Level 5 = "adverse impacts almost certain"). The West Kootenay basin had 14 days at Drought Level 5 in 2024 (BC Drought Portal, 2024).
- A University of British Columbia study (2020) found that glacier-melt contributions to August runoff in the Canadian Columbia Basin have already passed "peak water" — meaning future meltwater contributions will decline as glacier mass is lost, reducing summer baseflow security.
- North-facing watersheds in the Columbia Basin show lower spring melt rates and longer summer flow periods, making them more resilient sites for community water sourcing (Columbia Basin Water Monitoring Framework, Living Lakes Canada, 2023).
- BC's drought monitoring framework tracks real-time watershed conditions; water licence allocations operate under BC's Water Sustainability Act (2016), which gives priority to existing water-licence holders in times of shortage — new licence applications must demonstrate available unallocated water.
- Climate projections for the Columbia Basin indicate increasing frequency of hot-dry summers, reduced snowpack, and earlier spring runoff, all of which reduce summer water availability even as total annual precipitation remains relatively stable (Columbia Basin Trust climate synthesis, 2022).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Water security for a Kootenay settlement is a design and siting problem, not a resource scarcity problem — today. The key practitioner decisions are: (1) siting relative to a reliable spring or perennial stream, ideally in a north-facing catchment with protected forest cover; (2) water-licence security — BC operates prior-appropriation licensing and a new settlement will need a valid licence for domestic and irrigation use; (3) summer storage — cisterns and earthworks-based capture are essential as late-summer low-flow conditions worsen; (4) exposure to wildfire runoff — post-fire hydrology can severely degrade water quality for 2–5 years. The Kootenays' mountain topography means micro-watershed variation is enormous: one valley can hold a perennial spring while the adjacent slope is bone-dry by August. Community water planning here requires site-specific hydrological assessment, not regional averages.
