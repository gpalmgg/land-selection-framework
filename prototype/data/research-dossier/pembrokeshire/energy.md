# Energy autonomy — Pembrokeshire / West Wales, UK

**Headline finding:** Pembrokeshire is one of the UK's strongest wind-energy resources and a moderate solar resource. The Welsh One Planet Development policy framework *requires* successful applicants to meet 100% of their energy needs from on-site renewable sources — making energy autonomy not a design preference but a legal pre-condition for settlement in the open countryside.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas long-term PVOUT for Pembrokeshire: **~1,000–1,100 kWh/kWp/yr** (Solargis / World Bank Global Solar Atlas, 2020 release, ~1 km resolution). Roughly 55–60% of Alentejo's yield; broadly similar to the rest of southwest UK.

**Supporting facts:**
- Global Wind Atlas (DTU / World Bank, v3.3, 2023) shows mean wind speed at 100 m of **8.5–10.5 m/s** along the Pembrokeshire coast and Preseli ridges — IEC Class I conditions, supporting commercial-scale onshore and offshore wind. Pembrokeshire hosts the Celtic Sea floating offshore wind cluster (Crown Estate Round 5, 2024 award).
- One Planet Development policy (TAN 6, Welsh Government, 2010, with ongoing guidance) explicitly requires applicants to provide all electricity, hot water, and heating from on-site renewables; grid connection is permitted only for export.
- The UK Smart Export Guarantee (SEG, active 2020–) ensures licensed suppliers must pay for excess electricity exported from systems up to 5 MW; export tariffs vary by supplier but provide an income stream for over-built domestic systems.
- Micro-hydro feasibility: small but real; several Preseli streams have sufficient head and flow for sub-10 kW run-of-river systems; permitting via NRW abstraction licence remains the main practical hurdle.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Pembrokeshire is a planning-led energy story as much as a resource one. The OPD framework converts off-grid renewable supply from "expensive choice" to "the way you get permission" — which changes economics dramatically because the alternative is no settlement at all. The technical stack on established OPD sites (Lammas at Glandwr, Tir y Gafel, Brithdir Mawr) is typically: 2–5 kWp PV + 5–10 kW small wind + battery + biomass winter backup + Passivhaus-level building envelope. Solar-only does not work this far north and west; wind is the workhorse. Practitioners considering Pembrokeshire should price small-wind turbine cost, planning permission lead-time, and acoustic impact assessment into the initial feasibility — these are the dominant variables, not PV.
