# Soil & Biodiversity — Pembrokeshire / West Wales, UK

**Headline finding:** Pembrokeshire soils are predominantly shallow podzols and stagnogleys over slates, mudstones, and sandstones — modestly fertile, prone to seasonal waterlogging, with substantial soil-carbon stocks in upland peats and undisturbed grasslands. The Pembrokeshire Coast National Park and surrounding SSSI / Natura 2000 sites form one of the richest coastal biodiversity zones in the UK.

**Key data point (with vintage):** SoilGrids 2.0 (ISRIC, 2020 release) reports topsoil (0–5 cm) soil organic carbon for west Wales in the **~40–80 g/kg** range under permanent pasture and unimproved heath; upland peat patches (Preseli Hills) hold >120 g/kg. Natural Resources Wales' SoNaRR 2025 reports Welsh topsoil carbon levels overall stable but declining under arable/horticultural use.

**Supporting facts:**
- Dominant soil types: Stagnogleys, Brown Earths, and shallow Rankers/Lithosols on coastal slopes; Histosols and Stagnopodzols on upland Preseli Hills; deep loamy soils in some valley floors (Soil Survey of England and Wales / James Hutton classification).
- Wales' Natura 2000 network covers >700,000 ha — 7% of Welsh land area, 36% of territorial waters — including 20 SPAs and 92 SACs (Natural Resources Wales, 2024).
- Pembrokeshire Coast National Park (one of three in Wales) protects a unique mosaic of maritime heath, calcareous grassland, oak woodland, and seabird-colony islands (Skomer, Skokholm); SoNaRR 2025 flags coastal habitats as among the most pressured in Wales.
- Studies of European Natura 2000 sites show organic-carbon content significantly higher in protected grasslands and croplands than in non-protected equivalents (PMC6087710, 2018).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Pembrokeshire's soils are workable but require respect for the underlying climate constraint: cool, wet, often waterlogged. The traditional management response is permanent pasture rather than rotational cropping, with raised beds and improved drainage for horticulture. A practitioner inheriting cleared improved pasture is typically working with depleted SOC (50–70% lower than equivalent undisturbed soils per the SOMNRR analysis) and acidified ground — restoration via mob grazing, agroforestry strips, or conversion to silvopasture realistically rebuilds 1–3 g/kg topsoil SOC per decade in this climate. The Pembrokeshire OPD policy effectively requires applicants to demonstrate a credible food-production plan within the site's ecological constraints, which makes soil due diligence (depth, drainage, pH) part of the planning evidence base rather than an afterthought. SSSI overlap is common — check designations before any drainage or earthworks.
