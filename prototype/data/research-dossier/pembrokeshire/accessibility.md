# Accessibility + Demographics — Pembrokeshire / West Wales, UK

**Headline finding:** Pembrokeshire combines rural sparsity with the UK's general baseline of dense infrastructure — every village has a tertiary hospital within ~80 km, fibre broadband is widely deployed, and London is reachable in a long day. Population is ageing modestly and slightly declining in the rural west; coastal towns receive in-migration from across the UK and (post-Brexit) reduced inflow from continental Europe.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Pembrokeshire population density 2021 Census: **~78 inhabitants/km²** (ONS Census 2021 published 2022); rural ward density is materially lower (10–30/km²). Median age in Pembrokeshire 2021 Census: **~46 years** (vs Wales 42, UK 40). Welsh-language speakers in Pembrokeshire 2021 Census: ~15% (StatsWales 2022).

**Supporting facts:**
- Tertiary hospitals: Withybush General Hospital (Haverfordwest) is the main district general; major regional centre is Glangwili General (Carmarthen) within ~1 h; Morriston (Swansea) and University Hospital of Wales (Cardiff) are tertiary referrals at 1.5–3 h.
- Airports: Cardiff (CWL) 2.5–3 h drive; Bristol (BRS) 3–3.5 h; Birmingham (BHX) 3.5–4 h; no commercial scheduled service in Pembrokeshire itself (St Davids and Haverfordwest small aerodromes).
- Rail: West Wales Line Swansea–Carmarthen–Pembroke Dock / Fishguard Harbour; direct service to London Paddington (5 h to Pembroke).
- Road density: A40 and A48 carry the main east–west traffic; secondary network adequate; no motorway west of Swansea.
- Internet coverage: Fibre rollout in Wales has accelerated through Openreach + Welsh Government Local Broadband Fund; FTTH availability >80% in Pembrokeshire by end-2024 (Ofcom 2024 Connected Nations).
- Electrification: 100% grid; microgeneration regulated under UK Smart Export Guarantee.
- Population trajectory: Pembrokeshire population has been stable-to-slightly-declining since 2011; coastal areas (Tenby, Saundersfoot) see seasonal and retirement in-migration; rural inland wards lose young adults.
- Schools: Welsh-medium and English-medium primary networks both intact; secondary schools in Haverfordwest, Carmarthen, Aberystwyth, Cardigan; higher education at Aberystwyth University, Swansea University.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Pembrokeshire offers an unusually dense rural-UK service baseline — hospitals, schools, fibre and tarmac roads are all present and reliable in ways that are not true in rural Galicia or Transylvania. The cost is a relatively expensive land market (Western Europe pricing band) and a planning environment (Welsh Government + Pembrokeshire County Council + National Park Authority where applicable) that imposes structure on new builds. The Welsh-language dimension is real but quantitatively smaller in Pembrokeshire than in Gwynedd or Carmarthenshire — newcomers integrate in English at low friction in most communities.
