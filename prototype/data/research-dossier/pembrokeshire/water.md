# Water — Pembrokeshire / West Wales, UK

**Headline finding:** Pembrokeshire is water-abundant, with 1,000–1,800 mm annual precipitation, dense small-stream networks, and low WRI Aqueduct baseline water stress. The binding constraints are seasonal-flood management, septic-system performance on heavy clay-shale soils, and the One Planet Development policy's explicit demand that approved sites supply their own water sustainably.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 baseline water stress for Pembrokeshire and west Wales: **<0.2 (low)** across the entire region (Aqueduct 4.0 dataset, 2023 release). Drought risk remains low through 2050 projections.

**Supporting facts:**
- Annual precipitation 1,000–1,200 mm on the coast, 1,400–1,800 mm on inland Preseli Hills (Met Office 1991–2020 climatological normals).
- Bedrock geology is dominated by Cambrian/Ordovician shales and mudstones — fractured-bedrock aquifers with modest borehole yields (typically 0.5–3 L/s) but reliable spring discharge from shallow weathered zones. British Geological Survey (BGS) provides regional aquifer-vulnerability mapping.
- Drinking-water quality: Welsh Water reports the region's mains supply as generally compliant; private supplies (~3% of households nationally, higher in rural Pembrokeshire) face contamination risk from agriculture and septic drainage (DWI Wales annual reports).
- Pembrokeshire's One Planet Development planning policy (TAN 6, Welsh Government) requires applicants to demonstrate sustainable on-site water supply and wastewater treatment as a condition of permission for open-countryside settlement.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Water in Pembrokeshire is closer to Atlantic Ireland than to Mediterranean Iberia — abundant, occasionally too abundant, and only constrained at the household scale by quality and infrastructure rather than quantity. The OPD policy framework actively *requires* practitioners to engineer rainwater harvesting and on-site wastewater treatment to high standards as part of the planning approval pathway — which converts what is elsewhere an optional design discipline into a binding legal condition. Existing OPD smallholdings in the area (Lammas at Glandwr, Brithdir Mawr, Tir y Gafel) provide concrete worked examples: spring + roof catchment + reed-bed wastewater is the canonical stack. Practitioners siting here should engage Pembrokeshire Coast National Park Authority and the OPD planning policy framework early — they make the difference between a buildable parcel and a non-starter.
