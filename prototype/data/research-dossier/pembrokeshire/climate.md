# Climate — Pembrokeshire / West Wales, UK

**Headline finding:** West Wales is projected to warm by roughly +1.2–1.8°C in mean annual temperature by mid-century under SSP2-4.5 — slightly more than Atlantic Ireland, slightly less than central England. The dominant trajectory is wetter winters, drier summers, and a sharp increase in intense rainfall events. Practitioners site here for a relatively stable maritime climate, not for stability proof.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Mean annual temperature projection 2041–2060 under SSP2-4.5 for west Wales: **~11.5–12.5°C** (UKCP18 probabilistic projections, Met Office, accessed via World Bank CCKP CMIP6, 2024; consistent with Met Office UKCP18 land projections, updated 2023).

**Supporting facts:**
- Current mean annual temperature for Pembrokeshire (coastal) is ~10.5–11.0°C, with mild winters (mean Jan minimum 3–4°C) and moderate summers (mean July maximum 19–21°C) (Met Office 1991–2020 normals).
- UKCP18 projects winter precipitation increases of 5–15% by 2050 and summer precipitation decreases of ~15–25% under SSP2-4.5 / RCP 4.5 for southwest UK.
- Hourly rainfall extremes above 20 mm/h projected to become roughly 4× more frequent by the 2070s under high emissions, with flash-flood implications (PMC9992391, 2023).
- Wind regime: Pembrokeshire's exposed Atlantic coast sees mean wind speed at 10 m of 5.5–7.5 m/s; mean wind expected to decrease slightly through 2050, but storm-event intensity increases (Met Office, 2024).

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
West Wales sits in a similar climate band to Atlantic Ireland but with marginally more continental influence — slightly warmer summers, slightly more variable winters. The headline practitioner-relevant story for 2050 is *more intense, less predictable* rainfall: surface flooding, agricultural soil saturation, and overwhelmed septic systems are the realistic stressors, not heat. The UK's One Planet Development policy explicitly requires that approved settlements adapt their building and growing strategies to projected climate (TAN 6 guidance, Welsh Government), so the trajectory matters not only physically but also in planning permission. AMOC-shift sensitivity is similar to Atlantic Ireland — relevant but secondary tail risk. Practitioners can plausibly design for a slightly-warmer continuation of current conditions plus stormier weather.
