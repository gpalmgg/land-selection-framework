# Legal + Economic — Pembrokeshire / West Wales, UK

**Headline finding:** The UK places no nationality restriction on land ownership — non-EU and EU citizens may buy freehold rural land on equal terms with no residency or visa precondition. Wales offers the One Planet Development planning route (TAN 6) which is unique in Europe for legalising low-impact ecological smallholdings on agricultural land. Cost-per-acre in Wales rose sharply in 2024; Pembrokeshire averages typical Welsh figures but with strong sub-market variation.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Average farmland value in Wales (and England) early 2024: **£9,152/acre** (≈£22,605/ha / ≈€26,800/ha at Q1 2024 GBP/EUR); marginal rise to **£9,164/acre** entering 2025 (Knight Frank Farmland Index Q4 2024). Strong cropping land in Wales 2024 routinely exceeded **£10,000/acre**; Pembrokeshire bare-land sales 2024 typically **£8,000–£11,000/acre** for mixed/grass land, with prime parcels (Vale of Clwyd-like quality) reaching £20,000+/acre — Farmers Weekly *Farmland in your area 2024: Wales*. Stamp Duty Land Tax (Land Transaction Tax in Wales) on non-residential land begins at 0% to £225k, rising to 6% over £1m (LTT 2024 bands).

**Supporting facts:**
- No nationality restriction on land ownership in any part of the UK. Non-UK buyers acquire freehold or leasehold on the same legal terms as UK buyers (Law of Property Act 1925; Welsh equivalents devolved to Land Transaction Tax 2018).
- Land Transaction Tax (LTT, the Welsh successor to Stamp Duty) applies — non-residential property rates 2024: 0%/1%/5%/6% with thresholds at £225k/£250k/£1m (Welsh Revenue Authority 2024).
- Post-Brexit (transition end 31 Dec 2020) no impact on freehold ownership by EU citizens; visa-residency separated from land-ownership rights — EU buyers may purchase but must obtain visa (skilled worker, family, investor) for residency.
- One Planet Development (TAN 6, Welsh Government policy 2009–2024) — unique planning route allowing low-impact smallholdings on open-countryside land; requires detailed management plan, ecological footprint calculations, and 5-year subsistence trajectory. Iconic example: Lammas (Pembrokeshire).
- Pembrokeshire Coast National Park — covers ~620 km² of the coast; planning policy applies under the Park Authority not the County Council. National Park planning is materially more restrictive than the rest of the county.
- IHT Agricultural Property Relief was capped at £1m of value in the November 2024 UK Budget (effective April 2026) — material for family-succession planning.
- Collective-ownership structures: Community Land Trust (Housing and Regeneration Act 2008), Community Benefit Society (Co-operative and Community Benefit Societies Act 2014), Company Limited by Guarantee, Charitable Incorporated Organisation.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The Pembrokeshire legal-economic picture combines truly open land-ownership rules with the highly specific One Planet Development planning option. OPD is the most practitioner-friendly route in the UK for new ecological smallholdings but requires substantial documentation and a willingness to commit to the 5-year subsistence trajectory. Non-UK buyers should note: buying land is easy; *living on it* requires a visa, and most agricultural-purpose visas do not exist (Wales is open for ownership but not for non-UK self-settlement without a visa route). Inside Pembrokeshire Coast National Park, virtually all development applications face additional scrutiny — practitioners typically target land just outside the park boundary.
