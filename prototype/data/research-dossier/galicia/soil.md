# Soil & Biodiversity — Galicia, Spain

**Headline finding:** Galicia carries some of the highest soil organic carbon stocks in southern Europe under its forest and scrub cover, supported by udic (moist-temperate) moisture regimes and acidic granite/schist parent material. Agricultural soils are markedly poorer; eucalyptus plantations have measurably depleted SOC versus native Atlantic oak. Land selection here is a story about land-use history more than baseline geology.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Forest and scrub soils across Galicia and western Asturias under udic moisture regimes typically hold **>7% soil organic carbon in the topsoil**, with average stocks of **~260 t C/ha in the 0–30 cm layer** (Calvo de Anta et al., *Soil Carbon in Northern Spain*, Spanish Journal of Soil Science, 2015). SoilGrids 2.0 reports topsoil SOC in the 40–80+ g/kg band for native-forest areas (ISRIC, 2020 release).

**Supporting facts:**
- Dominant soil types: Cambisols, Umbrisols, and Leptosols over schist, granite, and slate parent materials; soils are acidic (pH 4.5–5.5), generally well-drained but locally shallow on upland ridges (European Soil Data Centre, 2020).
- Agricultural and cleared soils across the region show SOC stocks below 100 t/ha (0–30 cm) — a >2× depletion versus intact forest soils on equivalent parent material (Calvo de Anta et al., 2015).
- Galicia hosts ~12% of its surface in Natura 2000 sites including the Fragas do Eume Atlantic oak forest, Os Ancares, O Courel, and Costa da Morte coastal habitats (Fundación Biodiversidad, 2023).
- Eucalyptus plantations now cover ~28% of Galicia's forest area (Xunta de Galicia / IFN forest inventory, 2022) and are linked to reduced understorey biodiversity, increased fire risk, and SOC depletion versus native broadleaf systems.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
For a regenerative settlement, the soil reality in Galicia hinges on what was on the land for the last 50 years. A parcel coming out of mature native oak/chestnut/scrub is a soil-carbon gift. A parcel coming out of two rotations of eucalyptus is a long rebuild — acidified, organic-matter-depleted, and often compacted. Practitioners should treat pre-purchase soil sampling (SOC, pH, depth) and a clear-eyed reading of land-use history as binding due diligence, not optional. The good news: udic climate plus acidic but biologically active parent material means SOC rebuilds *faster* here than in Mediterranean Iberia — measured gains of 5–10 t C/ha per decade are realistic under managed silvopasture or food-forest conversion.
