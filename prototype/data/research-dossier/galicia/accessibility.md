# Accessibility + Demographics — Galicia, Spain

**Headline finding:** Galicia is paradoxically dense and depopulating — the third-most-rural NUTS-II region of Spain by aldea count (>30,000 inhabited settlements) but losing inland population while coastal A-Coruña/Pontevedra hold steady. Service access is good by Atlantic-Europe rural standards (Santiago, A Coruña, Vigo each within 1–2 h of most rural points), but interior comarcas of Ourense and Lugo are among Spain's most depopulated.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Galicia population density 2023: **~91 inhabitants/km²** overall (close to Spain national 95/km²) — IGE Galicia / INE 2023. Median age in Galicia 2023: **~49.5 years** (Spain's oldest NUTS-II region by median age; INE 2023). Interior Ourense province has lost **~15% of population since 2000** (IGE; UN-classified as one of Europe's most severely depopulating regions).

**Supporting facts:**
- Tertiary hospitals: Complexo Hospitalario Universitario A Coruña (CHUAC), Santiago (CHUS), Vigo (CHUVI), Lugo (HULA), Ourense (CHUO). Rural settlements typically within 30–60 km of one of these by road.
- Airports: Santiago–Lavacolla (SCQ, main intercontinental hub), A Coruña (LCG), Vigo (VGO). Porto Airport (OPO) is the de-facto budget gateway for southern Galicia — 1.5 h from Vigo. Madrid is 4 h by AVE high-speed rail from Santiago since 2021.
- Road density: AP-9/A-6/A-52 motorway spine; secondary network dense by Spanish standards. Inland Ourense/Lugo back-roads require 4×4 in winter for some parishes.
- Internet coverage: 4G near-universal in populated areas; FTTH deployment has been aggressive — Galicia exceeded 80% household fibre coverage by end-2023 (Xunta DigitalGAL programme). Remote aldeas still rely on fixed-wireless and Starlink.
- Electrification: 100% grid; Galicia is a net electricity exporter (wind + hydro); microgeneration regulated under Spanish RD 244/2019 with retroactive favourable amendments through 2023.
- Population trajectory: net negative for interior; positive net migration into coastal towns and the *neorrural* return-to-village movement is statistically marginal but locally visible. Saaremaa-style ageing dynamic without comparable inbound retiree market.
- Schools: rural CEIP school closures continued through 2024; many comarcas operate consolidated *agrupación de centros rurais* — bus runs are long for outlying families.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Galicia's accessibility geography is bimodal: the coastal arc (A Coruña–Santiago–Vigo) functions as a normal Western-European service belt, while interior Ourense/Lugo functions as a depopulation frontier. Many of the cheapest land opportunities are in the second zone, where school closures and hospital distance materially affect long-term family viability. For practitioners with school-age children, the comarcas around Santiago and the Rías Baixas combine reasonable land prices with intact service infrastructure. Fibre is real — confirm at parish level via *DigitalGAL* coverage map.
