# Political Stability — Galicia, Spain

**Headline finding:** Galicia sits inside one of the EU's most institutionally stable democracies, with regional politics dominated for over a decade by a stable centre-right Xunta. Conflict events are essentially nil; the practical stability risk is national-level political volatility (recurring Madrid coalition fragility, Catalan-amnesty disputes) which rarely projects into Galicia's daily governance.

**Key data point (with vintage):** V-Dem Electoral Democracy Index for Spain 2023: **0.83** (V-Dem v14, March 2024 release). UCDP conflict events recorded in Galicia 2019–2024: **zero** (UCDP GED v24.1). INFORM Risk 2024 for Spain: **2.6/10** (low risk; INFORM 2024 release).

**Supporting facts:**
- Galicia is one of Spain's 17 autonomous communities under the 1978 Constitution and the 1981 Statute of Autonomy. Devolved powers include rural development, language, health, and territorial planning.
- The Partido Popular de Galicia (PPdeG) has held the Xunta presidency continuously since 2009 (Núñez Feijóo, then Alfonso Rueda from 2022). February 2024 regional elections returned PPdeG with absolute majority — locally read as continuity rather than realignment.
- ETA's dissolution (2018) and the country's exit from the post-2017 Catalan crisis acute phase have lowered Spain's overall political-temperature significantly. Galicia was largely insulated from both.
- Civil-society response capacity in Galicia is dense: parish-level community structures (*comunidades de montes vecinales en man común* — common land collectives) function as both legal entities and de-facto coordination layers in rural Galicia.
- Government continuity outlook: stable Xunta through 2028; national Sánchez government (PSOE–Sumar coalition since November 2023) remains fragile but does not threaten regional administration.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
For 50-year community projects, the relevant Galician stability story is institutional consistency under a single regional party, not headline national politics. The single most distinct local layer — *montes vecinales en man común* (community-held commons covering ~600,000 ha of Galicia) — is a stability-enabling institution that has survived dictatorship, EU accession, and depopulation. Foreign-led projects should treat parish councils as the practical decision-layer for local integration.
