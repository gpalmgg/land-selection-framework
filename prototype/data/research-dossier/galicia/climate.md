# Climate — Galicia, Spain

**Headline finding:** Galicia's Atlantic-temperate climate is projected to warm more modestly than the Iberian interior — roughly +1.5–2.0°C mean annual temperature by mid-century under SSP2-4.5 — while precipitation timing shifts toward shorter, more intense events. Practitioners here are siting in one of Iberia's climate refugia, not on a stable baseline.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Mean annual temperature projection 2041–2060 under SSP2-4.5 for northwestern Iberia: **~14.5–15.5°C** (WorldClim CMIP6 v2.1 ensemble, 2024; consistent with AEMET's regional downscaling for the Atlantic Iberian zone, 2023).

**Supporting facts:**
- Current mean annual temperature for coastal Galicia is ~13.5°C; interior valleys (Ourense) ~14°C, with summer maxima rarely exceeding 30°C on the coast (AEMET climatological normals 1991–2020).
- A 2025 Land journal study analysing 1971–2022 Spanish climate data projects shifts in Köppen classification across northern Spain by 2050, with parts of inland Galicia trending Csb→Csa under intermediate scenarios (MDPI Land 14:85, 2025).
- Mean annual precipitation 1,100–1,800 mm depending on elevation and aspect; AEMET projects a 5–10% reduction in annual totals by 2050 with stronger seasonal redistribution toward winter (AEMET Climate Services, 2023).
- Galicia is identified as a partial climate refugium in IPCC AR6 WGII Mediterranean-region assessments — buffered against extreme heat by Atlantic maritime influence, though still warming.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Galicia is one of the few European regions where the practitioner's calculus shifts from "how do I cope with worsening climate" toward "how do I design with relatively favourable conditions that still drift." Summer heat is unlikely to become a primary stress this century; the bigger trajectory questions are: more intense winter storms, drought stress on shallow-rooted summer crops, and altered fungal/forest-pathogen pressure under warmer-wetter winters. AMOC-shift sensitivity is relevant — Galicia depends on Gulf Stream-warmed Atlantic air, and a substantial weakening could cool the region against the global trend. Practitioners should pencil this as a low-probability, high-impact tail risk worth scenario-planning, not a forecast.
