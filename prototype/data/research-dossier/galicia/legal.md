# Legal + Economic — Galicia, Spain

**Headline finding:** Spain places no nationality restriction on rural-land ownership; non-EU buyers acquire on the same legal terms as EU citizens, with only an NIE/foreign-identification number and (for >€500k transactions) a Banco de España declaration. Cost-per-hectare in Galicia is well below Spain's national average for general agricultural land, but the *minifundio* (fragmented smallholding) tradition means assembling a contiguous viable parcel is the real cost driver. The Spain Golden Visa programme ended April 2025; alternative residency routes remain.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Agricultural land cost-per-hectare in Galicia 2024 — typical dry pasture/rough land **€3,000–€8,000/ha**; *prado/labradío* productive parcels **€8,000–€18,000/ha**; Ribeira Sacra and Rías Baixas vineyard land **€40,000–€80,000+/ha** (MAPA *Encuesta de Precios de la Tierra* 2024 and Vinetur 2024). Spain national agricultural mean 2024: **€10,248/ha** (+2.8% YoY current prices) — MAPA 2024, published 2025. Galician dry vineyard average 2024: **€60,473/ha** (Vinetur 2024 cross-region series).

**Supporting facts:**
- Foreign ownership of rural land in Spain: open, no nationality restriction — Ley 19/2003 (foreign-investment regime), Real Decreto Legislativo 1/2010 (capital companies law).
- Required for purchase by non-residents: NIE (Número de Identidad de Extranjero) via Spanish consulate or local police; notarial deed (escritura pública) before a Spanish notary; registration with Registradores (Spanish Land Registry). Transactions >€500k must be reported to Banco de España for statistical purposes (not approval).
- Golden Visa (Ley 14/2013) was repealed for new applications April 2025; remaining residency pathways include the non-lucrative visa (passive income), the digital-nomad visa (Ley 28/2022), and entrepreneurial visa.
- Collective-ownership structures: Cooperativa (Ley 27/1999 / autonomous Cooperativas de Galicia Ley 5/1998), Sociedad Agraria de Transformación (SAT) for agricultural production cooperatives, and the regionally distinctive *comunidade de montes veciñais en man común* (Lei 13/1989 da Comunidade Autónoma de Galicia) — a uniquely Galician collective-ownership category with parish-level membership.
- Construction permits on rural land are governed by Xunta-level urbanism law (Lei 2/2016 do solo de Galicia) plus municipal *Plan Xeral de Ordenación Municipal* — *solo rústico de protección agropecuaria* and *forestal* heavily restrict residential construction.
- Land registry double-layer: *Catastro* (fiscal cadastre, Ministerio de Hacienda) and *Registro de la Propiedad* (legal registry, Registradores). They frequently disagree; reconciliation is the buyer's problem.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The defining Galician legal-economic feature is the *minifundio* — many parcels are <0.5 ha and held by family heirs scattered across the diaspora. Assembling a 10–20 ha viable parcel often requires negotiating with 30+ owners, several of whom may live in Buenos Aires or Caracas. Cheap headline prices mask high transaction friction and uncertainty about title. Buyers should budget 12–24 months for clean-title assembly and budget legal fees accordingly. The *comunidades de montes* are not a workaround — membership is hereditary by parish census, not purchasable.
