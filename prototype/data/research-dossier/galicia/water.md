# Water — Galicia, Spain

**Headline finding:** Galicia is water-abundant by Iberian standards, with 1,100–1,800 mm of annual precipitation and a dense network of small rivers, springs, and shallow hard-rock aquifers. WRI Aqueduct flags most of the region as "low to medium" baseline water stress; the binding constraints are summer-low-flow management and aquifer pollution, not absolute scarcity.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 baseline water stress for most of Galicia: **<0.2 (low)** in coastal and northern zones, rising to 0.2–0.4 (low-medium) in interior Ourense valleys (Aqueduct 4.0 dataset, 2023 release).

**Supporting facts:**
- Mean annual precipitation ranges from ~1,133 mm in southern interior to ~1,539 mm in the wetter Atlantic-facing slopes (Galician hydrological studies, 2022).
- Aquifer system is dominated by weathered and fractured schists/granites — high transmissivity in the upper few metres, limited storage in deeper bedrock; community-owned rural water supplies historically tap shallow boreholes and springs (Springer Environmental Earth Sciences, 2022).
- Spain's Third Cycle Hydrological Plans for Galicia (2022–2027) tighten abstraction controls and prioritise summer ecological flow protection (Consejo de Ministros, January 2023).
- Drought-projection direction for Galicia: AEMET projects summer-precipitation reductions of 10–20% by mid-century with concentration of rainfall into shorter winter events — implication is shorter recharge windows, longer summer drawdown.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
For a settlement designed around water resilience, Galicia offers the rare Iberian combination of generous annual budget plus *intra-annual* mismatch: it rains a lot, but largely between October and April. Roof rainwater harvesting is highly productive here — a 200 m² roof captures roughly 220,000–300,000 L/year — but storage sizing must bridge a 3–4 month dry summer. Hard-rock aquifers yield modestly (typically 0.5–3 L/s from small boreholes) and can be locally polluted by intensive eucalyptus and pig-farming areas, so water-source siting matters as much as quantity. Galicia's community-water tradition (*traídas vecinales*) is a culturally established and legally recognised model that maps directly onto cohousing and ecovillage water-governance designs.
