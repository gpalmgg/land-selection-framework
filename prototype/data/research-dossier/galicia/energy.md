# Energy autonomy — Galicia, Spain

**Headline finding:** Galicia's energy profile is wind-dominated, not solar — it is one of Europe's strongest onshore wind resources, with renewables already supplying ~81% of regional installed capacity. Micro-hydro is genuinely feasible on perennial Atlantic streams; PV is moderate-to-good but secondary to wind for high-latitude applications.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas long-term PVOUT for Galicia: **~1,200–1,350 kWh/kWp/yr**, depending on aspect and cloud cover (Solargis / World Bank Global Solar Atlas, 2020 release, ~1 km resolution). Substantially lower than southern Iberia but competitive with central Europe.

**Supporting facts:**
- Global Wind Atlas (DTU / World Bank, v3.3, 2023) shows mean wind speed at 100 m of **7.5–10+ m/s** along Galician coast and ridgelines — a Class 4–5 resource, among the strongest in continental Europe.
- Wind power represents 41% of Galicia's total installed electricity capacity, with overall renewables at 81.1% of installed capacity (Red Eléctrica de España system reports, 2024).
- Micro-hydro feasibility is strong: dense Atlantic-stream network, year-round flow on most main rivers, established small-hydro tradition (Galicia has hundreds of historical *muíños* / mills, many sites still usable for run-of-river systems up to 100 kW). Xunta de Galicia INEGA promotes small-hydro within the IDAE renewable energy framework.
- Spain's *autoconsumo* legislation (Real Decreto 244/2019) and the *comunidades energéticas* framework permit collective self-consumption and energy communities — directly applicable to settlement-scale renewable integration.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Galicia inverts the southern-European energy stack. A practitioner here can plausibly site a small-wind plus PV plus micro-hydro hybrid that smooths seasonal mismatch: winter wind is strong when PV is weak, and stream flow is most reliable from autumn through spring. The same Atlantic cloud cover that depresses PV yield depresses solar-thermal heating too, so domestic hot water is typically met by heat-pumps (running on cheap winter wind/hydro) or biomass rather than solar collectors. Permitting for small wind under 100 kW is bureaucratically slow but legally viable; coastal-ridge siting attracts community opposition due to dense rural population — interior valleys are more permissive. The grid is robust and bidirectional, so net export under *autoconsumo* is a real income stream.
