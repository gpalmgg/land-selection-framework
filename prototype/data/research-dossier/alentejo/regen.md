# Regenerative Ecosystem — Alentejo, Portugal

**Headline finding:** Alentejo hosts Europe's most internationally visible regen community (Tamera, since 1995) and a thickening layer of permaculture, agroforestry and water-retention practitioners. The funding stack combines EU CAP/PEPAC-Portugal Rural Development with active Portuguese impact-investment vehicles — but most regen funding still flows through national channels, not Alentejo-specific funds.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Ecovillage Network (GEN Europe) listed Portugal projects within ~150 km of central Alentejo (Tamera, Awakening Culture, Quinta das Moitas, Aldeia das Amoreiras, Vale da Lama, Mount of Oaks, others): **8 active entries** (ecovillage.org directory, accessed 2025). Tamera alone: 154 ha, ~160 residents, 20,000+ trees planted (Tamera site & Wikipedia, 2024).

**Supporting facts:**
- Tamera, Colos (Odemira municipality), founded 1995 — Water Retention Landscape recognised in EEA Climate-ADAPT case studies.
- Aldeia das Amoreiras (Odemira) — permaculture demonstration site, hosts PDC trainings via Bons Padroais collective.
- Vale da Lama (near Lagos, edge of Alentejo) — permaculture educational farm, Schumacher College partner.
- Permaculture Portugal (Associação Permacultura Portugal) maintains national regen network with strong Alentejo membership.
- Active 2024–2026 funder/programme list (verified): **PDR2030 / PEPAC-Portugal** (CAP Strategic Plan 2023–2027, agro-ecology measures, ~€7.3 bn envelope), **Fundo Ambiental** (Ministry of Environment grants for ecological restoration, annual calls), **Maze Impact** (Lisbon-based impact VC, agri/regen track), **Portugal Inovação Social** (social-innovation fund, co-finances community projects).
- Cork-oak agroforestry (montado) — UNESCO-recognised cultural landscape covering ~700,000 ha in Alentejo; provides a working regen model with multi-century stewardship traditions.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Tamera's longevity (30 years) makes Alentejo a uniquely well-mapped regen jurisdiction for outsiders — its experience with Portuguese permits, water access, and municipal relationships is partially documented in peer-reviewed studies. The danger is over-anchoring on Tamera; smaller projects report easier integration with local Portuguese farming neighbours than highly visible communities. EU PEPAC funding is highly competitive — successful applicants typically partner with an existing Portuguese legal entity.
