# Energy autonomy — Alentejo, Portugal

**Headline finding:** Alentejo is among the strongest solar-PV resources in Europe, with PVOUT figures rivalled only by parts of southern Spain and Sicily. Wind is moderate, micro-hydro is constrained by the dry-summer hydrological regime, and Portugal's net-metering (UPAC) and self-consumption rules favour on-site PV with battery storage.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Global Solar Atlas long-term average photovoltaic power output (PVOUT) for inland Alentejo: **~1,830–1,900 kWh/kWp/yr** (Solargis / World Bank Global Solar Atlas, 2020 release; resolution ~1 km). This places Alentejo in the top decile of European solar resource.

**Supporting facts:**
- Global Wind Atlas (DTU / World Bank, v3.3, 2023) shows mean wind speed at 100 m of ~5.5–7.0 m/s across central Alentejo — a moderate Class 3 resource; better near coastal ridges (Sines, Odemira) and southern Serra de Monchique foothills.
- Direct normal irradiation (DNI) ~2,100–2,300 kWh/m²/yr, supporting concentrated-solar applications as well as PV (Global Solar Atlas, 2020).
- Portugal's regulatory regime (Decreto-Lei 162/2019, updated 2022) enables individual and collective self-consumption (UPAC and CER), simplified licensing for systems up to 700 kW, and energy-community models — directly applicable to small-scale settlement scenarios.
- Micro-hydro is feasible only on the few perennial tributaries (Guadiana, Sado headwaters) and is constrained by 5–6 month low-flow summers; uncertain — most regional studies frame Alentejo as a solar-dominated resource portfolio.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
A 5–10 kWp rooftop PV system in Alentejo plausibly generates ~9,000–18,000 kWh/year — typically more than a well-designed household needs, even before battery storage. The practical bottleneck is not generation but matching daily/seasonal demand: summer over-production aligns with peak cooling load (favourable), while winter heating demand falls during the lowest-yield months (less favourable). Portugal's CER (community energy) legislation makes shared storage and inter-household balancing legally straightforward, which is an underappreciated advantage relative to e.g. Italy or France. For settlements, PV + lithium-iron-phosphate storage + a small biomass or LPG winter backup is the canonical stack.
