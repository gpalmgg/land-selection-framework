# Water — Alentejo, Portugal

**Headline finding:** Alentejo sits in WRI Aqueduct's "extremely high" baseline water-stress band for much of its territory, and the projected 2050 trajectory under business-as-usual deepens that stress. Settlement here is viable only with deliberate rainwater harvesting, deep storage, and Mediterranean-adapted hydrological design — not with assumptions of perennial flow.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 baseline water stress for the Alentejo region: **0.6–0.8 (high to extremely high)**, with the projected 2050 score under the BAU/SSP3-RCP7.0 scenario rising into the "extremely high" band (>0.8) across much of the southern interior (Aqueduct 4.0 dataset, 2023 release).

**Supporting facts:**
- Annual precipitation ranges roughly 500–650 mm, concentrated in October–April; summer months are functionally arid (IPMA climatological normals, 1991–2020).
- Groundwater abstraction across Alentejo has accelerated with the post-Alqueva irrigation expansion; EASAC's groundwater report for southern EU member states (2020) flags the region as facing intensifying competition between irrigation and ecosystem needs.
- Multi-decadal soil-water-budget modelling for southern Portugal projects a ~15–25% reduction in plant-available water by 2050 under SSP2-4.5 (Pereira et al., *Journal of Water and Climate Change*, 2024).
- The Alqueva reservoir (250 km², full pool, completed 2002) provides the dominant surface-water buffer for central Alentejo but redistributes water rather than creating new supply.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Water is the binding constraint on Alentejo settlement. The headline temperature trajectory matters less than the question: can a project sit through August on roof-captured winter rain plus a borehole? Most existing rural buildings cannot. A practitioner here designs from the rainwater-harvest equation backward — roof-catchment area, cistern volume sized for ~6 months of summer demand, swales and keyline earthworks to keep what does fall in the landscape. Boreholes work but levels are dropping in the Aqueduct-flagged sub-basins. Greywater recycling and dry-toilet systems shift from "nice" to load-bearing.
