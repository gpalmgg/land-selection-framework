# Legal + Economic — Alentejo, Portugal

**Headline finding:** Portugal's land regime is open to non-EU buyers with no nationality restriction on rural-property purchase — but the residency landscape changed materially in October 2023 when the Golden Visa real-estate route was removed. Cost-per-hectare in Alentejo remains among Western Europe's most affordable; trajectory is firmly upward as wine, olive, almond and regen-investment demand intensifies.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Agricultural land cost-per-hectare in Alentejo 2024–2025: **€8,000–€20,000/ha** for typical dry/extensive parcels; **€25,000–€45,000/ha** for irrigated land within or near the Alqueva scheme (Idealista Alentejo listings, Q4 2024–Q1 2025; CBRE Property Handbook 2024; Savills Global Farmland September 2024). Portugal national arable mean per Eurostat 2024 release: **€11,827/ha** weighted, with significant regional spread. *Note vintage uncertainty: high-end irrigated comparables are 2024; low-end marginal-land figures draw on 2023 listings adjusted forward.*

**Supporting facts:**
- Portugal allows foreign ownership of rural land with no nationality restriction; foundational instrument Decreto-Lei 555/99 (Regime Jurídico da Urbanização e Edificação, consolidated 2024).
- Collective-ownership structures recognised: Cooperativa Agrícola (Decreto-Lei 335/99), Associação (Code Civil articles 157+), or SCI/Sociedade Civil for asset-pooling.
- The Golden Visa programme was restructured October 2023 (Lei 56/2023 / "Mais Habitação"): real-estate investment route removed; remaining routes include €500k venture-capital fund investment, €250k cultural/heritage contribution, or scientific-research contribution. Land purchase itself does not grant residency.
- Non-EU buyers receive a Portuguese fiscal number (NIF) via a fiscal representative; land purchase is unaffected.
- D7 (passive-income), D8 (digital nomad), and D2 (entrepreneurial) visa routes remain available as alternative residency paths and do permit landownership.
- Construction permits on rural land run through municipal câmaras (Odemira, Beja, Évora, Mértola most common Alentejo offices); RAN/REN protected zones materially restrict construction on agricultural and ecological reserves — verify before purchase.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The biggest practitioner trap in Alentejo is assuming a land purchase implies water rights and buildable area. Water in Alqueva-served areas is contractually allocated; outside Alqueva, boreholes require ARH (regional water authority) authorisation. RAN-classified parcels can be bought freely but rarely permit residential construction — many a foreign buyer has paid for a farm only to discover their house plans are vetoed. Non-EU buyers face no land-purchase friction but should budget for slower SEF/AIMA residency processing (12–18 months as of 2024).
