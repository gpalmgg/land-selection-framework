# Accessibility + Demographics — Alentejo, Portugal

**Headline finding:** Alentejo is Portugal's emptiest region — population density roughly one-eighth the national average, ageing rapidly, with persistent rural depopulation offset by inbound European retirees and lifestyle migrants. Access to services is real but uneven: regional hospitals and Lisbon are within practical reach, but rural-municipality healthcare is thin and small schools continue to close.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Alentejo population density 2023: **~22 inhabitants/km²** (vs Portugal national ~111/km²) — INE Portugal, Censos 2021 updated 2023 estimate. Median age in Alentejo 2023: **~50 years** (oldest of Portugal's NUTS-II regions; Pordata 2023). Population trajectory 2011–2021: **−6.9%** for Alentejo region overall (INE Censos 2021).

**Supporting facts:**
- Nearest tertiary hospital: Hospital do Espírito Santo (Évora) and Hospital José Joaquim Fernandes (Beja); typical settlement-to-hospital distance 30–80 km depending on parish.
- Airports: Lisbon Airport (LIS) within 1.5–2.5 h drive from most of Alentejo; Faro Airport (FAO) 1.5–2 h from southern Alentejo; Évora regional airport (limited service).
- Road density: lower than national average but A2/IP2/IP8 trunk corridors cover north–south and east–west axes adequately; many concelhos require gravel/track access to outlying parcels.
- Internet coverage: 4G near-universal in populated areas; fibre (FTTH) deployment 2024 extends to most concelho seats but remains spotty in outlying freguesias — Starlink and fixed-wireless are common workarounds. ANACOM 2024 reported >85% of households with fixed broadband.
- Electrification: ~100% grid coverage; off-grid solar is widely adopted on rural properties and supported by APREN-tracked microgeneration regime.
- Demographic trend: inbound migration of EU retirees, French/German/Dutch/Belgian lifestyle migrants and digital nomads has partially offset rural decline since 2018, especially in Alto and Baixo Alentejo coastal strip.
- Schools: rural schools concentrated in concelho seats; remote families often homeschool (legally regulated by Direção-Geral da Educação) or rely on Évora/Beja boarding options.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Alentejo offers a near-unique combination in Western Europe: cheap, low-density land within a 2-hour drive of a capital-city airport. The flip side is that emptiness shapes daily logistics — a single tertiary hospital, sparse rural healthcare, and a school network on a long-term contraction trajectory. Community projects with families should map school options before settling on a concelho; medical considerations argue for proximity to Évora, Beja or Odemira. Internet quality varies by parish — confirm with neighbours, not maps.
