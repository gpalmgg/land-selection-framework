# Stability — Northern New Mexico (Taos area), United States

**Headline finding:** Northern New Mexico registers zero armed-conflict events in the UCDP Georeferenced Event Dataset (GED) — consistent with the entire contiguous United States and Canada, which record no state-based, non-state, or one-sided organised-violence events in the UCDP dataset at any spatial resolution. The region does experience ordinary civil crime, and there are documented tensions around resource access (water rights disputes, land-grant heritage conflicts, and occasional protest activity), but none of these rise to the threshold of organised armed violence as defined by UCDP. Political stability at federal, state, and county level is stable under US constitutional governance, with standard democratic institutional continuity.

**Key data point (with vintage):** UCDP GED v24.1 (Uppsala Conflict Data Program, 2024 release) armed-conflict events within ~200 km of Taos, NM (36.4°N, −105.6°W): **0 events** (consistent with all of North America above the Mexico border; UCDP tracks organised violence between armed groups or by armed actors against civilians — domestic crime is excluded). Confidence: **high**. This is the expected and correct value for the continental United States.

**Supporting facts:**
- UCDP GED v24.1 covers 1989–2023 with georeferenced event data on state-based conflicts, non-state conflicts, and one-sided violence. The contiguous United States and Canada record zero qualifying events across this entire period.
- New Mexico state crime indices: the state historically ranks above the US median for property crime and violent crime at the state level, but at the county level, Taos County's crime rates are more moderate than Albuquerque or cities in the southern Rio Grande corridor. These are civil-law matters, not armed-conflict risk.
- Land-grant heritage: northern New Mexico contains numerous historic Spanish and Mexican land-grant communities with unresolved title disputes dating to the 19th century; the Tierra Amarilla courthouse raid of 1967 (Reies Tijerina) is the closest historical precedent to organised political violence in the region, and no comparable events have occurred since.
- US political risk: standard federal-level policy volatility applies (election cycles, federal land management changes, water compact enforcement), but none constitute armed-conflict risk within the UCDP definition.
- Taos Pueblo, a UNESCO World Heritage Site and sovereign tribal nation, maintains independent governance within its reservation boundaries; relations with surrounding communities are stable and governed by federal Indian law, tribal jurisdiction, and longstanding co-existence agreements.
- The nearest international border (Mexico) is approximately 560 km south of Taos; border-related security dynamics do not materially affect northern New Mexico.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The zero conflict score is correct and honest, not a gap. The relevant stability risks for a long-horizon regenerative settlement in northern New Mexico are not geopolitical but institutional and resource-based: water compact enforcement (New Mexico is currently in debt to Texas under the Rio Grande Compact and faces potential federal curtailment orders), federal land-management changes (large portions of Taos County are US Forest Service or Bureau of Land Management land, whose policies shift with administrations), and the complexity of land-grant title history (some parcels carry clouded title from 19th-century surveying disputes that resurface in boundary litigation). These are real risks for project planning and legal due diligence, but they belong in the legal and water dimensions of this framework, not in the conflict dimension. The stability score here is the simplest entry in the dossier: the United States poses no armed-conflict risk by any defensible international standard.
