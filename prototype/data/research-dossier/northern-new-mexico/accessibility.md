# Accessibility — Northern New Mexico (Taos area), United States

**Headline finding:** Taos County is a sparsely populated, predominantly rural mountain county with a population density of approximately 6 persons/km² — well within the low end of the framework's range and roughly comparable to rural European comparators like rural Transylvania or West Cork. The town of Taos (pop. ~6,000) provides a functional small-city service base: hospital, university satellite campus, airport (small regional), broadband connectivity, and a substantial arts/tourism economy. The nearest major city is Santa Fe (~90 km south, pop. 85,000), with Albuquerque (~220 km south, pop. 560,000) providing international airport access and full urban services. The region is accessible by US highway but genuinely remote in feel — the combination of desert landscape, high elevation, and mountain terrain creates practical isolation that is a feature for intentional community projects.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Taos County population density: **6.05 persons/km²** (US Census 2020: population 34,489; land area 5,710 km²). The JRC GHSL (Global Human Settlement Layer) grid density for the broader northern NM rural plateau is similarly ~5–8 persons/km², consistent with the Census figure. Confidence: **high**.

**Supporting facts:**
- Taos County population 34,489 (2020 Census), land area 5,710 km², population density 15.66/sq mi = 6.05/km².
- 54.6% of Taos County residents live in rural areas (2020 Census); only 45.4% in defined urban clusters.
- Town of Taos (county seat, pop. ~6,000 municipal, ~16,000 metro): hospital (Presbyterian Taos Medical Center), Taos Municipal Airport (small aircraft, charter; nearest commercial airport: Santa Fe, 90 km south), University of New Mexico Taos campus, multiple grocery chains, hardware stores, and building supply yards.
- Wheeler Peak (4,011 m), the highest point in New Mexico, is in Taos County; the mean county elevation is 2,590 m — the highest of any US county outside Colorado. Elevation creates genuine seasonal access constraints on mountain roads (winter snow/ice).
- Broadband: Taos has fibre and cable broadband in the town and many surrounding communities; rural areas use satellite (Starlink is widely adopted in the region). The US infrastructure investment of 2021 (IIJA) is funding rural broadband expansion in NM.
- Driving distance: Santa Fe 1.5 hours, Albuquerque 2.5 hours (US 285 / NM 68), Denver 4.5 hours (US 285 north).
- Taos Pueblo (UNESCO World Heritage, sovereign nation) is adjacent to the town; the Taos Ski Valley is 30 km NE, generating winter tourism economy. The broader area has a functional year-round tourism economy supporting hospitality, trades, and arts employment.
- Taos County has the highest mean elevation of any US county outside Colorado (Wikipedia, 2024); this is both a climate buffer and a transport consideration.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Remoteness in northern New Mexico is graduated: the town of Taos is accessible and functional; the wider plateau and canyon country requires a vehicle for almost everything. The combination of sparse population density and mountain terrain means that medical emergencies, supply-chain disruptions, and extreme weather events (blizzards, flash floods, wildfire evacuations) have a different character here than in urban-adjacent rural Europe. A settlement design that is genuinely autonomous — food production, medical first-response capacity, water storage for multi-week supply disruption — is not paranoid but prudent for this context. The flip side is that the very isolation that creates these challenges also creates the conditions for genuine community: residents in rural Taos County tend to know their neighbours and maintain reciprocal support relationships out of practical necessity. The arts and tourism economy means there is a broader economic ecosystem — short-term visitor income, craft markets, workshop hosting, agro-tourism — that settlements can integrate with for cash-flow without depending entirely on agricultural production.
