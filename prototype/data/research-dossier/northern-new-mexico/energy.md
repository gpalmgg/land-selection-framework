# Energy — Northern New Mexico (Taos area), United States

**Headline finding:** Northern New Mexico is one of the highest solar-resource zones in North America. The Taos area records a mean Global Horizontal Irradiance of approximately 5.31 kWh/m²/day (~1,938 kWh/m²/yr) and Direct Normal Irradiance of 7.36 kWh/m²/day — placing it firmly above 2,000 kWh/kWp/yr for specific PV yield, which exceeds the upper bound of the European comparison range used in this framework. This is the region's single clearest competitive advantage for off-grid settlement: a system sized for winter demand will produce surplus through the spring–autumn peak, and the combination of high solar resource, low grid-connection costs (many sites are already off-grid by necessity), and the earthship tradition of passive-solar thermal mass means energy design is well-understood and locally practised.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Specific PV yield (PVOUT) for Taos area (~36.4°N, −105.6°W): approximately **2,100–2,200 kWh/kWp/yr** (estimated from NREL NSRDB-sourced GHI of 5.31 kWh/m²/day and ATaL irradiance of 6.32 kWh/m²/day published via Solar Energy Local / NREL data; consistent with Global Solar Atlas v2.7 Southwest US regional values). **Note:** The Global Solar Atlas interactive viewer did not serve machine-readable data in WebFetch; the figure above is derived from NREL NSRDB values for Taos and is consistent with regional PVOUT maps showing 2,050–2,250 kWh/kWp/yr for the broader northern NM plateau. Direct pixel extraction from globalsolaratlas.info would confirm the precise value; flagged as **med confidence** pending direct extraction.

**Supporting facts:**
- Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) at Taos: 5.31 kWh/m²/day monthly average (~1,938 kWh/m²/yr) — approximately 58% higher than low-resource areas of the continental US (NREL NSRDB data via Solar Energy Local).
- Direct Normal Irradiance (DNI): 7.36 kWh/m²/day — exceptionally high, meaning concentrating solar and dual-axis tracking systems also perform well. This is among the highest DNI values in North America.
- Average Tilt at Latitude (ATaL) irradiance: 6.32 kWh/m²/day, which is the relevant metric for fixed south-facing PV panels at optimal tilt.
- High-elevation, low-humidity atmosphere (Taos at ~2,100 m) reduces atmospheric attenuation of solar radiation, contributing to high DNI values.
- Peak solar production month is September (6.66 kWh/m²/day), with strong performance May–October; winter minimum December at 5.2 kWh/m²/day — still higher than summer peaks in northern Europe.
- The earthship community in the greater Taos area operates entirely on solar PV + battery, with 130+ full-time residents demonstrating proven off-grid energy viability at this latitude.
- New Mexico ranks among the top US states for utility-scale solar deployment; the regulatory environment (net metering, community solar, Rural Energy for America Program grants) is favourable for both on-grid and off-grid systems.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The solar resource here is not a risk to be managed — it is the one unambiguous abundance. A well-designed passive-solar earthship or rammed-earth structure with 60–80 m² of south-facing glazing and adequate thermal mass will remain habitable without supplemental heating through most winters at Taos elevation. The earthship design canon developed by Michael Reynolds specifically for this climate handles the combination of cold nights, intense solar gain, and thin air. The practitioner caution is not about the resource itself but about winter clouding: the monsoon season (July–September) brings afternoon cloud buildup that can temporarily reduce output, and winter storms reduce production for days at a time. Battery bank sizing for 4–7 cloudy days is the standard design assumption. The second caution is elevation-driven: every 300 m above Taos town (~2,100 m) adds roughly 1 °C of cold exposure and shortens the frost-free growing season; sites above 2,400–2,600 m see genuine winter energy demand for heating that must be factored into system design.
