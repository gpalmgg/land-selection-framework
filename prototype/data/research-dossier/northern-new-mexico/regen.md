# Regen Network — Northern New Mexico (Taos area), United States

**Headline finding:** Northern New Mexico — and the Taos area specifically — is arguably the densest concentration of intentional community, earthship, and permaculture practice in the continental United States. The Greater World Earthship Community alone houses 130+ full-time residents in 100+ off-grid structures on 634 acres, and the ic.org directory lists over a dozen intentional communities across New Mexico, several concentrated within 100 km of Taos. The region is the birthplace of the earthship movement (Michael Reynolds, 1970s–present) and has hosted decades of permaculture education, acequia culture revival, and bioregional land stewardship practice. This is a community-of-practice asset of unusual depth — not just a count of sites, but a living repository of hard-won desert-appropriate knowledge.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Intentional community / permaculture / earthship / ecovillage sites within ~100 km of Taos (36.4°N, −105.6°W): at least **15–25 sites** identified across ic.org directory (New Mexico listings, 2025), GEN directory, and direct documentation of the Greater World Earthship Community, Hummingbird Community (San Cristobal, NM), and permaculture centres in Taos, Santa Fe, and northern NM corridor. Exact count varies with definition (the ic.org NM directory lists ~11 addresses statewide, several in the 100 km radius; earthship parcels within Greater World alone number 100+). Confidence: **med** (directional value is robust; exact count requires systematic GIS query of ic.org + GEN + OSM data against the 100 km radius).

**Supporting facts:**
- Greater World Earthship Community, Tres Piedras NM (~30 km NW of Taos): 634-acre off-grid development, 100+ earthship structures, full-time population 130+, established by Earthship Biotecture. Includes the Lemuria permaculture reclamation project combining earthship water systems with keyline design (NuMundo, Earthship Biotecture).
- Hummingbird Community, San Cristobal NM (~25 km N of Taos): listed on ic.org as an active intentional community.
- Valverde Commons, Taos NM: cohousing community listed on ic.org (2025).
- Lama Foundation, San Cristobal NM (~25 km N of Taos): spiritual intentional community, one of the oldest in the US (established 1967), still active.
- Earthship Biotecture Academy and internship programme: ongoing educational infrastructure training practitioners in earthship construction, attracting international students and creating a perpetual inflow of skilled practitioners to the region.
- Santa Fe (90 km S of Taos) hosts multiple permaculture organisations, the Meow Wolf arts collective (regenerative economy adjacent), and several cohousing communities.
- New Mexico has a strong acequia association network — the New Mexico Acequia Association (NMAA) represents 700+ acequia associations statewide, many in the Taos corridor, providing a ready infrastructure of communal land and water stewardship.
- The ic.org Fellowship for Intentional Community directory (2025) lists New Mexico communities in Silver City, Cleveland, Santa Fe, San Cristobal, Socorro, Albuquerque, and Taos — broad geographic distribution but concentrated density in the northern corridor.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The regen network density in northern New Mexico is a genuinely unusual asset that does not show up in standard dataset queries. The earthship movement is not just a building style — it is a lived culture of off-grid engineering, visitor-economy integration, legal precedent-setting (Taos County issued the first alternative-construction permits in the 1990s after Reynolds' decades-long battle with building codes), and mutual aid between practitioners. A new settlement here does not start from zero: there are local contractors who have built dozens of rammed-earth and earthship structures, supply chains for used tyres, glass bottles, and natural plasters, and a social circuit of seasonal workshops and practitioner gatherings. The acequia association network adds a layer of commons governance infrastructure that is centuries old and legally recognised. The risk side: the regen network's visibility has attracted land speculators and short-term vacation rental operators (earthship B&Bs are widespread), which inflates land values and can price out genuine long-term community developers. The Taos area's fame as a destination also means that practitioners here are well-networked but sometimes in competition for the same scarce water and land resources.
