# Climate — Northern New Mexico (Taos area), United States

**Headline finding:** Northern New Mexico is one of the fastest-warming sub-regions of the continental United States. Taos sits at roughly 2,100 m elevation, giving it a cooler baseline (~8–9 °C mean annual temperature) than lower-elevation Southwestern cities, but that buffer is eroding: the broader Southwest is projected to warm ~1.5–2.5 °C above the 1995–2014 reference period by 2041–2060 under SSP2-4.5, and New Mexico as a state warmed 1.85 °C between 1970 and 2018 — the fastest rate of any contiguous US state in that window. The trajectory matters more than the current state: summers are lengthening, droughts are deepening, and wildfire risk is climbing on a trajectory that will define mid-century livability.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Projected mean annual temperature at the Taos / Sangre de Cristo centroid (36.4°N, −105.6°W) under SSP2-4.5, 2041–2060: approximately **10–11 °C** (multi-model ensemble mean, composed from WorldClim CMIP6 v2.1 baseline of ~8.3 °C plus the ~1.7–2.3 °C Southwest warming signal documented in the CMIP6 literature for this period relative to 1995–2014; consistent with the NCA5 2023 finding that NM "annual average temperatures are projected to most likely exceed historical record levels by mid-century" even under lower emissions). The exact pixel-level value requires direct WorldClim GeoTIFF extraction at 36.4/−105.6; the estimate above is bounded by cross-referencing the observed baseline and the published CMIP6 anomaly range.

**Supporting facts:**
- Observed historical mean annual temperature at Taos: ~8.3 °C (climate-data.org; derived from NOAA station normals), with July averaging ~20.5 °C and January ~−4.4 °C.
- New Mexico has warmed 3.32 °F (1.85 °C) between 1970 and 2018 — the highest statewide warming rate in the contiguous US (350newmexico.org synthesis of NOAA data).
- NCA5 (2023) projects New Mexico annual temperatures to most likely exceed historical record levels by mid-century even under a lower emissions pathway; end-of-century range 1.6–4.4 °C above 1901–1960 baseline under lower scenario, 4.0–8.1 °C under higher scenario.
- A CMIP6 ensemble study of the US, Central America and Caribbean (Sillmann et al. / Earth Systems and Environment 2022) projects the Southwest warming ~1.5–2.5 °C relative to 1995–2014 under SSP2-4.5 for 2041–2060.
- Maximum temperature of the warmest month (August) in the Taos area currently reaches ~27–30 °C at town elevation; fire-season conditions extend earlier in spring and later in fall as demonstrated by the 341,471-acre Hermit's Peak/Calf Canyon fire (2022), the largest in New Mexico history, which burned partly in Taos County.
- Annual precipitation is ~300–350 mm at Taos town elevation, bimodal (winter snow + summer monsoon); the monsoon is projected to become more variable and intense under climate change.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The high-elevation baseline is this region's greatest climate asset — and also its greatest illusion. At 2,100 m, Taos currently sits below the threshold where summer heat becomes physiologically dangerous, and passive solar earthship design performs exceptionally well in cool-night / warm-day desert conditions. But the same elevation gradient that buffers summer heat also concentrates snowpack and winter cold; projects sited above 2,400 m face genuine freeze-risk and shorter growing seasons. The more structurally important threat is wildfire: the 2022 Hermit's Peak/Calf Canyon event showed that Sangre de Cristo forests can sustain landscape-scale fire in a single season, with watershed-level consequences (the Gallinas watershed supplying Las Vegas, NM became unusable). Any settlement in forested terrain within 20–30 km of the montane belt needs a serious wildfire resilience strategy — defensible-space design, ember-resistant construction, watershed agreements — not as mitigation but as foundational design. The monsoon-season precipitation (July–September), while irregular, remains the landscape's primary recharge mechanism; earthworks that slow and sink monsoon runoff are more load-bearing here than any other water intervention.
