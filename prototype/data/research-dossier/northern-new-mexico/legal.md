# Legal + Economic — Northern New Mexico (Taos area), United States

**Headline finding:** The United States imposes no nationality restrictions on land purchase by foreign individuals or entities, making New Mexico as legally open to non-US buyers as any domestic purchaser — with one critical exception gaining traction at the federal level (USDA foreign-ownership reporting requirements, and ongoing Congressional proposals to restrict agricultural land purchase by nationals of adversarial states). New Mexico itself has not enacted foreign land ownership restrictions as of May 2026. The region's most distinctive legal feature is not foreign-ownership law but the acequia water-rights system: water rights are property, attached to land by priority date, and administered by democratically governed community ditch associations with statutory veto power over transfers — a commons-governance structure unlike anything in European land law. Land prices in Taos County are substantially above the New Mexico statewide agricultural average, driven by the region's amenity value and tourism economy.

**Key data point (with vintage):** Land prices in Taos County, NM: median listing price **~$12,846/acre** (LandSearch market data, 2024–2025); average cost per acre on listed properties ~$23,529/acre. USDA NASS 2025 New Mexico statewide farm real estate average: **$725/acre** (representing the lowest statewide agricultural average in the continental US). The wide gap reflects Taos County's amenity premium — most listed parcels carry recreational, residential-development, or views value above purely agricultural utility. Pure rangeland / dryland parcels closer to the USDA baseline do exist but are less common in active listings.

**Supporting facts:**
- **Foreign ownership**: No New Mexico state law restricts foreign purchase of land. The federal Agricultural Foreign Investment Disclosure Act (AFIDA) requires foreign persons owning US agricultural land to report to USDA within 90 days of acquisition; non-compliance is financial penalty, not prohibition. New Mexico experienced a 358,149-acre increase in foreign-held agricultural acreage from 2022–2023, primarily wind energy leases, without legal challenge.
- **Land purchase process**: Foreign buyers can purchase through standard US real estate closing; a US tax identification number (ITIN) suffices for non-residents. Closing costs typically 2–4%. No visa or residency requirement to own land; an EB-5 investor visa (minimum $800K capital deployment in rural areas) is one path to residency but not required for purchase.
- **Water rights — acequia law**: Acequias are legal subdivisions of New Mexico state (NM Stat. Ch.73 Art.2). Any transfer of a water right into or out of an acequia requires written approval from the acequia's elected commissioners; transfer may be denied if it would harm the acequia or its members (§73-2-28, 2025). Water rights are sold separately from land in many cases and must be permitted by the NM Office of the State Engineer (OSE). Priority date governs allocation during shortage; senior rights are served first.
- **Prior appropriation doctrine**: New Mexico operates "first in time, first in right" — new water users acquire junior rights, the first to be curtailed in drought. The Upper Rio Grande is fully appropriated; new surface-water rights are not available. New well permits are possible for groundwater where hydraulic separation from surface water can be demonstrated, but this is increasingly difficult in Taos County sub-basins.
- **Rainwater harvesting**: Legal in New Mexico without permit for domestic use; effectively unlimited since HB 366 (2012). This is legally cleaner than in many western states and is foundational to earthship-style water systems.
- **Alternative construction**: Taos County has a history of issuing permits for earthship and alternative construction materials (rammed earth, straw bale, adobe) under the New Mexico Construction Industries Division alternative methods process. State building codes apply; alternative materials require engineering documentation or approved alternative methods approval.
- **Zoning and land use**: Most rural Taos County land is zoned A-1 (agricultural) or A-3 (rural residential) at the county level, with 5-acre or larger minimum lot sizes. No municipal zoning applies outside town limits. Subdivision of agricultural land triggers a platting process through the county. The county does not restrict earthship or communal living structures beyond standard occupancy and sanitation requirements.
- **Intentional community legal structures**: LLCs, land trusts, and limited partnerships are all recognised under New Mexico law for collective land ownership. The New Mexico Community Land Trust Act (2007) provides a statutory framework for CLTs, which can hold land permanently and provide long-term affordable access. A Community Land Trust structure would be well-suited to the ethos and long-term horizon of a regenerative settlement.
- **Property taxes**: New Mexico has relatively low property taxes (effective rate ~0.55% of assessed value, among the lowest 10 in the US). Agricultural land classified under the Agricultural Assessment Act may receive further reduction if used for grazing or farming.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
The acequia water system is the central legal complexity for a new settlement in the Taos corridor. Buying acequia-served land is buying into a governance obligation, not just a water allocation: you will be expected to attend parciante meetings, contribute to ditch maintenance (limpia), and vote on water distribution decisions. For a long-horizon regenerative community, this is philosophically aligned — it is exactly the kind of commons governance that the regen movement theorises about — but practitioners must be prepared for actual participation, including in drought-year conflicts when water is scarce and tensions run high. The alternative (rainwater harvesting + permitted well) avoids acequia obligations but is more technically demanding and has genuine hydrological limits. The Community Land Trust structure deserves serious consideration for long-term tenure security: a CLT holds the land permanently, can offer individual parcels on 99-year leases, and prevents speculative resale while enabling multigenerational stewardship — well suited to a 50–100-year settlement horizon.
