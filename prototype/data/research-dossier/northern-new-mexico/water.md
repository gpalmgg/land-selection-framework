# Water — Northern New Mexico (Taos area), United States

**Headline finding:** The Upper Rio Grande basin, which supplies surface water to the Taos area, is in a structural water-deficit that is worsening under climate change. WRI Aqueduct 4.0 places the broader Rio Grande basin in the "extremely high" baseline water-stress category (score >0.8), and the 2050 business-as-usual projection deepens that stress: USGS modelling shows streamflow declining further as snowpack-driven peak flows arrive earlier and total annual volume shrinks. New Mexico holds a cumulative delivery debt to Texas of 153 MCM (124,000 acre-feet) as of late 2024 under the Rio Grande Compact — a political and legal signal that the basin is already over-allocated. Settlement here is viable only through deliberate, closed-loop water design.

**Key data point (with vintage):** WRI Aqueduct 4.0 (2023 release) baseline water-stress score for the Upper Rio Grande sub-basins covering northern New Mexico: **"Extremely High" (score >0.8, category 5 of 5)**. The 2050 BAU scenario (SSP3-RCP7.0) maintains or deepens this classification. New Mexico's cumulative delivery deficit to Texas under the Rio Grande Compact: **153 MCM (124,000 acre-feet)** as of December 2024 (New Mexico Water Advocates, 2025), representing the worst sustained dry period in the Compact's recorded history.

**Supporting facts:**
- The 17-year period 2009–2025 produced native basin flows at the Otowi Bridge gauge nearly identical to the 1948–1964 drought — the worst sustained dry period in Compact record (NM Water Advocates, 2025).
- USGS streamflow modelling for the Upper Rio Grande projects that peak annual runoff will arrive earlier in the season by end-of-century, negatively impacting a watershed where demand already exceeds supply year-round (USGS SIR 2021-5138).
- Taos Pueblo and the town of Taos draw water from Taos Creek (a Rio Grande tributary) and municipal wells; acequia systems serve the surrounding agricultural lands. The NM Office of the State Engineer administers all surface and groundwater rights under a prior-appropriation ("first in time, first in right") doctrine — new users acquire junior rights, which are the first to be curtailed in shortage.
- Acequia associations (governing community irrigation ditches) have statutory authority to veto water right transfers into or out of their ditch systems; any settlement seeking acequia water access must negotiate with the relevant parciante (shareholder) association (NM Stat. 73-2-28, 2025).
- Groundwater in many Taos-area sub-basins is hydraulically connected to surface water and is subject to the same prior-appropriation system; new wells require State Engineer permits and may be refused if senior rights would be impaired.
- Annual precipitation at Taos town (2,100 m elevation): ~300–350 mm, heavily concentrated in winter snowpack (October–April) and summer monsoon (July–September). Summer months are functionally arid.

**Practitioner-relevant nuance:**
Water is the single most load-bearing design constraint in northern New Mexico — more so than in most European comparators, including Alentejo. The acequia system is both a resource and a governance mechanism: parcels served by an acequia carry historic water rights attached to the land, but those rights are administered communally by an elected mayordomo and commissioners. A settlement that purchases acequia-served land is buying into that governance structure, with all obligations (maintenance labour, proportional water shares, voting rights) that come with it. This is a feature, not a bug — acequia culture is the deepest living expression of commons-based water management in North America — but it requires genuine participation, not passive ownership. For projects not served by an acequia, the only realistic paths are: (1) purchase land with existing permitted well + water right, (2) aggressive rainwater harvesting (legal in NM, effectively unlimited for domestic use since 2012 HB 366), and (3) greywater reuse (legal under NM plumbing code). An earthship-style closed-loop water system — harvest, use, treat, reuse — is not merely philosophically appropriate; it is hydraulically necessary for off-acequia sites.
